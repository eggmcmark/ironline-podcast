# The Compact Economy — World-Building Reference

## How It Emerged (historical logic for the writer)

The Compact didn't arrive as an ideology. It arrived as a series of practical responses to cascading crises:

1. **2030–2050: The Automation Displacement**. AI didn't replace blue-collar work first — it replaced white-collar work. Administrative, analytical, legal, financial, marketing. The jobs that required judgment but not physical presence. ~60% of white-collar employment eliminated over two decades.

2. **2041: The Great Wage Collapse**. Not a depression — an inflection point. When income stopped being the primary vehicle of human value, society reorganized. Slowly, messily, with enormous political conflict. The people who still did physical, verifiable work found themselves newly valued.

3. **2050s–2070s: The Guild Revival**. Trade organizations formalized certification. Apprenticeship programs expanded. The cultural status of skilled physical work inverted — carpenters, electricians, engineers became the new prestige class. Universities survived but lost their monopoly on status.

4. **2069: The Compact Framework Act** (Massachusetts, first). Codified equity participation, the Value Ledger, and CapEx Boards as governing economic infrastructure. Other regions followed. By 2098, the Northeast Compact Territory was the largest Compact jurisdiction.

## What Daily Life Looks Like (2125)

The writer should internalize these details so they emerge naturally, not as exposition:

- **No one has a "job"**. People have skill portfolios and take projects through CapEx Boards. A master carpenter might do a brownstone renovation Mon–Wed and Guild workshop furniture Thu–Sat.
- **Money (EQU) exists and is used**. It's not a barter economy. But EQU is backed by real asset appreciation, so inflation is structurally impossible.
- **Housing works differently**. Equity participation is standard. A homeowner who wants a kitchen renovation posts to the Board, gets proposals from individuals and small collectives, negotiates equity terms.
- **Children are apprenticed at 14**. Not forced labor — structured learning. Guild certification at 21. The most prestigious path.
- **AI runs the background**. Logistics, scheduling, supply chain, compliance, health monitoring. Invisible infrastructure. No one thinks about AI any more than we think about TCP/IP.
- **Healthcare is a Commons asset**. Maintained by the Commons Council, funded by the appreciation tithe. Not employer-linked, not insurance-based.
- **Roads, bridges, public infrastructure** built by Guilds under multi-year Compact contracts.
- **No income tax**. All public revenue from the 4% appreciation tithe.
- **Status comes from craft**. The title "Founder" is the highest social rank and is earned, not inherited. Requires Guild certification across three domains.

## What Went Wrong (the cracks Jonah finds)

- **The Skill Forward**: A derivative that allows EQU to flow before work is completed. Technically legal because Guild certification scores serve as collateral. But the scores can be gamed, the platforms add abstraction layers, and soon there are instruments eleven derivatives removed from any actual human doing actual work.
- **The structural parallel to 2008**: CDOs were bundles of mortgage risk; Skill Forwards are bundles of human capability risk. Both create the illusion of value through mathematical complexity.
- **The São Paulo incident**: A major Guild project where the contracted workers never existed. Their certifications were real — years old, passed through platform intermediaries, layered with abstractions. The building failed inspection. People got hurt.

## Key Economic Tensions (for story conflict)

1. **Physical vs. Abstract**: The Compact privileges physical, verifiable work. But the world still needs organizing, creating, managing. The "Ghosts" who do this work feel oppressed. They have a point.
2. **Innovation vs. Stability**: The Compact was designed to prevent financial abstraction. But Mara argues that preventing all credit strangles growth. She has a point too.
3. **Inherited vs. Earned**: Founders pass EQU to their children. Over generations, this creates hereditary wealth — the thing the Compact was supposed to prevent. The Hale family is exhibit A.
4. **Local vs. Global**: CapEx Boards are regional. But the economy is global. Arbitrage between jurisdictions creates pressure. Not every region adopted the Compact.
