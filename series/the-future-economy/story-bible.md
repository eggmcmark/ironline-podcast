# THE FUTURE ECONOMY — Story Bible
## "The Weight of Real Things"

---

## Logline

When a Brooklyn contractor's handshake equity deal with a cash-poor professor accidentally becomes the template for a new global economic order, three generations of his family must fight to build it, defend it, and finally decide whether to save it — as the world they created begins to betray everything they believed.

---

## Central Question

What is real value? What does a society look like when it finally agrees on the answer — and what happens when it starts to lie to itself again?

---

## Thematic Core

Every generation builds its paradise on the ruins of the last generation's corruption — and plants the seeds of the next generation's ruin in the foundation of its virtues.

The builders always win. But winning creates its own entropy. The real heroism isn't constructing the system — it's being willing to understand *why* you built it, even when your own grandchildren have forgotten.

---

## Tone and Voice

- **Third person limited**, close POV — one consciousness per season
- **Audio-first prose**: written for the ear. Shorter sentences. Strategic repetition. Sensory grounding. Atmospheric transitions, not conjunctions
- **Sensory and physical**: the prose honors materiality. Wood, steel, plaster dust, the weight of a level in the hand
- **No exposition dumps**: world-building arrives through lived experience, overheard conversation, things characters notice and don't explain
- **Dialogue is character**: each person speaks differently. Marcus is terse, concrete, thinks in materials. Nadia is expansive, precise, thinks in systems. Elena is sharp, strategic, thinks in leverage. Jonah is careful, observant, thinks in patterns
- **Humor is dry and observational**: never jokes, never quips. The comedy of competent people dealing with incompetent systems
- **No camp. No cheese. No prophecy**: characters don't know they're in a historical moment. They're just living

## World Rules

### The Compact Economy (fully established by ~2090)

1. **The Asset Foundation**: Wealth denominated in real property, productive capacity, registered skill equity. The Equity Unit (EQU) is backed by a rolling index of real asset appreciation. You cannot print EQU — only earn it by creating verifiable real-world value.

2. **The Capability Exchange (CapEx Boards)**: Regional registries replacing stock markets. Individuals post verified skills and capacity. Projects post needs with equity participation offers. Matched algorithmically, executed relationally. Financial speculation on human capability is constitutionally prohibited.

3. **The Value Ledger**: Distributed record of every material transaction, build, harvest, and craft event. Nothing abstract trades on the Ledger — only physically auditable things. Descended from blockchain but vastly more sophisticated.

4. **The Asset Stewardship Council (ASC)**: Governance organized by asset class (Land, Built, Productive Capacity, Commons), not geography. Representatives elected by registered holders. No income tax — infrastructure funded by 4% tithe on property appreciation events.

### Social Classes (by 2125)

- **Founders (Builder Class)**: Highest status. Skilled makers with Guild certification across three domains. Can negotiate equity participation on any project. Title earned, not inherited.
- **Keepers**: Middle class. Registered property owners with moderate skill portfolios. Most people. They live well.
- **Abstract Class ("Ghosts")**: Growing underclass living in the AI-managed administrative layer, doing work the Ledger can't verify as real. Growing political tension.
- **Neo-Abstractors**: Sophisticated faction engineering synthetic EQU instruments — complex derivatives that pass Ledger audits but are divorced from real output. They are reinventing finance.

### Technology

- AI manages logistics, scheduling, supply chain, legal compliance, health monitoring
- Humans do the deciding, building, and relating
- Automation eliminated ~60% of white-collar employment (2030-2070)
- Physical skilled work is the highest-status activity
- Guild certification at 21 after apprenticeship starting at 14
- Universities exist but confer no advantage without Guild certification
- The most prestigious universities are trade academies

### Daily Life (2125)

- A carpenter works CapEx-matched projects Mon-Wed, Guild workshop Thu-Sat. No "job" in the old sense.
- Kitchen renovations are posted to CapEx Boards; contractors bid with equity participation terms
- EQU used for everything; Ledger barter normalized for small transactions
- Children apprenticed at 14
- Barter creates zero tax obligation under the Compact charter

## What This Story Is NOT

- Not a utopia. The Compact has real problems, real inequities, real blind spots
- Not a dystopia. People live better than we do. The system works, mostly
- Not a polemic. No character speaks for the author. Every position has merit and cost
- Not speculative hand-waving. Every extrapolation traces to a real-world trend
- Not about AI. AI is infrastructure, like plumbing. The story is about humans
- Not Ayn Rand. The builders are right about value but wrong about entropy. Virtue must be re-chosen every generation

## Episode Structure Pattern

Each episode follows this general architecture (adapted per story needs):

1. **Cold open** (~500 words): Drop into a scene. Sensory, immediate, mid-action. No context. The listener must lean in.
2. **Development** (~5000-6000 words): The episode's central story unfolds. Multiple scenes. Time may pass. New characters may appear. The thematic question is explored through action and dialogue, never stated.
3. **Complication/Turn** (~800 words): Something shifts. A revelation, a consequence, a choice that can't be unmade. The episode earns its place in the larger arc.
4. **Landing** (~500 words): Not a cliffhanger. Not a summary. A resonant image or moment that echoes forward. The listener should feel they've arrived somewhere and that the road continues.

## Continuity Rules

- Characters age in real time within the story's timeline
- Technology mentioned in one episode must remain consistent
- Economic concepts introduced must follow their own internal logic
- Named locations must be geographically consistent
- Weather, seasons, and time of day must track within an episode
- No retcons. If something was said, it was said. Characters can be wrong. The narrator cannot.
- Character speech patterns must remain consistent (see character files)
- The Hale family tree is canon — births, deaths, relationships are fixed

## Research Anchors (real-world grounding)

- Austrian economics (Mises, Hayek) — intellectual roots of the Compact's value theory
- Medieval/Japanese guild systems — certification as accountability
- Henry George's land value tax — precursor to appreciation tithe
- Distributed ledger technology — plausible extrapolation of Value Ledger
- 2008 financial crisis mechanics — Mara's Skill Forwards are structurally identical
- Matthew Crawford's "Shop Class as Soulcraft" — physical work as philosophy
- Crown Heights brownstone architecture — tactile accuracy for Season 1
- Real construction practices — tools, materials, sequencing, trade culture
