# Episode 6: "What She Read"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin reads her grandmother's log through the night, leaves the archive at midday, and travels south with Dez through unclaimed territory toward Meridian and the first of Adaeze's three instructions.*

---

The entry was dated September 12, 2089.

She had been reading for five hours.

It was not a long entry. Adaeze wrote short entries when the decisions in them were large, and she had kept to that across thirty-five years of practice.

*Corridor access changed this month. The Curator has taken sector management from mile twenty to mile sixty, including the water relay stations. Nnedi is ill. Received word through Laure at the junction post. Path back to the settlement is possible but not clean — three Curator patrols in sector and the new relay station at mile thirty-one will log a key transit.*

*Going home is not the choice this week.*

*Beginning arrangement for interior placement. Sable River is the best candidate — large enough, Commons services available, Rootstock records accessible through the Territorial Archive. Estimated time to establish: eighteen months to two years.*

She put the log flat on the table and held it there with both hands.

The reading-room light was warm and steady above her. On the other table, Dez was asleep in her chair, her head against the wall, her pack settled against her feet. The climate system held the temperature constant. A faint sound from the ducts — air moving, the building doing what it was built to do in the middle of the night, with or without anyone in it.

She had been calculating dates since early in the reading, when she had understood that this was a different kind of document than she was trained for — not surface and composition but sequence and weight. Adaeze had committed to Sable River before Kirin was born. Before Kirin's parents were married. Before the infrastructure accident that killed them both in the eastern district in 2104, eight years into Adaeze's tenure in the apartment — an apartment she had held for thirteen years by the time Kirin arrived in it at age seven, an apartment Adaeze had built into a life that looked quiet from the outside because looking quiet from the outside was the function.

The log did not say this. The log said what it always said: event, action taken, result. The entry for September 12, 2089 recorded a decision about corridor access and initiated the establishment of a cover. It did not say *I will not be going home for a very long time.* It didn't have to. The dates said it.

She turned the page.

*September 28, 2089. Sable River provisional. Naila has secured transit north — not Salt yet, Salt is later. Commons placement in the health aide track, Level One. The gap in my records ends here. Adaeze Okonkwo stops. Adaeze Saleh begins.*

She read for two more hours. Through 2091 and the apartment's first entry — the herb pots on the window ledge catalogued as *practice maintenance, corridor plants, Rootstock variety, keep alive*. Not decoration. Not the small joy of growing things. A practice. A form of memory kept alive in clay pots on a third-floor ledge in a city four hundred miles from where the plants originally grew.

She read through 2097 and the entry about Tomas Bergström's mother, which she had already encountered. She read past it.

She read through 2098 and three entries about the corridor's water management grid — a cycle of junction assessments Adaeze had been tracking, the condition of specific components rated against their installed specification, the numbers logged with the dry precision of someone who had been trained to document structural decay and had reasons to care about the decay's timing. The junction components in question were in the sectors Dez had described: eastern grid, secondary water management, installed 2082. Adaeze had been rating them annually. The ratings ran from marginal to below-spec to flagged-for-replacement over six years of entries. The Compact's infrastructure board had never responded.

She read through 2100 and an entry about Naila, made during a transit through the corridor: *Naila at the way-station three years running. Not a Rootstock position — her own arrangement, her own rules. The distinction matters. Her interest in the corridor's operation does not require the network and the network does not own her. This is a feature, not a vulnerability.* The assessment was not warm and was, Kirin understood, a form of respect. Adaeze's form.

She read through 2101 and an entry about Kirin's parents. Not about the accident — this was three years before the accident. An entry about a walk in the garden district, a Commons festival Adaeze had attended with Kirin's mother. The entry was brief and described a plate of food and a conversation about whether the Compact's current appreciation-tithe structure was a stable long-term equilibrium. It did not describe whether the conversation was interesting. It noted, in the final line: *She is the best thing Kirin's father has done.*

She stopped at that line.

She was aware of being twenty-eight years old in a reading room in a basement under a building that did not appear on any map, reading an entry her grandmother had written about her mother in 2101, six weeks before the New Year. Her mother had been twenty-six years old in 2101. Kirin had exceeded her mother's age and had not noticed the crossing.

She read through 2104 and stopped.

The entry for August 14, 2104 was the longest entry in the log's first hundred pages. She read it once. She did not read it again.

She stood up from the table and walked to the end of the reading bay and stood at the wall and looked at the concrete — the poured surface, the aggregate visible in the finish coat where the form had been stripped, the small shadows each aggregate piece made in the reading-room light. She stood there for a few minutes. Then she went back to the table and sat down and opened the log to the next entry.

The dawn light came through the shutter seam — a gray line at the tops of the windows. The climate system changed its cycle. The door's locking mechanism disengaged with a soft pressure change, the building unsealing, the warmth of the interior breathing slightly out through the new gap around the frame.

She put the log in her pack between the atlas and the first soft layer.

Dez was awake. She had the stillness of someone who had decided not to announce it. She straightened in her chair and looked at Kirin with the assessment she ran on entering any new situation.

"How far."

"Twelve years in. I'll finish on the road."

Dez stood and stretched her back without ceremony. "Eat first. The route doesn't have supply points for the first forty miles."

* * *

The kitchen was spare.

The campus produced what it could from its agricultural operations and supplemented from network supply runs on no fixed schedule. Fen maintained an inventory on a board above the counter, chalked in the tight column-alignment of someone who had learned to be precise about material constraints. Kirin read the inventory while Dez assembled the rations.

They ate standing. Flat bread three days old and still sufficient. Hard cheese. A preserved vegetable she couldn't identify by taste.

Fen came in before they were finished.

He had been up and out already — he had the look of someone who had done the battery-room check and the water walk and come in from the outside cold. He was in his coat with condensation still on the shoulders. He put his cup under the water heater and looked at the counter without comment.

"Leaving now," Dez said.

"Twenty minutes is fine." He drank his tea while they finished. He did not make conversation. The room was large enough for three people doing three things.

Kirin put her empty cup on the counter. "The campus. After we go."

"What about it."

"The clearance window."

"Forty-four hours from now." He looked at her with the even patience he'd maintained since they arrived. "Done this before. Less often than I'd like. But done it." He turned his cup. "The archive is not the building. If they come and see a sealed building and a campus manager with nothing useful to say, the archive is still there. Been there since 2088."

She accepted that.

He walked them to the south perimeter — through the back of the property, between two of the unused buildings, the path narrowing where scrub had moved in from both sides. At the break in the low embankment he stopped.

"First water is at the old agricultural cistern. Six miles. Tank is twelve meters, painted green, east side of a former tree farm. South-face spigot."

"You told us last night," Dez said.

"Telling you again." He looked at the path ahead — the scrub and former field in the middle distance, the flat pale sky. "Go straight before you angle south. First quarter mile there's a low area that looks passable. It isn't."

"Thank you," Kirin said.

He looked at her. He had the expression of someone accepting a formality that was nonetheless genuine.

He turned back toward the campus.

They went through the break and out onto the ground. Within ten minutes the buildings had disappeared behind them, absorbed into the low-growing vegetation, gone.

* * *

The route south was different from every road she had been on since leaving Sable River.

Not worse. Adequate — a former agricultural track, the ground compacted by decades of equipment use, the compaction holding even where scrub had moved back in at the edges. What made it different was that it had not been managed in a long time. No recent work. No intention visible. Just the remnant of a purpose that had ended when the purpose ended, and no one had continued it.

Dez navigated by terrain reading. She changed direction twice in the first hour without explanation, and both times the reason became clear within two hundred meters: once a low area that would be wet footing in the wrong season; once a concrete structure with the profile of a monitoring unit. She had walked this route twice before, following someone who knew it the second time, and she did what she had watched him do.

The key was cold in Kirin's pocket. Cold since they sealed the archive building the previous evening. No corridor nodes here. No infrastructure to respond to. They were walking through the gap between two systems that did not acknowledge each other, and the key was dormant in the gap the same way it had been dormant in Sable River — inert, waiting for something that spoke its language.

Old fence posts at forty-meter intervals. A field management grid that had gone out of use when the population contracted and the agricultural program was abandoned. The autonomous systems that were supposed to maintain it had eventually stopped being told what to maintain. Nothing pointed to it as loss. It just was what it was — the remnant of a thing that had been built, used, and left.

Three hours from the campus, she took out the point-to-point comm.

It was in a sealed case in her outer pack pocket. Amira had pressed it into her hands in Sable River with a solar charge cell and a piece of paper with coordinates. She had been carrying it for three weeks without using it.

She opened the case and keyed the coordinates and held the device at arm's length facing south.

Dez glanced back. "Backchannel."

"Amira's."

"When you find out what's on the other end, tell me. I have interests in who the Sable River backchannel runs through."

The indicator went green. A man's voice, careful, testing the connection.

"Sable River relay. Is this Kirin Saleh."

"Yes."

"Confirm the item from your grandmother's collection."

"A metal coin the size of a thumb. Four raised squares on the back face."

A silence. A door in the background. Footsteps.

"Perun sends a word." Another pause. "He says the letters are formal. Administrative correspondence. Two offices — his word, he said to say offices. He has rendered six of the twelve. Full translation through Amira when complete." Another pause. "He says you should know the first letter is dated 2085. The last is dated 2088. The last is addressed to a woman named Okonkwo. He says the letter tells her she is the choice."

Kirin held the comm and looked at the ground.

"Tell him I understand. Tell him to keep going."

"He says he needs another week."

"Tell him there is no deadline." She looked at the horizon. "Tell him thank you."

The connection went quiet. She turned the comm off.

Dez had not moved from where she stood twelve paces ahead, back to Kirin, watching the south.

"Administrative correspondence," she said. "Not personal."

"A succession agreement. 2085 to 2088. Addressed to my grandmother at the end." She put the comm away. "The last letter tells her she is the choice."

Dez turned. The look she gave Kirin was full assessment, close range — not the watchful scanning she ran on landscapes but the look she had used at Larch, reading a situation.

"She didn't choose," Dez said.

"She accepted. That's not the same."

"No." A pause. "It changes something anyway. If the appointment was institutional — if an office made it and she accepted it — the obligation ran both ways. She was owed something too. By the network. By the Outside."

"She was owed thirty-five years of a life she built herself in a city four hundred miles from her home."

Dez was quiet for two steps.

"That's one way to read it," she said.

"I read the log," Kirin said. "That's what it says."

They walked.

The land changed in accumulation, the way large changes worked. The old-growth scrub thinned. The field boundaries in the soil composition became more recent, the tilling patterns less deeply impressed. Specific entries from the log surfaced in what she passed — not because she was trying to recall them, but because the log had been written by someone who had been watching this kind of ground for decades. Entry 47, 2097: *native scrub reclaiming old field boundaries at approximately four meters per decade.* The log's contents mapped onto the terrain the way the key's pulse had mapped onto the corridor's infrastructure — with the precision of something that had been built to correspond.

Six miles from the campus, she saw the cistern through the scrub. Twelve meters tall. Painted a green that had been correct for the surrounding vegetation when it was applied and had since drifted from it as the scrub changed character.

The spigot on the south face ran clean.


* * *

She filled her two bottles and the collapsible bladder and set Renata's chit on the flat top of the concrete pad at the cistern's base.

The chit was smaller than the key — thinner alloy, worn at the edges, the contact pads on the back face in the same configuration but lighter. She had carried it in her chest pocket since mile twenty-two, since she had found Renata propped against the guardrail on the corridor road with her pack opened and this object in her coat pocket, missed.

On the back face: one glyph etched into the metal, not one of the raised contact pads. A separate mark, deliberate, sized to fit in the space between the lower pads without crowding them.

She could read it now.

The glyph was a character from the Tamsat alphabet — the same reconstructed script as the letters in her waxed pouch. She had seen it in the archive, in the labeled series of aisle seven, and later in Adaeze's log where she wrote occasional phrases in Tamsat and then translated them immediately, as if she had started to forget and was reminding herself. The character was the initial consonant of the name *Okonkwo*. The name Adaeze had been born with and had stopped using in 2089 when she crossed the frontier.

Whoever had put this mark on Renata's chit had known the name. Had known it mattered. Had carried it into the corridor on a chit that the Curator's operative searched for at mile twenty-two and did not find.

Dez sat against the concrete base of the cistern and ate from her provisions. She looked at what Kirin was looking at.

"You know what it says now," Dez said.

"The first letter of her original name." Kirin put the chit back in her chest pocket. "Renata knew the name. Or whoever gave her the chit did."

"They would have been enrolled at Salt. Naila would have given them the enrollment mark."

"Naila knew."

"Naila didn't tell you."

Kirin looked at the cistern wall. The concrete was old. It had been poured in sections that had settled unevenly, the seams visible as step cracks, the lower course showing efflorescence where water had been working through for years. None of it was imminent failure. The structure would stand for another fifty years without repair and then probably fifty more if the water table stayed where it was.

"The operative on the corridor road," she said. "He found Renata. He opened her pack and didn't find the chit."

"He was looking for the key."

"He didn't look at her face."

"No." Dez looked at the orchard's dead trees. "You know this because Naila told you."

"He opened the pack and didn't find what he was looking for and moved on." She looked at the dead trees. The orchard configuration was still visible in the spacing, the trees grown out into individuals over decades but still in the original pattern. "Renata would have been through Salt. Naila would have had her in the ledger."

"Yes."

"Naila didn't say so."

"Naila says what the situation requires her to say. The two categories — what she says and what she knows — they overlap sometimes and don't other times." Dez finished her provisions and sealed the wrapper. "If it matters, you'll find out. If it matters, Naila will tell you."

"When does it matter."

"When you need to use the chit for something." Dez stood and shouldered her pack. "Come on. Three hours before the light goes."

* * *

The country shifted in the third hour.

The old orchard and field land gave way to ground with a different history — smaller use, more fragmentary. The remnant hedgerows stopped. The soil changed from the deep-tilled dark of former agricultural land to a lighter, more variable composition, and the grass communities shifted toward shorter coastal precursor species, the kinds that established themselves in the zone between true inland and true coast where the water table was close to the surface but not yet salt.

Dez navigated without a map, the way she had navigated everything. She changed direction once when the slope ahead showed the drainage pattern of a seasonally wet area and once when she saw a structure in the middle distance — concrete mount, angled sensor housing, Free Zone atmospheric monitoring. Not interested in traffic. She steered them well around it anyway, without commenting.

"Meridian," Kirin said. "Dara is one reason you're going."

Dez was quiet for a step.

"What's the other reason."

"A contact in the Trade District," Dez said. "Separate from yours. Infrastructure sector. Someone who tracks water management component projections. The twelve-year-old report in the archive documents the vulnerability. A current projection tells you when the vulnerability becomes critical. Those are different things."

"You've been going to Meridian anyway."

"I've been going to Meridian if the route made sense. The route makes sense now." She paused. "You should know I'm not going to tell my contact what you're carrying. I have interests in what you're carrying. I also have interests in not cross-contaminating my contacts. The interests are compatible."

"And if your contact finds out anyway."

"Then I'll have to decide what to do about it." A brief glance back. "Which is always how it works."

Kirin walked. The low sun came through a break in the cloud cover, raking the short grass from the side. Long shadows off the monitoring post behind them, off Dez ahead of her, off Kirin herself. Three shadows moving south through unclaimed ground.

She thought about the infrastructure report in the archive — the twelve-year-old document in its box, the tables of component specifications and failure mode projections, the address line reading the Compact's infrastructure review board. *No record of a response in the file.* Dez had said this and closed the report with the deliberate care of someone deciding how to handle something that had just changed its character.

She was carrying the documentation. She was heading toward the person Adaeze had named as the one who would know what to do with it. The instructions were specific and she was following them. That was the shape of what she knew.

What she did not know: whether Brigid would receive the infrastructure report the way Adaeze assumed. Whether Brigid's *three of three returned* was what Dez had read it as — active waiting, a supervisor who held a position for a reason. Or whether it was a managerial phrase Adaeze had read too much into, late, from four hundred miles, declining in health, in an apartment in Sable River with the herb pots on the window ledge and the key in the closet and three weeks left.

She did not say this to Dez. It was the kind of thing that needed to stay unspoken until it became a problem.

The light went. They made a dry camp in the lee of a long concrete pad — the foundation of a building stripped long ago, nothing left of the structure, the pad itself occupied by scrub through the expansion cracks. Above the water table. Out of the wind on three sides. Dez set a small portable heater between them, compact, low-signature, heat without light.

Kirin sat against the concrete and looked at the sky. The clouds had moved off completely. Stars, clear and numerous, the way they were only in places without ambient light. She had seen this sky three times: once as a girl on a school excursion to the eastern agricultural district, once on the first night east of Kesmark when she had crossed the frontier and found the sky was a different object than the one she'd grown up under, and now. Three times. She was twenty-eight years old.

She took out the log. She did not open it. She held it in her lap in the dark.

She had not read the sections that contained her own life. She had read up to 2104 and stopped at the August entry, the long entry, and she had not gone further. The log ran to 2126. Thirty-five years of entries, and she had read twelve.

She was not ready for the next twenty-three years. She was not sure ready was the right frame.

Dez was watching the northern sky. Not searching it — the focused observation of someone monitoring without expecting to find anything, the professional version of looking.

"When you found out she was gone," Dez said. "Your grandmother."

Kirin held the log.

"Three weeks before I opened the box," she said. "I was sorting the apartment. I thought I knew what I was looking for."

"You thought you were looking for what she left you."

"I was looking for what she left me. I was looking for the part of a person's life that transfers when the person is gone." She paused. "I didn't find it. I found the box. Which isn't the same thing."

"No." Dez looked back at the northern sky. "It usually isn't."

The heater put out its minimal warmth between them. A night animal moved through the short grass somewhere in the middle distance. The stars did not move in any way she could detect.

She put the log back in her pack.

"How long to Meridian."

"Two days if the weather holds. The last part depends on the border crossing and who's working it." Dez looked away from the sky. "I know one of the regular operators. If he's on shift, it's not complicated."

"And if he's not."

"A different kind of complicated." She lay back and closed her eyes. "Sleep. The first two hours are the best ones."

* * *

She woke before Dez.

The sky overhead was the deep charcoal of an hour before dawn, the stars still visible but faded. Cold settled into the concrete pad. She sat up and found the log by feel in her pack and put it in her lap and did not open it.

She had been reading for most of a night before leaving the campus. She had read five more hours in her head since then — not the words, but the shape of the document. The log had a shape. It was the shape of a working life in which very little was accidental and very little was explained, in which a person made decisions and noted them and moved to the next item, in which the days of grief and the days of plain satisfaction appeared in the same format because the format was the discipline and the discipline was the point.

Adaeze had been a logistics auditor before she was a witness. The log read like audit reports. That was not a failure of intimacy. That was what she had.

Dez woke without announcement — one moment still, the next sitting upright and looking at the horizon with the alert, organized assessment of someone whose waking and operating states were not meaningfully different.

"Go," she said.

They packed the camp without speaking. The heater was cold; its fuel cell was depleted exactly as designed, nothing to dispose of except the empty cell, which Dez pocketed. Kirin ate a piece of flat bread from her pack while walking. The first light was coming up in the east when they reached the second monitoring unit — same profile as yesterday's, different position. Dez looked at it briefly. Not steered around this time. Straight past, close enough to read the unit's serial plate.

"Same operator," Dez said.

"Is that good."

"Means it's systematic, not targeted. The systematic ones don't chase."

They walked on through the broadening light.

* * *

The man who came to the campus arrived on foot from the south road, thirty-one hours after Kirin and Dez left it.

Fen watched him from the battery room window as he crossed the perimeter — a man in his forties, in Compact travel clothing that was clean and correctly sized, with a pack right for a three-day field deployment and a case on his hip that could have been a tool case or a data case and was, by the balance of probabilities, both. He walked without the corridor's conserving gait. He walked like someone covering a predetermined distance on a predetermined schedule.

Fen put down the battery discharge log and went to the north building's door.

The man came to the property fence. He looked at the buildings with the systematic survey of someone making a record rather than a decision.

"What can I do for you," Fen said from the doorway.

"Routine transit survey. Maintenance grid assessment, eastern sectors." The man's voice was unhurried, institutional. "Are you the facility manager."

"Manage this campus," Fen said. "Yes."

"Personnel on-site."

"One."

"That's you."

"Yes."

The man wrote something on a handheld unit. He looked at the yard and the buildings with the systematic survey of someone making a record rather than a decision.

"Unauthorized transit through this area in the last seven days."

"People pass through the corridor." Fen leaned against the door frame. It was a stance he had arrived at over three years of this kind of conversation — not aggressive, not welcoming. A person with a door who had decided whether to open it. "What sector are you assessing."

"Secondary grid. Sectors six through nine."

"Campus is in sector seven."

"Yes." Another entry on the handheld. "I need to confirm the status of the maintenance access terminal for this sector. Location records indicate a terminal on the south building."

"South building is sealed."

"I need to access it for the survey."

Fen looked at him. The man looked back. The afternoon light was flat and neutral between them.

"Terminal is not currently in survey cycle," Fen said. "If the sector assessment requires terminal access, you'll need to file through the infrastructure division. Can give you the contact address."

"I'm authorized for direct access."

"Contact address is on the public registry." He did not move from the door frame.

The man studied him with the quality of someone applying a diagnostic — checking his read of the person in the doorway against the person's responses, updating the model. Fen was familiar with this kind of study. He had been on the receiving end of it many times. He had nothing to change in his presentation because he had nothing he was performing. He was a campus manager with an administrative constraint and a door.

"I'll note the non-compliance in the survey record," the man said.

"That's appropriate."

The man wrote something. Then he looked at the yard — not the buildings but the space between them, at the ground, at the condition of the compacted soil and gravel. Looking for track evidence. It had been thirty-one hours since two people walked out of this yard. Tracks on compacted surfaces did not last thirty-one hours.

"One other question. Has anyone accessed the sealed building's access panel in the last seventy-two hours."

"Don't know what the sealed building's access panel does," Fen said. "Building is sealed."

"A reader panel on the ramp beside the south building's main door. It would indicate a recent access event."

"Panel might have an indicator. Haven't looked at it."

The man made a final entry. He looked at Fen for a count of three.

"Thank you for your cooperation."

"Sure," Fen said.

He watched the man walk back to the perimeter road and continue south. The man's pace did not change. He did not look back.

Fen went inside and stood at the counter.

The network alert would go through the relay channel that didn't run through the north terminal. He knew the procedure. The network would close the site faster than the current window specified — that was probably already in motion from the off-campus relay, which would have noticed the survey query before the man arrived, because the survey query had gone through the infrastructure division's standard channel and the standard channel was monitored.

He picked up the battery discharge log from the counter.

He looked at the page.

He put it down and picked up his tea instead.

The campus was quiet around him. The solar arrays were producing at the afternoon rate. The cistern was holding its level. The agricultural bay's cold storage was three degrees above spec and he would need to look at the thermostat before dark. These were the things that needed doing and he would do them, in order, and the site would be ready to close when the network said to close it.

He went to the thermostat.

* * *

The outer circulation road appeared below them on the second afternoon.

Not the smell, which had been in the air since the night camp — that flat, mineral openness that arrived before salt did, the air of land transitioning between two states. The smell had been there. But the road was the first definitive thing, and she looked at it from the crest of the slope for a moment before going down.

A working road. Vehicles in both directions, continuous enough to keep the verge trimmed to the concrete. Not the managed-sector spur with its systematic sight-line clearance and fifteen-degree drainage cuts. Not the corridor's careful infrastructure budget in a zone nobody was supposed to know was funded. A road a city needed, maintained because it needed it, for no other reason.

"It looks the same," Kirin said.

"Roads do."

"I know roads do." She paused. "I was expecting different."

"The different part is in the city." Dez looked at the road. "Out here it's just a road."

They went down the slope.

The descent took thirty minutes on a path that had been a service track once, marked by occasional concrete culverts where the slope drained. At the bottom: a flat margin of gravel between the track and the road's verge, wide enough to stand in and watch the traffic without being in it. She stood there for a moment.

Three vehicles in two minutes. A cargo unit moving east at regulated speed, its load secured under gray cover. A passenger vehicle going west with the particular road-worn look of long-distance transit. A smaller two-wheel unit in the far lane that passed quickly and was gone.

None of them looked at her.

She had been in a world where everything she carried made her a person of interest — where her jacket pocket and her pack configuration and the way she distributed her weight were readable by everyone who had reason to look. She was at the edge of a city of — how many people? She did not know Meridian's population. She did not know if the Compact had accurate figures, or if the accurate figures were the kind of thing a Compact citizen would think to look up. She had not thought to look it up.

She would be nobody here. A traveler with a pack, arriving from the transitional zone, heading for the Trade District with a contact address written in her grandmother's hand on an index card she had been carrying since the archive.

She stepped off the gravel and onto the road's verge and kept walking south.

The outer edge of Meridian's approach zone announced itself in small infrastructure decisions visible from the road: a monitoring post at the quarter-mile, a roadside service bay at the half-mile, a transit schedule board with current information. Not flashy. Functional in a different mode than the Compact's functional — the service bay carried advertisements on its outer wall, three of them in competing visual registers. Advertisement was not a category the Compact used. There was nothing to advertise: you needed something, you went to the CapEx Board, the Board matched you to the resource. You did not need to be persuaded.

She read them as she passed and looked away.

"Gilles' shift starts at second hour," Dez said. "We have forty minutes."

The transit rest stop was a covered pavilion with benches and automated dispensers. Two other travelers were there — a man in heavy work gear with his pack at his feet and the expression of someone waiting out a delay, and an older woman reading a tablet with the complete absorption of a person who had been in transit long enough to stop registering her surroundings. Neither looked up.

Dez bought two items from the dispensers. A beverage in a sealed container and something in a flat wrapper — a protein bar in a flavor Kirin didn't recognize. She handed both to Kirin without comment and bought the same for herself.

The beverage was cold and synthetic and fine. She ate the protein bar in three bites. Her body processed calories without consulting her preferences.

"Gilles," Kirin said.

"He's worked this crossing for six years. He knows me, knows I bring through legitimate travelers, knows that legitimate for me does not mean registered in Meridian's system." Dez ate her own bar. "He has a fee. It is not unreasonable."

"What currency."

"He takes barter or Free Zone scrip. He does not take EQU. He does not take EQU because EQU cannot cross the border, which you know. But also because accepting EQU would mean acknowledging that the person offering it came from inside the Compact, and Gilles has a professional disinclination to acknowledge things like that in his crossing log."

"What does he take in barter."

"I have trade items for it." She looked at Kirin. "What you're carrying stays in your pack."

"I wasn't going to offer it."

"I know you weren't. I'm telling you so you don't look like you're considering whether to offer it, because that expression is legible and Gilles reads legible very well."

Kirin looked at the man across the pavilion. He was asleep now, or close to it, his head against the post behind him. The woman reading the tablet had not looked up once.

The man woke briefly, looked at both of them with the flat disinterest of someone who had been in transit too long to be curious, and went back to sleep.

"The day-shift operator," Kirin said. "The one you don't prefer."

"Lemke. Two years at this crossing. He has not yet developed Gilles' understanding of what crosses quietly and what doesn't." Dez watched the time indicator on the pavilion's information board. "He also has a secondary processing facility referral rate that I find professionally offensive."

"He's a problem."

"He's a problem if he's on shift. He's not on shift." She stood and put her wrappers in the disposal. "Come on."

* * *

The crossing was a low building at the road's edge, staffed from a window on the right side of a single-lane approach. A barrier arm blocked the lane. A camera on a post tracked it.

A man in his fifties leaned out the window as they came to the lane.

He saw Dez before he saw Kirin. His expression shifted — not warm. Something more efficient than warm. The recognition of a known quantity.

"Dez."

"Gilles." She put her hand on the window ledge. "Two for the Trade District, one-night transit."

He looked at Kirin. The look was the standard entry-assessment she had been getting since the corridor — boots, pack configuration, how she was carrying her weight. He filed what he found.

"Anything I should note."

"Two travelers, Trade District bound, nothing to declare."

He looked at Kirin again, briefly, with the quality of a look that was recording rather than reading — putting a face with a transit event for a log that would not be reviewed unless something went wrong. She met it without adjusting herself.

"Barter rate is one for two, current commodity list," he said.

Dez reached into her pack and produced two items in sealed packaging. She put them on the window ledge without breaking the exchange into negotiation. Gilles looked at them, picked them up, put them in the desk behind him without examining them.

He pressed the barrier control.

The arm lifted.

"Trade District is four kilometers east on the ring road," he said. "Follow the numbered signs."

"Thank you," Dez said.

He was already looking at the next thing on his desk.

* * *

They walked the ring road as the day's last light went.

The city announced itself in layers. First the changed air — warm from aggregate, carrying combustion and the particular quality of a place where many people lived in close proximity and had been doing it for a long time. Then sound: not loud, but continuous, the baseline of a working city that had not yet committed to night. Then lights — not the strip-LED efficiency of the Compact's functional illumination but a varied, uncoordinated scatter of types and temperatures, a place that had grown without a unified standard because growth had been enough to ask of it.

The ring road fed onto the Transit Corridor. The Transit Corridor fed into the Trade District's outer approach.

She could see the second floor of a building above the dyers' lane from the entrance. She did not know which building it was yet. She would know it by the smell, Adaeze had assumed, without explaining how Kirin would know what dyers smelled like.

She would find out tomorrow.

The Trade District was narrower than she had expected from its name. The Transit Corridor was wide and lit and moved fast, but the District itself stepped down off the main artery into a grid of secondary streets where the buildings were older — two- and three-story structures in a construction style she didn't recognize, the upper floors built out over the street level on wooden brackets so that the street narrowed as it rose. Signs on most of the storefronts, many of them hand-lettered, in three or four scripts she could identify and at least one she couldn't. The smell changed from the street-level exhaust of the main corridor to something more layered: food from an open counter on the left, oil and hot metal from a workshop with the shutters still up, and underneath both of those a sharp vegetal smell that she associated with nothing in her experience and that got stronger as they went east into the block.

"Dyers," Dez said, without pointing.

Kirin looked. A building on the right, its ground floor open to the street, lengths of dyed material hanging in the interior in the particular compressed-vertical display of a working operation. The smell came from the vats she could not see from the street but could locate by the ventilation stack on the building's side.

Second floor above that.

She stopped at the approach entrance and looked at the city. It had the density and noise and particular irreducible business of a place where human needs were being continuously negotiated at close range. The Compact had cities too. She had grown up in one.

She had expected to feel like she was arriving somewhere foreign.

She felt like she was arriving somewhere. That was enough.

She put her hand in her jacket pocket. The key was cold — dormant in the city the same way it had been dormant for most of her life, inert, waiting for something that spoke its language. Whatever language the city's infrastructure spoke, the key did not yet know it.

It would find its relationships when they reached the right room.

She took her hand out of her pocket. She picked up her pace.

She followed Dez into the light.
