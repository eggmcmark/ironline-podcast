# Episode 5: "The Reading Room"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin and Dez are escorted east from Harrow Creek to a campus in the managed sector, where an authentication key opens an underground archive and the inheritance turns out to be a library.*

---

The person who came from east came on foot.

She appeared in the road as a figure in the long afternoon light, walking toward the junction post without looking at anything except the post. She had the even stride of someone who had covered a lot of flat ground on foot and had learned to reserve everything for the distance. She was forty, possibly older. Her clothes were the plain-functional kind that the corridor seemed to produce in people over time: no patches, no insignia, nothing that announced who she worked for. She had a canvas pack slung at a slant that said she knew how to carry weight and had been doing it today.

She reached the post. She lifted the cap. She read the chip.

She held it up and said, to both of them at once: "Two, eastbound, Naila's account."

It was not a question.

"Yes," Kirin said.

The woman pocketed the chip. She did not return it. She looked at Kirin for a moment, then at Dez, with the assessment-pass of someone who had been reading arriving strangers for a long time and had grown efficient at it. Whatever she concluded, she kept it.

"Vehicle is a quarter mile east," she said. "Left it off the road. Walked in so the approach didn't announce." She picked up her pack from where she'd set it to use both hands on the cap. "You have forty miles on foot or forty miles in the vehicle. Your call."

"Vehicle," Dez said.

The woman nodded. She turned east on the road and walked at the same even pace.

Kirin and Dez picked up the loaded frame and followed.

The vehicle was where she said it would be, pulled off the road behind a low concrete shelter that had once been a rest area and now was a shell with the roof intact and the interior walls tagged with a notation system Kirin didn't recognize — not graffiti, smaller, methodical. She looked at the marks as they passed. A maintenance record. Someone had been logging visits here in a code that was not Compact format and not any Free Zone standard she'd been trained to cross-reference at the Board. A third system, adapted to this specific use, which meant somebody had been here often enough and long enough that improvising a system had been worth the trouble.

The vehicle was a light four-wheel with an enclosed cab and an open cargo bed. Old — not museum-old, but older than any working vehicle she had seen in Sable River, the body repainted over original rust in stages, each new coat a slightly different shade of the same gray-brown, the seams of the old panels filled with something that held. The engine ran on LPG and the tank was full. The woman knew this because she checked the gauge by touch without looking at it, the way a person checks a thing they check every time they get in.

They loaded the frame. Dez took the front seat. Kirin went to the back.

"Ama," the woman said, starting the engine.

That was her name. She did not repeat it or expand on it.

She pulled onto the road east and drove at a steady pace, and neither Kirin nor Dez asked her anything, and she offered nothing, and the vehicle moved through the late afternoon with the windshield catching the low sun dead ahead.

* * *

The road east of Harrow Creek was different from the maintenance spur between Larch and the junction.

Kirin had her hand on the cab behind the passenger seat and could feel the road through the vehicle's suspension, and what she felt was age — the deep patched aggregate of a road that had been repaired many times over many years by many methods, none of them exactly matching. The spur from Larch had been recently resurfaced in sections, new work on an old substrate. This road had been maintained continuously for a long time. Not new work but accumulated work: thirty years of small decisions adding up to a surface that was functional without looking it.

The key was warm.

It had begun warming when the vehicle moved, and it had not stopped. Not the cycling warmth of the spur — not the handshake-and-release pattern she'd learned to read between Larch and Harrow Creek. A lower, steadier warmth, like a pilot light: something on, a signal continuously maintained. She took it out of her pocket and held it in her fist and felt it pulse, faint and even, twice per minute. Something out here was listening for it and finding it on each pass.

She counted the maintenance posts on the road's verge. At the Board, she had cross-referenced post spacing in secondary infrastructure protocols as part of provenance verification on materials claims. Standard Compact secondary roads used four-hundred-meter post spacing on open corridors. These posts were at two-fifty. A tighter grid, which meant tighter monitoring, which meant the resources assigned to this corridor east of Harrow Creek were greater than the resources assigned to the spur west of it.

Somebody had invested more here.

She did not say this to Ama. Ama drove.

The road went east through a landscape of old-growth reclaimed by native scrub — birch and sumac growing up through the broken asphalt of secondary roads that the main route crossed over twice on narrow concrete bridges. At the second bridge, the key pulsed four times rapidly instead of twice, and Kirin held still in the back seat and counted to be sure.

Four.

She put her arm out the cab window and looked back at the bridge as they cleared it. From the underside, partly visible from the angle: a dark square housing, bolted to the interior of the span, new hardware in an old span. Not the same size or profile as the Curator's installation at mile forty-four. A different form factor — larger, and with a second cable run alongside the first that the Larch installation hadn't had.

She pulled her arm back in.

Dez looked at her over the seat back. Dez had seen her reach. Dez's face was doing the thing it did when she was filing information for later: nothing visible, something stored. Kirin met her eyes and said nothing. Dez looked back at the road.

They drove in silence for another twenty minutes, through country that flattened as it opened. The old-growth scrub gave way to managed agricultural land — not the wild managed grass of the maintenance corridor, but actual cultivation. Rows, spacing, the dark soil of recent tilling. A crop she didn't recognize on the right, growing in low hummocks that the evening light caught from the side to make look like a field of small shadows. A stand of fruit trees on the left, bare still from winter, in an orchard configuration that somebody had planned.

"Who works this land," Kirin said.

"People who live here," Ama said.

That was all she said.

Kirin did not ask a second time.

The key's pulse steadied to once per minute, longer between, as if whatever was listening had moved from active search to passive monitoring. She became aware that the sensation in her hand had changed: the warmth was no longer just radiating up from the contact pads but was distributed through the whole object, as if the key itself had warmed its interior rather than its surface. She had not felt this before. She had felt the key react to external things. This felt like the key had stopped reacting and started being.

She did not know what to do with that observation, so she set it aside the way she set aside anomalous readings at the Board — recorded, not dismissed, return to it when more data arrived.

* * *

The campus showed itself slowly, the way large things sometimes showed themselves.

A changed quality of the light first, a reflection from something flat and angled ahead. Then, resolving through the windshield, a line of solar panels in a ground-mounted array at forty-five degrees behind a low embankment — positioned to be invisible from road level except at this specific approach angle, in this specific evening light. Then a water tower, painted to match the surrounding scrub. The tower had a freshly welded patch on one side, the weld beads still bright, done inside the last month. Someone up there was maintaining something that needed maintenance.

Then buildings.

They were low and widely spaced, with the construction character of modular prefab that had been modified extensively over a long period. Not planned as a campus — built as something else and adapted into a campus function over decades. The adaptations were visible in the material record the way they always were: a loading dock on a building that had no logistical use now, sealed but not removed; a wall of windows on a building whose current interior would not benefit from them, the windows shuttered from inside; the deliberate low-profile orientation of every structure, nothing more than one story above the surrounding ground cover, nothing vertical enough to read at a distance against the skyline.

This place had been designed to be invisible from every direction except the road you were already on.

Ama pulled off the main surface into a track between two buildings and stopped. She left the engine on. She got out and went to the cargo bed and unloaded the frame with the same compressed economy as the man at Larch had loaded it — no wasted motions, nothing double-handled, everything set on the ground in the balanced configuration it would need to be picked up in. She got back in the vehicle. She turned it around on the track, tight, in two passes. She pulled back toward the main road.

Kirin stood in the track with her pack on the ground and watched the vehicle disappear around the building.

She waited for the sound of it on the main road. She heard it accelerate and diminish and then she heard nothing.

"Well," Dez said.

"Yes."

Dez looked at the north building. At the lit windows behind the shutters — a yellow spill at the shutter edges.

"Lights on."

"She said north building."

Dez picked up one end of the frame.

They went in.

* * *

The north building was warm.

Not the residual warmth of a wood stove, not the close warmth of a room with too many people in it. The steady, contained warmth of a building with a functioning climate system maintaining a specific temperature, the air moving with the faint, purposeful circulation of ducts. A system working. Not recently switched on — the walls had been warm for hours, the floor warm through her boots as she walked in from outside.

The hallway light overhead was LED strip, cool white, on. At the end of the hallway a door stood open onto a common room. She went toward it.

The common room had tables and chairs and a long counter along one wall with kitchen apparatus on it: a water heater, a cabinet, a stack of ceramic cups in a production run she didn't recognize, made for durability rather than design. Two of the cups had rings in them from recent use. A third was still half full of something dark — not quite cold yet, the steam gone but the surface still settling. Somebody had left it there a short while ago.

A pair of boots was just inside the door to a side room, toe-out the way boots are left when the person wearing them is not in a hurry to be somewhere else.

The key was warmer here than it had been in the vehicle. Deeper warm. The distributed warmth she'd felt on the road, but more of it.

Then the side room's door opened and a man came into the common room.

He was in his late thirties, Kirin estimated, or older in the way that people who worked outdoors aged — the skin weathered, the eyes clear, the body in the kind of shape that wasn't cultivated but just remained, the way a good tool remained when it was used. He had on work clothing that had been washed many times without ceremony. He had a data reader in his hand that he set on the counter when he saw them, without looking down.

He looked at Kirin. He looked at her jacket pocket with the specific focus she'd been getting since Larch, and by now that look was familiar enough that she simply put her hand in the pocket and took out the key and held it up.

He looked at it for a moment. Something in his face settled, the way a reading settles when the assay confirms what the surface suggested.

"Been waiting on that," he said. "Not specifically on you. On the key." He went to the counter and picked up his half-full cup and turned to the water heater. "Fen."

"Your name," Dez said.

"Yes. You want something hot."

Kirin set her pack down. "Yes."

"Water or the tea. Tea is an acquired taste. I've acquired it, which tells you something and I'm not sure what."

"Water," Dez said.

"Tea," Kirin said.

Fen made two cups from the water heater without ceremony. He handed them across the counter. He did not offer a chair. He did not come around to their side of the counter.

"How long were you at the post."

"Four hours."

"Ama usually makes it in three. Last stretch of road went soft in the winter. Haven't fixed it yet." He picked up his own cup. "How much do you know."

"Three factions in the corridor. The Curator. The coordinates I was given at the Archive in Sable River."

"You're a materials analyst."

"Was."

He looked at her steadily. "Still," he said, with no particular inflection, as if he were correcting a measurement error.

He drank his tea.

"I've been here three years," he said. "Eighteen months of that alone. The previous steward went west and didn't make the arrangement for a handover. I didn't know that until after. The network said somebody would come with the key. They were approximate about when." He set down the cup. "The key opens the archive. You know what the coordinates were registered to."

"A Rootstock Protocol equity claim."

Fen looked at her. "That's what the registration says."

"That's what the records at the Territorial Archive say."

"The records there are the summary. The archive here is what the summary was made from." He put his cup on the counter and went to the side room and came back out with his boots. He sat on a stool behind the counter and began putting them on. "The claim is registered to a location. The location is here. The asset isn't equity in the way the Compact reads equity. You'll understand it when you see it, and you should see it in the daylight, what's left of it." He tied the boot, stood, and looked at her. "There's a campus. Eleven buildings, eight in current use, two I don't use, one that's sealed. The sealed building is the one you want." He came around the counter and went to the hallway without looking back. "The key opens the lower level. If you try it on the main door, it opens the main door. Don't use the main door. There's a panel on the right side of the ramp — lower right, Rootstock interface format. Use that one."

He waited at the hallway entrance.

They followed him out.


* * *

Fen walked the campus the way he'd described it — practically, without performance.

Here is the agricultural structure, three bays, two in winter prep, one running cold storage for the root crop that came out of the field in March. Here is the cistern and the purification setup, sand and carbon filtration, UV final stage. Here are the solar banks and the battery room — don't go in the battery room without telling him first, the ventilation was configured for a specific loading cycle and the charge state on the east bank was unpredictable. Here are the two eastern buildings he didn't use, both sealed at the door with a horizontal pin through the hasp, no padlock, no complexity. One of them held equipment that had come with the campus and had no use he'd found. The other he'd been told was for the network's use when people passed through and he'd never seen anyone use it.

Here is the sealed building.

It was the same low prefab form factor as the others, indistinguishable from a distance. Up close: the modifications. A door that had been cut wider than the original framing at some point, new framing installed around the opening, the joint between old and new material visible as a color difference in the painted surface — the original gray and the new gray aging at slightly different rates. Windows shuttered from inside. A concrete ramp leading to the wider door, poured more recently than the building — the aggregate was a finer grade than the surrounding ground pours, the surface finish smoother, a different mix design.

And beside the ramp, at the door's right, a reader panel.

The panel was the same type she'd used at Larch but older, the aluminum housing oxidized through two or three finish cycles, the contact-reader window darkened at the edges from years of use. The Larch panel had been five years old. This one was closer to fifteen, maybe more. It had been serviced in the interval — she could see where someone had cleaned the contact surface with an abrasive and re-seated the frame with fresh compound — but it was the original hardware.

The key was very warm now. Not the cycling warmth of the corridor nodes or the sharp heat of the overpass tap. A deeper warmth — distributed through the whole object the way a stone was warm after a full day in sunlight, warmth that had gone down into the material. She had been feeling this change since the vehicle turned off the main road and she had not said so and she said so now to no one.

She stepped up the ramp. She took the key from her pocket. She pressed it flat against the reader panel, contact-pads down, the four raised squares in the corners of the back face finding the window exactly, the fit not approximate but precise.

Nothing for a count of three.

Then the panel's status light went from amber to a color she had not seen a status light go: a deep green that was almost blue, which meant nothing in the Compact's standard hardware encoding and presumably meant something in whatever language this hardware spoke.

The door seal released with a sound she could feel through the ramp under her feet — not a click but a pressure change, the equalization of a room that had been holding its temperature and now released it, the warmth of the interior breathing out around the door's edge. The smell that came out with the warmth: paper and something else, the clean mineral smell of acid-free storage containers. She had smelled it at the Territorial Archive in Sable River. She had smelled it in the reference stacks at the CapEx Board. This was deeper than either of those — older, more concentrated.

The key went still in her hand.

Not cold, not warm. Still. The question asked and answered, the circuit completed and at rest.

Fen pulled the door open from the outside edge and stepped in. Kirin and Dez followed.

* * *

The lower level was larger than the building's footprint suggested.

It extended underground — the ramp outside came to grade level at the south face, but the interior stepped down to a basement that ran under the footprints of the two adjacent buildings, supported by a poured-concrete structural grid she could see in the low ceiling above her. The grid was sound. She read it in the column spacing, in the way the concrete had settled evenly, in the absence of the hairline cracks that meant differential settlement, in the clean faces of the columns, no spalling, no rebar ghost.

Whoever had poured this ceiling had poured it to last.

The room was divided into a regular grid of metal shelving — not improvised, not the found-object shelving of a person making do, but purpose-built open shelving, cold-rolled steel with adjustable bracket positions set at a consistent height throughout. Library format. Standard aisle width. Twelve aisles, each numbered on a small card holder at the end cap. The aisles ran north-south, long, disappearing into the lit middle distance of the room. Overhead: LED strip lighting at calculated intervals, cool white, on a circuit that had clearly come on when the door opened — she hadn't seen anyone flip a switch.

The material on the shelves was in white acid-free archival boxes, labeled on the spine with a nomenclature system she didn't immediately recognize. Not Compact registry format. Not the free-field labeling of a private collection. A systematic code: two letters, three numbers, a date range. The date ranges ran from 2088 to the present year. Some of the date ranges ended at 2125. Some were open.

She walked to the nearest shelf and pulled a box from the middle. Label: MR-014, 2097-2103. Inside: printed volumes in a waterproof wrap, tightly packed, the compressed form factor of books that needed to take up minimal space. She pulled one and read the title page.

*Population Movement: Eastern Corridor Districts, 2097–2099. Compiled by Outside Records, Sector Seven.*

She put it back.

She pulled a box from further down: MR-031, 2103-2107. Infrastructure reports. Inside: technical documents with the specific formatting of engineering assessments — tables, figures, condition ratings, maintenance recommendations. She read the header on the first document. *Maintenance Grid Assessment: Secondary Water Management, Sectors 4–8.* The document had been produced by an organization whose name she didn't recognize, and it had been addressed to a board whose name she also didn't recognize, and both of those facts occupied a corner of her mind like two measurements whose difference she hadn't yet resolved.

She put the box back.

She looked down the aisle.

"What is this," she said.

"What it looks like," Fen said, from three meters back. He had not followed her to the shelves. He stood at the entrance to the aisle with his hands in his pockets, watching her with the particular watchfulness of a person who had been alone with something for a long time and was now watching someone encounter it for the first time.

"It's a library."

"It's an archive. There's a difference." He looked at the shelves as though he'd had this thought before and was stating it from habit rather than wanting to explain it. "A library is for reading. An archive is for keeping. You can read here. The primary function is existing."

"What does it contain."

"Twelve aisles. Population records. Infrastructure documentation. Economic records. Trade records. Medical records going back to 2088. Historical records from the transition era — documentation that was never transferred to the Compact's records system because what it documented wasn't Compact-compatible." He paused. "And three aisles of material that don't fit a clean category."

"The Outside's records."

"The Outside's records of itself. What it is. Where it is. How large it is. What it has built." He looked at her. "Your grandmother's section is in aisle seven."

* * *

Aisle seven.

The boxes here were sparser than the surrounding aisles, more carefully spaced. The labels used the same nomenclature as the rest of the archive, but the date ranges were shorter and the codes included a suffix she hadn't seen in the adjacent aisles: a single letter after the number. MR-047a. MR-047b. MR-062a.

She pulled the nearest one: MR-047a, 2091-2095.

Inside: printed documents in waxed sleeves. She opened one sleeve and found a folded map — not printed, hand-drawn, in ink on drafting paper, with a legend in the lower corner in a handwriting she recognized without preparing herself for it.

She held the map to the reading light. The legend read:

*Ledger coverage: present (solid line); projected six months (dashed line); six-month error margin (shaded). Site references: see companion file MR-047b.*

The handwriting was her grandmother's.

The map was dated in the upper right corner: March 2095. Kirin had been eight years old in March 2095. Adaeze had been in her early sixties, working as a Commons health aide in Sable River, and she had been here — in this room, at one of these reading tables — making this map in her own hand.

Kirin set the map flat on the top of the shelving unit and looked at it.

"How long has she been coming here," she said.

"First signature in the records is 2087," Fen said. "That's the earliest I've found. The archive's formal record begins in 2088, so she was here before the formal record began. She may have been here before I can account for." He paused. "She was here in 2124 and in 2125. Late September into October."

"What was she doing."

"She pulled three files. Read them in the east reading room. Replaced them exactly — I could tell because she filed one of them in a different sub-position than it had been, and when I re-shelved it I found the note." He moved to the end of the aisle and took a small card from the holder on the end cap. He held it out.

Kirin took it.

Two items in Adaeze's hand. The first: a document reference, MR-116c, third enclosure. The second: a single sentence. *Read it in the east reading room. Not the north terminal.*

"What's the north terminal."

"An older reader panel. It will authenticate your key. It will also authenticate to a secondary network I don't control. The east reading room has a standalone terminal — no external connection." He looked at her. "Your grandmother knew about the north terminal. She was the one who told me it had been compromised. Two years ago. She sent word through the network."

"She sent word."

"A data transfer through a courier. She didn't come herself that trip." He paused. "The next time she came was September twenty-one twenty-five. The trip you know about."

Kirin put the index card in her jacket pocket. She folded the map and put it back in its sleeve and the sleeve back in the box and the box back on the shelf. Her hands did the work without asking her to supervise them.

She looked down the aisle. Thirty-plus years of her grandmother's documented presence here, in boxes on a steel shelf in a basement under a building that didn't exist on any map she had ever read. The air was cool and dry and very still. The climate system held the temperature constant.

She did not reach for the key again. The key was already warm.

* * *

Far south of the campus, at a way-station called Salt, two people arrived in a vehicle she had not seen on this route before.

That was the first thing Naila noted: the vehicle. Not a Compact-colors unit, not the gray-green of Free Zone field crews — a plain utility vehicle with civilian-cut gear that was too complete. Travelers ran short of something. They patched, made do, wore the wrong boot for the weather. These two had the right boot, the right coat, the right pack configuration for a two-day corridor transit. Someone had outfitted them for a specific job and done it correctly, which meant the job had been planned in advance.

They asked for her by name.

She came to the gate. She read them the way she read everyone at the gate — boots, weight distribution, what hand reached for what in a stress reflex, what their eyes did when they weren't trying to direct them. What their eyes did, in both cases, was go to the gate latch and immediately away. Not the curiosity of travelers trying to work out the mechanism. Automatic door-mapping.

"I'm Naila," she said. "What do you want."

"Two travelers," the closer one said. "One Kirin Saleh. One unknown female companion. We were told they were here."

"I have a lot of people here."

"Last week."

"Then I've seen them and they're gone and I don't know where."

The farther one was looking at the outbuildings. She did not look at what he was doing. She looked at the one who was talking.

"You keep a record," he said.

"Not for strangers."

"We're not strangers to the corridor."

"You're two people I've never seen who know my name and want to look at my ledger. I have a specific record for that type of person and the record is no."

The talker paused. She watched him recalculate — the small adjustment of a man who had expected one kind of friction and was getting another.

"She's carrying something valuable," he said. "Something that should come back."

"People who come through here carry a lot of things."

"This is a specific object. We need to locate the person who has it."

Naila looked at him for a moment. Then she looked at the road south — the road she had actually sent Kirin on, three days ago, in Dez's vehicle before she gave the vehicle to the Larch depot. She looked at it in the way a person looks at something they are about to tell a careful truth about.

"Woman and a companion left here heading north," she said. "Three days ago. I don't know what they were carrying. I don't track what people carry."

The talker wrote something on a small device.

The far one came back from the outbuildings and said one word to his partner: "North."

"North," Naila confirmed.

They did not say thank you. People on jobs of this kind rarely did. She watched them get back in their vehicle and turn it north on the main road, and she watched the vehicle until it was out of sight.

Then she went inside and stood at the counter.

She did not look at the ledger. She already knew what she would find if she did — Adaeze's entry from September, three nights, the question and its answer, and the open credit entry she had been carrying since the afternoon Adaeze left going west with the knowledge that the Curator was still on his route and the corridor was not clear.

Adaeze had told her once, on one of the early visits, that the watching would go on after her. That the key would come east with whoever she'd left it to. That Salt might be one of the last places the key would stop before it reached where it was going. She had said it the way she said most things: practically, as briefing, not as valediction.

Naila had believed her and had continued believing her for thirty-five years, and Adaeze had come back one last time in September to confirm the corridor was not clear, and then she had not come back again.

The kettle was on. The tea would be ready in a few minutes.

Naila picked up her cloth and continued what she had been doing before they came.


* * *

The east reading room was a bay cut from the north wall of the archive.

Two tables. Four chairs. A freestanding terminal on a steel stand, the housing case a style she recognized as early-Compact era — manufactured before the Guild certification system had standardized the industrial aesthetic, when things were still being made to the specs of whoever made them rather than to a unified standard. The screen faced east, away from the main aisles. A panel light above the terminal came on separately when Fen hit a switch near the bay entrance, warmer light than the aisle LEDs, a temperature useful for reading.

"Three hours to full dark," Fen said. "After that the exterior lights go off. The building seals — door relocks on a timed mechanism, six hours, opens at first light."

"And if we're not inside when it relocks."

"Then you're not inside." He looked at her. "I suggest you're inside."

He left the bay.

Kirin set her pack against the near wall. She pulled her jacket off and draped it over the back of a chair. She looked at the room.

Then she walked the aisles.

She went through aisle two first, reading the shelf labels at the end caps without pulling boxes. Then three, four, five, six. The economic records in aisles five and six were the most densely shelved — boxes packed tight, the date labels running in overlapping ranges, material from multiple sources cross-referenced by a notation system whose logic she was beginning to understand. The Outside had been doing this systematically, not as a personal archive but as an institutional one. Someone had designed the organization. Someone had maintained it. Fen had maintained it for three years, and before Fen someone else had, and before that someone else.

She stopped at the entrance to aisle three, labeled *Outside, general* at both ends.

The boxes here were the largest in the archive, the labels the most recent, the date ranges running to the present year and some to the open end.

She went to aisle twelve, the one Fen had told her to leave.

She looked at it from the entrance without going in.

The shelves in aisle twelve held material in different containers than the rest of the archive — not acid-free boxes but sealed metal cases, the kind used for storage in environments with high humidity or temperature variation. The cases had no visible labels, only numeric codes stamped into the metal at the top. The cases were locked, small barrel locks at the front edge, the key pattern she did not recognize.

She went back to the east reading room.

Dez was already in the adjacent aisle, two shelves over, pulling a box from the mid-range. She had been reading the labels in the infrastructure section since Fen left. Kirin sat at the table and looked at the terminal.

She had not tried the terminal yet. It would be waiting.

"Aisle two," Fen said, from somewhere near the entrance. She had not heard him come back. "Box MR-022, 2121 through 2125. Most recent complete survey. The methodology section is in the first volume. I'd read the methodology first."

"I would have," Kirin said.

He made a sound that was not laughter and was not nothing.

"The number in that box," he said, "is not a few hundred thousand."

He left again.

* * *

She brought box MR-022 to the reading table.

Heavy. Two volumes inside, printed, spiral-bound, the binding format of documents meant to be referenced rather than read straight through. She opened the first to the methodology section and read for fifteen minutes.

The methodology was specific and considered. Settlement enumeration using ground survey and aerial observation, cross-referenced with supply chain tracking, cross-referenced with water use and agricultural output by region. Error ranges clearly stated. Confidence levels given for each regional subsection. The compilers knew what they were doing — the survey had been designed by someone who understood where the error sources were and had built the methodology to bound them.

She read the methodology section until she was satisfied it would hold.

Then she turned to the population tables.

The tables ran by region: sector, subsector, estimated population, survey method, confidence rating. The regions covered the corridor, the eastern zones, the southern territories, and sections of the former Midwest and Southwest that she had spent her whole life understanding as depopulated, as genuinely empty, as land that the population collapse had returned to something like wilderness.

The sector population estimates were not large on their own. Hundreds, thousands. A subsector here at four thousand, a cluster there at fifteen hundred. The kind of numbers that the Compact's "few hundred thousand in the empty zones" figure could absorb, if you took the figure charitably, if you believed the summary rather than the accounting.

The bottom-line total was on the last page of the first volume.

She read it.

She sat with it for a moment.

Then she closed the volume and looked at the table across from her, at the empty chair, at the terminal on its steel stand.

Six million, conservative methodology. The low number. The number the survey compilers had endorsed as their reliable floor. The note beneath it: *high-estimate methodology (including associated and partial affiliation) yields 11.2 million. Methodology debate ongoing. Conservative figure used for planning purposes.*

Six to eleven million people. Not between Compact cities. Not in the transit corridors. In the empty zones, in the places the Compact had written off in 2090 and stopped mapping, in the infrastructure of a world that had been built for ten billion and now sheltered four and a half and left the rest for anyone who found use for it.

She put the volume back in the box and the box back on the shelf in aisle two.

She went to get Dez's box.

Dez had pulled the infrastructure assessment she'd been reading and brought it to the second table in the east reading room. She was still reading when Kirin came back with box MR-047 from aisle seven — her grandmother's section. Dez did not look up.

Kirin set the box on her table and opened it and looked at what was inside. The documents in their waxed sleeves, the maps in their labeled rolls, the check-out record in a paper folder at the back of the box. She took out the check-out record and read through it.

Adaeze's name appeared eleven times in this box's record, running from 2091 to 2125. The 2125 entry was the last. *September 27. One day. MR-047a through d; MR-062 series; MR-116 series. East reading room. Returned exactly.* The notation "returned exactly" appeared after several of the entries. She looked at the earlier ones where it was absent and could not figure out the pattern.

She went to the index-card holder at the end of aisle seven and took the card — the one with Adaeze's note, the document reference and the warning about the north terminal. She looked at the document reference: MR-116c, third enclosure.

She went to the shelf and found MR-116c and brought it to the table.

Inside: three enclosures in waxed sleeves, numbered in pencil on the exterior. She took out the third.

It was a thin bound volume, hand-sewn binding, printed pages inside. The cover was labeled in Adaeze's hand: *Personal log. Adaeze Okonkwo. Witness record, Compact interior. 2091–2126.*

Not a letter. Not an address to Kirin. A log.

She set it on the table.

She did not open it yet.

* * *

Dez said: "The maintenance grid."

Kirin looked up.

Dez was looking at the document she'd pulled — a bound report, older, the cover showing the handling of something that had been consulted many times. She was not reading it now. She was looking at a specific page near the middle, not moving, in the way she'd looked at the road between Larch and Harrow Creek when she was working something out.

"The infrastructure assessment in section four," Dez said. "There's a vulnerability report in here. Twelve years old."

"What does it cover."

"A set of junction components in the secondary water management grid. Eastern sectors — sectors six through eight." She turned one page. "Installed in 2082 under a Compact maintenance contract. Never replaced. There's a failure mode: at a certain load cycle the casing fractures. When that happens the junction drains into the water table instead of the managed channel."

"What's the affected area."

"The water table for sectors six through eight." Dez looked up. "The sectors the Compact wrote off in 2090."

Kirin held still.

"The report is twelve years old," she said.

"Yes. Addressed to the Compact's infrastructure review board in 2113." Dez closed the report, slowly, with the deliberate care of someone deciding how to handle something that had just changed its character. "No record of a response in the file."

The climate system held its temperature. The reading-room light was warm and steady.

"You know those junctions," Kirin said.

Dez was quiet for a long time.

"Four years, before the corridor," she said. "Maintenance-grid infrastructure. Secondary grid, eastern districts. I worked the water management side for two of those years." She looked at the closed report. "I was at a junction that matches this profile. Same installation year, same load spec, same casing material. I ran it for eight months." She put the report on the table. "I didn't know what was downstream. I thought it was empty." She was quiet again. "I thought it was empty because that's what I was told."

The room was quiet.

Kirin said: "Is that why you're in the corridor."

Dez looked at her. Not with heat. With the flat steadiness she'd maintained since Salt, but underneath it something different — not a fracture, a seam. A place where the calibrated neutral had been maintained at cost.

"I'm in the corridor because the corridor is where I work," she said. "I'm still in the corridor because—" She stopped. She looked at the report. "Because I have interests that require me to be here. That's what I told Naila when she asked me the same question."

"And now your interests have changed."

"My interests have more content now." She looked at Kirin. "Which means our terms can have more content. If you want terms."

Kirin looked at her.

"I'm going to read my grandmother's log," she said. "Tonight. I'm going to take it with me when I leave, because leaving it means leaving it to the Curator when he comes and I'm not doing that. I'm also taking what I can of the population atlas."

"Fen may not agree."

"Fen may not."

Dez looked at the report on the table.

"I can photograph the infrastructure assessment," she said. "Before we leave. I have a tool for it."

"So do I."

"Then we photograph it twice and carry it separately. Two copies, two people, two routes out." She put the report back in its box, carefully. "If one of us doesn't make it, the other one does."

Kirin looked at her.

"Is that an offer or a condition."

"It's a term," Dez said. "I told you I had interests. I'm telling you what they are."

* * *

She opened the log.

The pages were printed, not handwritten — Adaeze had used whatever output method she had access to, different printers at different points in the thirty-five-year run, the early pages sharper and more formal, the later pages with the slightly warmer character of a more personal machine. The entries were dated, numbered, and specific. No lyric passages. No reflections on what things meant. Adaeze had written events the way Kirin had been trained to write assessment reports: date, object, condition, finding, action taken, result.

*Entry 1. September 14, 2091. Arrived Sable River. Transit from Larch depot via Naila's way-station. Object secured. Compact credentials established. Beginning employment at Commons health aide, Level One. Ledger registration under family name Saleh.*

She read forward.

The early entries were about establishing the life in Sable River. Finding the apartment. Building the cover. Making it real enough to hold — and the entries made clear that the effort was not cosmetic. Adaeze had committed to Sable River the way she had committed to the archive: practically, without sentimentality, doing the work. She had built the herb pots on the window ledge not as decoration but because she needed to know what she was doing when she visited the settlement gardens on the east edge of the corridor. She had made the tea from plants she grew because the plants were part of a practice she was keeping alive inside the Compact's frame.

*Entry 23. April 2094. Tomas Bergström's mother passed through the corridor. Did not stop at Salt. No contact. Made the apartment suitable for hosting. She did not come.*

Kirin stopped.

She looked at that entry for a long time.

*Tomas Bergström's mother.* Tomas, who had been her best friend since they were fourteen. Who had packed her bag for this journey and told her to buy salt and known what he was talking about because Tomas had a habit of giving instructions whose reasons emerged on the road.

She turned more pages. She was not reading every entry — too many, in the time she had. She was reading the ones that contained names she recognized and the ones in the last year of Adaeze's life.

The last thirty pages were 2124 and 2125, one or two entries per month, and then the entry dated February 14, 2126.

She read it.

It was short.

*Entry 287. February 14, 2126. Health is stable; less so than three weeks ago. Completed the review of MR-116 series. The three items I've marked are accessible in aisle seven, their references noted on the end-cap card. Do not use the north terminal — it will authenticate the key but will also authenticate to the Curator's secondary array. East reading room terminal only.*

*The three instructions are: One: take the population atlas to Dara, Meridian, Trade District, second floor of the building above the dyers. She will know what to do with it. Two: deliver the infrastructure report to the Foundation Assessor at Sable River — Brigid Aelfgar will receive it without asking how you found it. Three: find the child in the photograph. Her name is Nnedi Eze and she is not a child anymore.*

*Do not let the key remain in the archive building for more than three days. The north terminal logs presence.*

*One other thing: the network will ask you to take the witness role I held. You will know when they ask. You do not have to say yes. But you should know that it is a real question, not a formality, and that the answer has consequences in either direction.*

*The road is long, but there is water at the end of it.*

*Adaeze Okonkwo. Witness, Compact interior.*

Kirin sat back in her chair.

She had not planned to tell Dez what the log said tonight. She told her now.

She read it aloud, not the whole log but the last entry, from the beginning. She read it straight through without looking up. When she finished she put the log on the table and looked at the ceiling — at the underside of the building above, the poured concrete of the floor, the sound of the climate system.

Dez listened. When Kirin finished, Dez was quiet for a moment.

"Brigid," she said.

"My supervisor at the CapEx Board."

"She's been waiting for this."

"I don't know that."

"She held your position." Dez looked at her. "Three of three returned. She's been waiting for someone to bring something back. She just didn't tell you she was waiting."

Kirin said nothing.

"The child in the photograph," Dez said.

"Her name is Nnedi Eze." She picked up the log. She held it by its cover without opening it. "I don't know where she is."

"The archive might."

"The archive might."

Dez looked at the infrastructure report in its box. "We're not going to be able to take everything."

"No."

"So we take what she told you to take. The atlas. The infrastructure report photographed. The log."

"Yes."

"And we go south."

"Meridian first. Then Sable River."

Dez looked at the table. She looked at the empty chair across from her. She was, Kirin understood, doing the same thing Kirin was doing: adding up what she owed the corridor and what the corridor owed her and seeing if the numbers cleared.

"All right," Dez said.

* * *

Fen came back after full dark.

He found them at the reading tables, Kirin with the log open to an early entry and Dez with the infrastructure report documentation spread in front of her, working through the photographs. He came in without announcing himself and looked at what was on the tables and did not comment.

He went to a small cabinet at the base of the east wall and came back with a flat tin that he set on the counter. Inside: dense flat bread and a soft cheese, the kind of food that kept without refrigeration. He divided it without asking how much either of them wanted and set the portions on the edge of the nearest table.

He ate his own portion standing at the bay entrance. He ate the way he did everything — without wasting motion, without performing ease.

"Timing changed while you were reading," he said.

Kirin looked up.

"Seventy-two hours from now, the network wants this site cleared. Not tonight. Not tomorrow. But the window I thought I had has shortened." He paused. "There is concern — directed concern — that someone is coming."

"The Curator."

"That's the concern." He looked at the table. "You can stay and read until tomorrow midday. I can give you that and I can't give you more."

"We're taking some of it with us," Kirin said.

Fen looked at her. He was deciding something. She watched him decide it.

"You take it out of this building, you take on the risk of it," he said. "The network's protocol is that material doesn't leave the archive."

"I know."

"The protocol exists because material that leaves can be taken."

"I know."

He looked at the log on the table — at Adaeze's name on the cover in Adaeze's hand. He looked at it for a moment with the specific quality of a person who has an opinion and has decided the opinion is not his to give.

"All right," he said. "Use the time you have."

He picked up his portion from the counter and left the reading room.

Kirin looked at what was on the table.

The log. The population atlas, already earmarked. The infrastructure report, documentation photographed twice on two separate devices. Three items. Three instructions in Adaeze's last entry, written in the flat dry hand of a woman who had been keeping a watch for thirty-five years and knew, in February, that she had four weeks left.

She thought about Brigid. She thought about Brigid saying *three of three returned* in that careful voice at the Board. She thought about what Dez had said: *she's been waiting for someone to bring something back.*

She did not think, yet, about the child in the photograph whose name she now knew.

She put the log in her pack, carefully, between the two soft things she had left. She put the atlas beside it. She checked the seal on the first compartment.

Dez had folded away the documentation and was eating the cheese without looking at anything in particular.

"We leave at first light," Kirin said.

"The door opens at first light," Dez said. "So yes."

"South toward the coast."

"South. I know a route." Dez picked up her bread. "The route doesn't go through the main corridor."

"Will it keep?"

"It'll keep." She looked at Kirin. "Long enough."

The climate system held the temperature in the room. Outside the sealed building, outside the campus perimeter, the dark was full and still, the eastern corridor running toward a distance that Kirin had not yet seen and was now carrying the shape of in three physical objects in a pack.

The key was still in her jacket pocket on the back of the chair.

She picked up the jacket and put it on and put her hand in the pocket. Cold, now, the way it had been cold on the sensor-free path from Larch to Harrow Creek. The archive's systems were not the corridor's systems. The key's relationships were specific.

She had been carrying this key for two weeks and she was only beginning to understand what it had been doing the whole time. Not an artifact. Not a token. A credential for a network of obligations that had been operating since before she was born, in the spaces that the world she grew up in had not been able to see.

Her grandmother had carried it for thirty-five years.

She looked at the terminal on its stand, the early-Compact case, the screen dark now. She looked at the reading light. She looked at the two tables and four chairs in a bay in the north wall of an archive in the basement of a building that did not appear on any map.

She sat down and opened the log to page one.

She had until midday tomorrow.

She started from the beginning.
