# Episode 8: "The Cut"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin crosses into the Outside at a maritime transit facility where bureaucracy moves at its own pace, a missing carrier turns out not to be missing, and twelve letters finally say their last lines.*

---

The query came back clean.

Dara looked at the response for fifteen seconds — the timestamp, the originating node, the query status — and then she looked at the lane below.

The man from the east financial district was not there.

He had been there twice more in the four days since Kirin's visit, each time for longer than the last, each time from a slightly different position on the lane. She had put the question to her contact in the east block as she had intended, and her contact had told her: a firm called Veritas Structured, registered 2112, principals in the Meridian financial registry under two names, actual operational principals under a third name she had heard before and not in a context that made her comfortable. The firm's market activity for the last eighteen months suggested they had an auction position they were trying to close.

Four days of watching the lane. Then: the clean query response, and the man gone.

She had expected the market query to arrive within seventy-two hours of Kirin's departure, as she had told Kirin. It had arrived in thirty. The response had been clean — no new data, no movement in her subscriber account, nothing to suggest a change in the atlas's custodial status. When she had not responded within the market's standard review window, the firm had escalated: a second query, then a third, the interval between them shortening, the phrasing shifting from inquiry to what was, within the market's formal vocabulary, a demand for confirmation.

She had not responded to any of them.

And then the queries had stopped.

In her experience, this meant one of three things: the firm had found another avenue; the firm had lost confidence in its model and was recalibrating; or the firm had found what it was looking for through a different channel and no longer needed what she had.

She did not know which.

This was unusual. In eleven years of monitoring the Rootstock market, she had not been in a position of not knowing.

She looked at the atlas on the shelf. She went back to work.

* * *

The road south of Meridian ran coastal for thirty kilometers, then cut inland.

Kirin and Dez had left the coast road at the inland cut — a marker Dez knew: a concrete post with two notches at shoulder height, easy to miss if you were looking for it and invisible if you weren't. The route from the cut took them southeast through scrub and former dune land now recovered in coastal grasses, the ground firm underfoot but yielding, the texture of land close to salt water that has been given time.

The key had begun warming at the third kilometer from the coast road.

She tracked it with the passive attention she had maintained since Harrow Creek. Not watching for it, but aware — the way you were aware of a sound you were not listening to. The warmth this time was neither the cycling pulse of the managed spur nor the steady pilot-light of the campus road. Something she didn't have a frame for yet. A warmth that built rather than cycled — starting in the contact-pad face and working inward, the same progression she had noticed on the road east of the junction, but slower, more deliberate. Whatever the key was converging with was still some distance away and the distance was closing.

"It's reading something," she said.

"How far has it gotten."

"Two degrees warmer than room, building. Pads are the source." She looked at the path ahead. "Something out here speaks to it."

Dez walked without adjusting her pace. "The facility's at six kilometers from this post. Whatever it's reading is either the facility's infrastructure or the perimeter sensors."

"Different from the managed spur."

"Different system." A glance back. "The managed spur used a Compact maintenance substrate. The key was reading something it recognized from training. What's out here is something else." She paused. "Not Compact. Not Free Zone. The third kind."

The coastal grasses gave way to low scrub — windbreak planting, sixty years old, the original species surviving in diminished form. Through the scrub: a concrete structure in the middle distance, low and wide, with the salt-weathered surface of a building exposed to open-air maritime conditions for long enough that the concrete's original color was no longer accessible beneath the oxidation. On the roof: two antenna masts and a solar array at the same tilt angle as the campus installation — the same base profile, she noticed, which meant the same design generation.

Somebody had built both of those arrays, or somebody had taught the same methodology to whoever built them separately. The network had its own engineering culture. It showed in the work.

A chain-link fence ran the property's perimeter. Behind it: a container yard, the old freight racks repurposed for supply storage — not at random, in a system, each item in a position that suggested a deliberate allocation logic. Not freight management. Community management. The difference was visible in the spacing between things: freight management packed close and moved fast; what she was looking at had been arranged to be found.

A path led to the facility's main entrance — not marked, just the worn quality of a route used regularly enough to stay open in the coastal scrub. They took it.

At the entrance: a door with a handwritten placard, laminated against the salt air. *Transit Processing. Appointment or referral required. Hours: 07:00 — 19:00.*

She checked the sun through the scrub. Past noon.

She knocked.

A slide-panel opened at eye height. A woman in her forties looked through it — not at Kirin's face, but at her pack, her hands, her boots. A professional assessment, thorough and unhurried, the kind executed by someone who had done this specific thing many times and had learned to read incoming travelers the way you read a material surface: composition, wear pattern, what it had been through and how recently.

"Who referred you," the woman said.

"Naila Brace. Salt way-station, eastern corridor."

A pause that was not surprise but was something. The sound of a different gear engaging.

"And the other."

"Dez Marwan. Same referral."

The panel slid closed. A moment of sound from inside — a drawer, movement across a floor. The door opened.

The woman held it and looked at them from the other side.

"Come in. I'm Crucita. I do intake."

* * *

A Compact CapEx Board ran its verifications through the Ledger: physical audit, materials certification, registered Guild credentials. The verification was about the object or the skill, not the person presenting it. The person was the delivery mechanism. What mattered was the deliverable.

This room worked the inverse.

The desk held a log — actual bound log, not a terminal. Crucita sat behind it and opened to the current page and wrote the date and two entries that Kirin understood, after a moment, to be their names as given through the panel. Below the names: a line for the referring contact.

"Naila Brace," Crucita said, writing it. "Salt." She wrote something below the name. "Naila's been on the referral list since 2099." She looked at Kirin over the log. "That's her credential here. Not yours." A pause. "I need a witness name. Someone the network can confirm knows you and is prepared to stand for your identity."

Kirin looked at the log.

"My grandmother's name was Adaeze Okonkwo."

Crucita's hand on the pen stilled.

She set the pen down. Not at Kirin — at the pen on the log, at the line she had been writing, with the quality of someone deciding how to proceed with unexpected information.

"Okonkwo is in this log," she said. "Last entry, September 2125."

"I know."

"Are you the inheritance."

The word landed with a weight Crucita had perhaps not calculated. Kirin looked at her.

"Yes."

Crucita picked up the pen. She wrote something in the margin of the entry — not the line she had been composing. A marginal note. Private notation, the kind you added to a record when the record needed context that the standard format couldn't contain.

"I need the key," she said.

She pointed to a device mounted on the desk's left edge — a small unit bolted to the wood, weathered to the same salt-gray as the building's exterior, with a contact pad on its upper face and a cable running to a relay box on the wall behind her. Old hardware. Not Compact-standard. Not Free Zone standard. A third profile that Kirin had not seen before. The contact pad had the same four-pad configuration as the key's lower face, the spacing matching to within a millimeter.

"Contact pad on the lower face of the key," Crucita said. "Hold it flat on the reader. Don't press — just place it."

Kirin looked at her.

"I'm telling you because you'll be asked to do it again at the transit gate," Crucita said. "This is orientation."

Kirin placed the key on the reader.

For the first time since Sable River — since the Board's assay lab, where she had run it through every standard analysis protocol and gotten results that didn't match anything in the database — the key was fully active.

Not warmth. Not cycling warmth or building warmth or any of the graduated responses she had catalogued over seven weeks of travel. This was something different from all of those: a response that was not about temperature at all. The key vibrated. Not perceptibly to the hand — she could feel it in the contact pad against the reader's surface, a faint structured resonance. Hardware executing a protocol. A smartcard, as Fen had said once and she had filed and not forgotten. A smartcard doing what it was built to do.

The reader's indicator lamp went from amber to green.

Crucita looked at the relay box on the wall. A line of text had appeared in its small display.

She wrote it in the log without looking at Kirin.

"Authentication confirmed," she said. "Adaeze Okonkwo, interior witness, Compact territory, 2088 to 2126." She wrote. "Successor authentication, current bearer." She looked at the log. "The network registered your key as active two weeks ago. Record shows you were in Meridian at that time."

"I came from Meridian."

"I know where you came from." She closed the log to a bookmark. "The key's authentication log contains the last three active readings." She looked at Kirin. "Campus in sector seven. Harrow Creek junction. Here, forty seconds ago."

She stood up.

"Your companion stays in the intake area. Come with me."

Behind her: the sound of Dez's pack settling against the bench along the intake area's wall. Not a sound of resignation. A pack against a bench.

Crucita went to the door at the back of the room, and Kirin followed her through it.

---

The back room was smaller and had more paper in it than anywhere she had been since the Archive.

Not the Archive's organized accumulation — that had been the record of a specific project, filed with the logic of someone who expected the records to be used. This room had the paper of administration: forms, receipts, transit records in manila folders stacked by date, a bulletin board holding current-month intake summaries behind a layer of older summaries that had not been removed because there was no obvious reason to remove them. The oldest visible date on the board was from eleven years ago. The font was the same as the most recent.

Somebody had been using the same form for eleven years. That was its own kind of continuity.

On the south wall: a physical board, larger than the bulletin board, horizontal, running the width of the room. Not paper — names, written directly on the board's surface in a dark ink that had been added to over many years, some entries faded to gray and some still sharp, the newer ones denser because they had been written into the remaining space. Not a list. A record of witness-vouches: names in the left column, dates in the middle, notations in the right. The notations were brief and consistent in format — two or three words each, in a shorthand she did not fully know.

She scanned the board.

She found *Okonkwo A.* in the left column three times. The dates were 2098, 2109, and 2125. The notation beside the 2098 entry read: *interior/transit*. The notation beside 2109: *interior/transit, third*. The notation beside 2125: two words she could read and one she couldn't — the last word was in a script she had not seen before, four characters, written with precision.

"What does the last notation say," she said.

Crucita looked. "It's in the Outside's internal witness language," she said. "The word is roughly 'closing.' As in closing an account. Completing a term."

She said it without particular inflection. It was a notation. It had the weight of a notation.

Crucita opened a flat file box on the secondary desk. She produced a single page — a transit record, the same format as the log entries, printed on the facility's form stock.

"Adaeze Okonkwo, fourteen September 2125. Purpose of transit: cross-verification, archive documentation. Duration of stay at transit facility: seven hours outbound, two hours return. No registered interior travel." She set the page down. "She went to the archive directly from here. Came back through on the return."

"The elder-care facility in Sable River had her on shift the same week."

"The transit log shows she was here September fourteenth to the seventeenth. The elder-care record would show her on shift the nineteenth." Crucita set the page flat. "Three days between. Not comfortable, but possible. She would have known the exact margin." She paused. "She had been running this route for thirty-five years."

Kirin looked at the transit record. The facility's form, Adaeze's name in the name field, her purpose stated in six words. The record of a visit that the Sable River elder-care facility's schedule had been arranged to accommodate without appearing to. She had packed the corridor into a three-day gap between shifts and come here to close an account and come home and died of natural causes three months later, at eighty-nine years old, having never mentioned any of it.

"There's a second item," Crucita said.

She opened a different folder. A different page.

"Carrier line, Okonkwo routing, authorized entries." She read without looking up. "Four names registered since 2118." She read three that Kirin recognized: her own name, Renata's, a notation of *returned — no transit* that would be the one who turned back. The fourth: *Berin Kosta. Admitted fourteen February 2125.*

"Admitted," Kirin said.

"Crossed in. Completed transit. Registered interior."

"He's inside."

"As of his admission date, fourteen months ago. I have no record of an outbound transit." Crucita closed the folder with the flat efficiency of someone filing a resolved matter. "If he has not crossed back out, he is still inside."

Kirin was quiet for a moment.

Yusra at the Larch depot: *One carrier going east and not returning.* Said with the corridor shorthand that covered everything from defection to death, and she had been running it as the darker accounting ever since. Fourteen months since his admission. He hadn't come back because there was no reason to come back — he had made it through.

"I want to know if he can be reached," she said.

"I'll send word through the interior network. If he's close enough, word will find him." Crucita made a note on the intake form. "The name to ask for at the second way-station is Ade. She manages the interior relay."

"Thank you."

Crucita looked at her with the quality of someone who does not require thanks but accepts it as a social convention.

"One more thing," she said. "Your companion's intake record."

She opened the log again. "Dez Marwan. Referral from Naila Brace. What's her purpose of visit."

"She's not crossing in," Kirin said. "She's going back west."

Crucita wrote something. She did not look up. "Then I'll complete her transit record as outbound." She wrote. "She needs to sign the record before she leaves the facility. Have her come to the desk."

* * *

Dez was standing at the bench with her pack, reading the laminated protocols on the opposite wall — a densely printed document covering intake procedures, transit categories, and facility rules. She glanced at Kirin when she came back.

"Clear."

"Clear." Kirin paused. "Crucita needs you to sign the outbound record."

Dez moved toward the desk without question.

Kirin sat on the bench.

Outside the intake area's single window: the supply yard, well organized, the afternoon light coming in at an angle that showed the condition of the rack surfaces — clean, the metal not corroded, maintained. Not new but maintained. The distinction she had been reading in the corridor's infrastructure since she left Sable River: the difference between a system that kept things alive and a system that let them go.

She watched the yard until Dez came back.

Dez settled on the bench and opened her pack. She worked in silence for a moment — not with urgency, with the deliberate economy of someone who had reorganized their load many times and knew exactly where things needed to be. She took one item from the pack's main compartment and set it on the bench beside Kirin.

A small rigid case. Dented at one corner, the kind of denting that came from repeated use rather than from a single impact. She opened it.

Inside: a set of twelve clay discs, roughly the size of a thumb, each stamped on one face with a glyph. The clay was fired, not raw — not dusty, not fragile. The stamps were clean and deep. Each disc was labeled on its back face in small penciled notation.

"Outside route markers," Dez said. "First seventy miles from the transit gate. Each disc corresponds to a junction glyph on the network's primary corridor map. I made them from a copy of the notation system — got it from Fen, second visit to the campus, two years ago." She looked at Kirin. "I don't know your specific route once you're inside. But the junction posts will be marked, and the discs will read them."

Kirin looked at the case. At the twelve clay discs, each made by hand, each useful.

"I'll get them back to you when I come back," she said.

"Don't." Dez closed the case and put it beside Kirin's pack. "They're clay. I made twelve sets."

Kirin looked at her.

"You made twelve sets," she said.

"I had a lot of evenings in the corridor." A brief silence in which the corner of Dez's mouth moved in a way that was not a smile and was not nothing. "The infrastructure contact — I'm going back through the corridor. There are three points between here and Brigid Aelfgar where the finding needs to get placed before the failure window closes. I know the first two. The third one I'll build when I get there."

"Eighteen months."

"At the short end. I intend to be done in nine." She looked at Kirin with the direct measuring quality she had used since Salt — not the assessment-pass that was her resting mode but the more focused look she had used twice before in specific moments: once at the cistern, once in Meridian at the ring road café. The look that meant she had run the account and was stating the result. "You did this right," she said. Not warmly. Accurately. "The whole route. You did it right."

She picked up her pack.

She did not look back.

Kirin sat with the clay disc case in her hands and listened to the sound of Dez's footsteps on the facility's poured floor, and then the sound of the outer door, and then the sound of nothing.

* * *

The transit waiting room was behind the main building, accessed through a corridor. Six chairs. A water dispenser. A window giving onto the supply yard from the rear angle. A bulletin board with outdated route notices and, pinned in the corner, a hand-drawn map of the facility's surrounding kilometer — property fence, perimeter road, access paths, scaled roughly, enough to locate yourself if you needed to.

Two other travelers in the room. One asleep in the chair closest to the wall, pack at his feet, with the pressed-into-themselves stillness of someone who had been in transit long enough to sleep wherever they were. The other: a young man, early twenties, in the chair adjacent to the window, with a pack that was definitely Outside-made — different hardware, a different strap arrangement, the whole construction reflecting a manufacturing approach that did not match either Compact or Free Zone standard. He had the relaxed alert posture of a person comfortable in their body, not tense, not performing relaxation.

He was sorting something in his pack with careful deliberation. Counting items. Reorganizing.

He noticed Kirin take a chair and gave her a nod — not warm, not suspicious. Transactional. Two travelers sharing a waiting room. He went back to his pack.

After a minute he said, without looking up: "Where are you coming from."

"The Free Zones. Before that, the corridor."

He did not look up. "Before that."

"A city called Sable River. Inland."

He looked up then. Not at her face — at her boots. Then her face. "Compact city." Not a question. A filing. "They still do the long-fermentation bread there? Someone told me they use a specific regional grain and a two-day rise process. I've been trying to find out if it's different from the standard wheat-rise or just a different approach to the same outcome."

Kirin had no idea what bread he was referring to. "I'm not sure I'd know the difference."

He nodded as if this was precisely the problem. "Nobody knows until they've had both. That's the gap." He resumed his sorting. "I'm going to find out."

He said this with the matter-of-fact certainty of someone who has made a logistical decision and is executing it.

"You're going west," she said.

"First time." He straightened a strap. "Logistics work. A quarter of our water treatment supply routes originate in Free Zone processing facilities. I've been working the routes for three years and never been to the origin. Better to go once." He paused. "The bread thing is separate. Personal interest."

She looked at his pack. At the hardware — brass buckles in a configuration she had not seen before, the strap anchors riveted rather than welded, the main compartment's closure using a fold-and-tuck rather than a buckle. Every choice was functional. Every choice was also specific to a manufacturing tradition she did not know.

"Your name," she said.

"Marek." He did not ask hers.

She looked at his pack again. At the key in her jacket pocket. At the shape the key made in the fabric.

"The key on you," Marek said. He was looking at her jacket pocket. "That's a Rootstock unit."

She looked at him.

"I know what those look like because Nnedi Eze had one." He said it without particular significance, the way a person mentions a mundane biographical fact. "Different form factor — older, I think, the casework is different. But the contact-pad arrangement is the same. She showed it to us when we were young." He paused. "She said it was a key to something. We thought she meant it literally." The corner of his mouth moved. "We were young."

Then he looked at her face with a quality that was different from his logistics assessment — more specific, quicker. "You're going to the plateau settlement."

"Eventually."

He nodded. He did not ask more. He put the item he had been working on back into the pack and closed it and leaned back against the chair and looked at the supply yard through the window with the patience of someone accustomed to waiting for things that arrived on their own schedule.

Kirin looked at the window.

Nnedi Eze. The child in the photograph — identified in Adaeze's log, named as the third instruction: *find Nnedi Eze — she is not a child anymore.* She was the founder of the plateau settlement. She was somewhere on the other side of the transit gate. And she had a key.

Dara's accounting: two confirmed destroyed, one unaccounted for, two in Outside custody, Kirin's key in Compact hands. Six original keys from the Rootstock Protocol registration. Six total.

If Nnedi's key was one of those six — one of the two "in Outside custody" that Dara had named — then the Outside's custody arrangement included Nnedi specifically. Or it was the "unaccounted for" key, which meant Dara's network had not found it because Nnedi had been holding it quietly, in a plateau settlement that did not appear on anyone's map.

Or the Rootstock Protocol had issued more than six original keys, and Dara's accounting had a floor she had not told Kirin about.

She did not have enough information to answer. She filed the question.

She looked at the supply yard, and the afternoon light moving through it, and waited.

* * *

He came from the north approach road and did not go to the main entrance.

He parked the vehicle — registered to a Free Zone freight operator, indistinguishable from the several hundred other vehicles of its type on the coastal routes — at the service access turnaround, the closest approach the road permitted without going through the facility's entrance gate. He locked it. He walked the perimeter road north, past the fence line, to the maintenance access used by Free Zone utility operators under a standing arrangement for the perimeter systems.

At the maintenance access post: a log panel. He input a query code. The panel returned a record without ceremony: *last transit, 11:22 this date. Two subjects, Compact-origin identifiers, referral via Salt.* He looked at the timestamp.

Eleven twenty-two.

He checked his watch. Fourteen thirty-five.

Three hours and thirteen minutes. He was in the outer corridor at nine-fifteen. He had been three hours behind her at the point he was closest.

He looked at the facility's roof. The antenna masts. The sealed quality of a building that processed transit events and kept its own counsel.

He took out his comm and dialed a single contact. Two rings. A voice — unhurried, indoor, not tactical in its register. The voice of someone accustomed to receiving reports from people in the field and processing them without urgency, because urgency was a response to surprise, and this was not a surprise.

"She's crossed," the operative said. "Transit confirmed at eleven twenty-two."

A silence. Not surprised. Assessing.

"Timeline."

"Nine-fifteen, outer corridor. She was through before I reached the perimeter."

A pause of eight seconds. He counted.

"The key is inside."

"Yes."

Eight more seconds.

"It's a different problem now," the voice said. "File the report. Come back."

He looked at the facility. He thought — briefly, practically — about what it would take to get an operative inside the Outside's transit network. The access cost. The time. The exposure. The Outside's administrative seriousness meant their records were real records, not theatrical ones, and their intake officers had been doing this for thirty years without a successful penetration by anyone who wanted one.

"Understood," he said.

He walked back to the vehicle.

At the road junction he turned west without stopping. The problem was a different problem now, which meant it required a different approach, which meant he was not the approach anymore. He drove at regulation speed, held the road, and let the facility fall behind him and then out of sight.


* * *

Late afternoon. The transit waiting room had emptied and refilled twice — travelers processed out, others arriving, a steady rotation of people moving in each direction through the facility's gate. Kirin was still in the same chair. She had the log open on her lap to 2124, reading entries she had been approaching since the archive with a specific kind of reluctance.

The 2124 entries were thinner than the years around them. Operational notes, a route adjustment, a contact maintenance. Adaeze at seventy-three. The handwriting had changed across the log in ways Kirin had been tracking without meaning to: firmer in the middle decades, more deliberate in the recent ones, the letters formed with the care of someone who is not slowing down but is managing the distance more precisely. Not deteriorating. Adjusting. There was a difference.

She set the log down and took out the second comm.

It was in a sealed case in her outer pack pocket — sealed since Sable River. The second of the two units Amira had pressed into her hands at the corner of the agricultural district, with a solar charge cell and a piece of paper with coordinates. She had used the first at the agricultural cistern, six weeks and six hundred miles ago. The second she had been carrying with the half-attention of something she was waiting for the right moment to open, without being sure when the right moment would be.

She keyed the coordinates. Held it pointing south at arm's length. The indicator light blinked searching for thirty seconds, then for forty. Distance was longer now. Signal path doing more work. At fifty seconds: green.

A woman's voice. Not the careful man she had used before.

"Sable River relay." A pause. "Is this Kirin Saleh."

"Yes."

"Confirm the item."

"Four raised squares. Back face."

"Received." Ambient sound behind the connection — a kitchen or a workshop, something operating at low register. "Perun sends twelve."

Kirin stopped.

"Twelve," she said.

"He says twelve of twelve. He says they're all formal. Administrative correspondence all the way through — no personal address until the last letter, and in the last letter only one personal line." A pause. "He says: tell her the appointment was made in the old style. The language for it is specific. It is not a role assigned to a person. It is a role assigned to a key. The person who holds the key holds the appointment. There is no mechanism in the text for revoking it." Another pause. "He says he checked the language three times."

Kirin was very still.

"He says the last letter's final paragraph addresses the first bearer by name. Not the successor — the first bearer, the one who received the key at the beginning. It says: the key will find its own way. Your task is only to hold it long enough for that to happen." The woman paused. "He says the last line is: be patient with what it asks of you."

The last words settled like something returning to the place it had started from.

"Tell him I received all twelve," Kirin said. Her voice was even. "Tell him — " she stopped. "Tell him he did a good thing."

"He says you're welcome." Brief silence. "He says he hopes the road has water at the end of it."

The connection closed.

She held the comm in her lap.

* * *

The key was not an object that conferred authority on the person who held it.

The key was an appointment. The appointment was the key. The person who held it inherited not a thing but a function, the way an office worked — the desk was always the desk, regardless of who sat behind it. Adaeze Okonkwo had not been chosen because she was Adaeze Okonkwo. She had been chosen because she was holding the key when the choice was made, and the choice was not a committee assessment or a skills review. The key had been placed in someone's hands at the beginning, and it had moved as hands changed, and the appointment moved with it, and there was no mechanism in the text for revoking it.

Be patient with what it asks of you.

Said first in a letter from 2088 to a woman named Adaeze Okonkwo, forty-three years before Kirin was given the box. Said last in Adaeze's handwriting, on the back face of the note inside the box, the words she had read three weeks after her grandmother's death in a third-floor apartment with herb pots on the window ledge and a key she could not identify and no context for anything she was looking at.

She had not known, when she accepted Dara's terms in Meridian, that the hold came with a title. She had not known, when she requested leave from the CapEx Board, or when she walked east out of Kesmark, or when she turned the pages of the log at the reading table in the archive's basement. She had been making decisions inside an appointment she did not know she held, and she had, evidently, been making them correctly, because here she was.

She looked at the supply yard through the window.

A woman crossing the yard with a load of equipment in a frame — walking efficiently, not hurrying, the weight distributed correctly. She set the frame down at the storage rack and began unloading it with the practiced speed of someone who did this regularly. She did not look at the building's window. She had work to do.

Kirin watched her for a moment.

She was also, she noticed, furious. Not at anyone. Not at the key or the network or the text of a letter written forty-two years ago by someone who had not known who would eventually be reading it. Furious at the fact of it — at the fact that her grandmother had built her quiet life so thoroughly around this work that her death had come and gone before Kirin knew any of it. At the fact that Adaeze had been patient with what the key asked of her and had not asked Kirin whether Kirin wanted to be asked.

She was also going to do it anyway. That was the other fact, and it had been the other fact since Sable River, and there was no point being surprised by it now.

She put the comm back in her pack.

She picked up the log and found her place and kept reading.

* * *

Crucita came for her at a quarter past four.

She had a clipboard — spring-loaded, the kind Kirin had not seen in active use since her first year at the Board, before the migration to terminals was complete. Two pages clipped to it: a printed transit record and a handwritten second page.

"Interior registration," Crucita said. She handed the printed page to Kirin. "This goes in your pack. Show it at intake at any of the network's registered way-stations. It confirms your authorization date and the key's authentication record." She waited while Kirin looked at it. Her name. The date. Adaeze's name in the reference field. At the bottom: the authentication record in full, timestamped, with the three prior active readings listed above the facility's reading.

A document she had not thought to need.

"Second page," Crucita said. She held it out. "My notes."

Three items, written in the same careful shorthand as the log.

*Route from transit gate: north along coast, four kilometers. Then east on maintained track. No junction markers for first seven kilometers — first settlement uses its own system. At seven kilometers: junction post, Outside glyph notation. Discs will read it.*

*Second way-station: ask for Ade. Interior relay contact. Message sent regarding Berin Kosta.*

The third item: *Outside internal census, current date: 7.1 million. Dara's atlas figure is 6.2. Census completed June 2125. Three months before Adaeze's last visit. She did not have the current number.*

Kirin read that line twice.

Six point two million in the atlas she had carried from the archive to Meridian and placed on Dara's desk. The number that established the EQU-convertibility play's viability — the number that made the Compact's review board see a constituency instead of a rounding error. The number that Dara had been waiting for partial sections of across ten years, because without a large enough figure the claim was unfillable.

And the actual number was seven point one.

Not dramatically different in scale. But different. The atlas Kirin had delivered was the most complete single document Dara had held, the complete assembly of Adaeze's ten-year partial survey — and it was nine hundred thousand short.

"The census is internal," Crucita said. "It doesn't leave the network in a format Dara would have access to. Your grandmother's survey was the only external-facing documentation that reached Meridian." She looked at Kirin. "The atlas is still the best document in existence for that purpose. I'm telling you so you know the floor."

Kirin put both pages in her pack.

"One more item," Crucita said.

She reached into the clipboard's back pocket and took out a small envelope. Sealed. Small. The paper aged — not recent.

"Adaeze left this in her September 2125 transit record. Notation in the ledger: give to the next bearer at first crossing." She held it out. "I don't know what it says."

Kirin took it.

The envelope was light. Sealed with a paper tongue in the flap, the way Adaeze had sealed everything: minimal, functional. Nothing spent on style that wasn't doing something.

She put it in her jacket pocket. Not with the key — the other pocket. She was aware of wanting to keep them separate until she knew what the letter said, which was not rational but was the feeling she had.

"Thank you," she said.

Crucita looked at her for a moment.

"The gate is on the east side of the yard," she said. "There's no specific time. When you're ready."

She did not say good luck. She went back to her desk.

* * *

The transit gate was not much to look at.

A section of the perimeter fence — chain-link, salt-oxidized, with a pole-and-hinge arrangement that the Outside's supply crews had been swinging open and shut for decades. The hinges were well-maintained; the chain-link was less so, the coating oxidized but not through to the wire. A lock panel on the gate post. Another Rootstock reader mounted below it: same design as the desk unit, same contact-pad configuration, hardwired to the facility's relay system with a cable that ran along the fence post in a surface-mount conduit.

Old hardware. Built when the Compact was still being formalized, by people who were deciding which protocols they trusted.

She put her hand in her jacket pocket. Not the key — she left it there and felt the envelope instead. The paper, the sealed edge, the slight thickness of however many pages were inside. She took her hand out.

She placed the key on the reader.

The vibration. The protocol executing. The amber lamp on the reader going from amber to green.

The gate swung open.

Not a motor — the hinges in balance and a light coast wind doing the rest. It moved through its arc without resistance and rested open, neither pulling shut nor needing to be stopped. The way a door moved when it had been adjusted to expect use.

She stepped through.

On the other side: the same coast grass. The same wind coming off the water, cold and direct, carrying salt and the smell of distance. The perimeter fence behind her. The facility behind the fence — the antenna masts, the solar array, the salt-weathered concrete, the transit log with her name in it. Behind that: the yard with its organized racks. Behind that: the intake area, and the waiting room, and Marek going west for the first time with his questions about bread. Behind all of that: the transit road north, and the coastal approach, and the path worn through the scrub, and the six kilometers south from the inland cut, and Dez walking west toward her contacts and the infrastructure work and the thing that needed to be done before the failure window closed.

She stood on the other side of the gate.

The key in her pocket was not dormant.

Not cycling warmth or building warmth or any of the readings she had catalogued across seven weeks of travel. Not the vibration of authentication, which had stopped when she stepped away from the reader. Something she did not have a word for yet — not warmth exactly, though the key was warm. A quality it had not had before she stepped through the gate. A settled quality. Not a signal from outside. Something internal to the key, the way a thing felt when it was in the condition it had been designed to be in.

She had been carrying it dormant since Sable River. Since before she was born, it had been dormant — passing through Compact territory, waiting for a language it could speak.

She stood on the outside of the fence and held the key through the pocket fabric and felt it.

Then she adjusted her pack. She walked north along the coast.

* * *

The track north from the gate ran four kilometers along the coast before angling inland, as Crucita had said.

She walked it in the late afternoon light. The coast on her right, below the scrub line — the sound of it, the smell of it, the specific quality of salt air that was different in each direction you came at it from, and this direction was new. The facility getting smaller behind her. Then a rise, and the facility behind the rise, and then gone.

At the second kilometer: a bench.

Not placed by the facility, not in range of the perimeter cameras. Someone had brought a concrete block from somewhere and positioned it at the track's edge facing the water, and someone else had come later and added a smaller block at a good angle for a back, and the result was a bench in the specific way that anything stabilized by multiple people over time becomes a bench. The concrete was worn smooth at the sitting surface. Initials carved into the lower block in several scripts, several depths, years of people passing through and stopping here and marking it.

She did not stop. She noted it. She kept going.

She was five days on foot from the first settlement. In her pack: the log, the transit record, the clay disc case, the infrastructure photographs, the envelope from Adaeze she had not opened and would not open tonight. In her pocket: the key, warm and settled and speaking some language she was only beginning to learn. Behind her: the Compact, and Sable River, and a CapEx Board where Brigid Aelfgar was waiting for a report that Kirin was going to need to deliver before eighteen months were out. The infrastructure failure. The EQU-convertibility play, with Dara holding the atlas against the market and Veritas Structured moving to a different avenue and the watcher gone from the dyers' lane. Tomas, who had known about the corridor since 2118 and would not know yet what had become of his oldest friend.

Everything she was walking away from and would have to come back to.

At the four-kilometer mark: the track turned inland, east, as Crucita had noted. The sound of the water changed — from the direct coast sound to the filtered sound of water heard through intervening ground. Then it diminished. Then it became the baseline she was moving away from.

She walked through flat coastal scrub as the light continued to fall. The scrub transitioned — short coastal species giving way to taller shrub as the elevation changed and the salt gradient shifted. A different world than the corridor. The corridor had a road: a managed line, a curated spur, a known route with known actors and a maintenance record she could read. This was different. This was ground that asked different questions.

At the six-kilometer mark, in the last of the usable light: a wooden post. Plain. Eye height. A glyph stamped into the face of it — a specific mark, precise, from the Outside's primary junction notation system.

She opened the clay disc case. She moved through the twelve discs, reading the back-face labels in Dez's small penciled script. She found the match on the sixth disc. She read the direction from the disc's back face.

She oriented herself.

She took the direction.

The land ahead was quiet in the specific way of unpeopled ground — not silent, not empty, but quiet in the way of a place that does its living at its own pace and does not adjust for observers. Wind in the scrub. An animal moving somewhere in the middle distance, unhurried. The sky overhead had gone from gray to the first deep charcoal of approaching dark, the stars coming through at the zenith first, then spreading.

She walked until she could not comfortably read the track.

Then she made a dry camp at the base of a long windbreak hedge — old growth, dense, the interior of the planting cavity dry and sheltered on three sides. She set the portable heater between her pack and the hedge. She ate from her provisions.

She took out the envelope.

She held it in the heater's minimal warmth and looked at it.

She put it back in her jacket pocket.

She would read it when she reached the second way-station. When she knew the route had water at the end of it. When she had covered enough of the ground between here and what her grandmother had been trying to tell her that she could receive the telling the way it had been meant.

She lay back.

The stars overhead were the same stars she had seen from the corridor, from the transitional zone, from the campsite east of Meridian. The same stars as Sable River, where she had stopped noticing them because there had been no reason to look up. They did not change. They were the one piece of the whole route that had not needed to be read or assessed or carried — they were just there, doing what they did, patient with what they asked of nothing.

She listened to the wind in the hedge.

After a while, she slept.
