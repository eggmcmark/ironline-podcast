# Episode 7: "Meridian"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin delivers the population atlas to Dara in Meridian's Trade District and discovers what Adaeze's project was actually designed to prevent.*

---

The alert came in at seven-eighteen.

Dara closed it without answering and got up from the desk. She went to the window.

The dyers' lane below was still dark, the first shift not yet in. She stood at the window and drank her tea and looked at the empty lane. She liked this hour specifically — before the vats ran, the street smelled like stone and morning damp, which was what it actually was. After the first shift arrived, it smelled like the work the street did. This was fine. It was the purpose of the lane. She had been living above it for nine years and had long since stopped noticing the smell the way you stopped noticing city noise: not by liking it but by absorbing it until it became the baseline against which other smells were measured.

The query had come through the Meridian Equity Research Network. A clearinghouse for pre-Compact financial instruments — one of six in the city, the one with the best granular data on Rootstock Protocol authentication records. She had been a subscriber for eleven years. In eleven years she had received two genuine alerts on Rootstock Protocol queries. The rest had been her own queries, testing whether the system would catch what it was supposed to catch.

This was the third genuine alert in two months.

She set her cup on the sill and went back to the desk. She looked at the query's metadata — not the full content, which cost separately, but the timestamp and the originating node. East financial district. A registered address in the market block where a specific kind of firm operated: the kind that described its activity as one thing and conducted another.

She had a contact in that block. She would put a question to him in the afternoon, at a transit café, in the way they asked each other questions — obliquely, over bad coffee, in sentences that contained the thing without stating it.

She noted the timestamp. She filed the alert.

Below the window, a figure arrived at the workshop entrance — the first weight of footsteps on the lane's stone — and unlocked the door and went in. The first shift. The lane would smell like itself for another twenty minutes.

She sat back down at her desk.

She had been waiting seven months. One more morning was not a problem. But the queries were. Two of the same query from a single originating node, in sixty days, meant a project. Someone was building toward something, and they were building with urgency.

She went back to work.

* * *

Kirin had been awake since five.

The room above the workshop was five square meters — a bench, a window painted shut through several historical layers, the seams sealed with the permanence of a thing done once with no plans to undo it. The city came through the wall. She lay in the early dark and listened to it — the continuous particular baseline of a city in its nighttime register, more present than Sable River's residential murmur and less formed than the corridor's silence. A different kind of noise that she was still learning.

She had the log open on the bench when the sky in the gap above the buildings across the back lane turned from black to gray.

The entries she was reading were from 2108. Adaeze on a corridor rotation, adjusting her timing around the Curator's patrol schedule. The patrol schedule shifted quarterly — not unpredictably, on a cycle, with the regularity of an administrative process rather than a field operation. Adaeze had been tracking the shift for years and logging when it happened and what the new timing was, without comment. The Curator ran an office. Offices had rhythms. She had learned the rhythm and moved accordingly.

Kirin read through three entries. She closed the log and sat on the edge of the bench and looked at the painted-shut window.

The log had a shape she was still working out. In the early years — 2091, 2092, 2093 — the entries were brief and operational: transit routes, contact confirmations, cover maintenance. By 2100 they had expanded. Not into reflection. Into more observation. Adaeze had started noticing the texture of things she passed through rather than just their logistics. The herbs on the window ledge, catalogued as a practice. A conversation at the Commons clinic where she worked afternoons. The quality of September light in the corridor east of the Larch junction, noted once and not repeated. These entries were not lyric. They were still structured as observations, still in the flat reportorial voice. But they contained the surplus detail of someone who had stopped having to remind herself to pay attention.

She had not yet found Brigid's name.

She packed the log and went downstairs.

* * *

The lodging manager was at his counter reading a printed page with both hands, the specific focused attention of a man who had made deliberate choices about his information consumption and saw no reason to revisit them. He did not look up.

Dez was at the corner where the back lane met the first cross-street, her pack adjusted for a short transit, her weight on her heels.

"My contact can see me this morning," she said. "Café on the north ring road — open counter, transit post adjacent. Early afternoon."

"If you're late."

"Stay put. Late here is not the same as late in the corridor." Dez adjusted her shoulder strap. "In the corridor, late means something is wrong. In a city, late means the conversation ran long or the transit was slow or my contact decided he needed to think before talking. It means something. It doesn't mean the same thing."

"And if it means something is wrong."

"You'll be able to tell the difference." She looked at Kirin. "Don't walk the dyers' building twice before you go in. Once. You know where it is."

"I know where it is."

She left. Kirin watched her for a moment — the way Dez moved through a street, reading it, filing what she found, keeping the assessment internal. She moved like someone who had made a decision long ago about how much of her process to show.

Kirin went the other direction.

* * *

The Trade District in the morning operated at a register the evening hadn't shown her.

The ground-floor storefronts were open — not the quiet open of a shop waiting for customers but the active open of places mid-work. A metalsmith's at the first corner with the shutters fully retracted, the counter crowded with pieces in various states of completion and sale. A food counter two doors down with a line of five people that moved fast because the counter operator had developed choreography over years of doing this at speed every morning. The smell of the food mixed with the hot-metal smell and with the exhaust from a vehicle making a delivery to the third building down.

She walked through it. She read what was useful and filed what wasn't.

The dyers' lane was four blocks east and one block south. She smelled it before she turned the corner — the sharp vegetal smell from the previous evening, more concentrated now, the morning shift running and the vats at temperature. The steam from the ventilation stack on the right-side building caught the morning light and made it visible: a pale column rising into the narrow air between the buildings.

She walked the lane once.

The building on the right had its ground floor open: lengths of dyed material hanging in the interior in the dense vertical display of a working operation, the colors vivid in the way working colors were vivid when the work was recent. A woman at the counter sorted bolt ends with the focused economy of someone accounting for material before the rest of the day could begin. She glanced at Kirin as she passed — not at her face. At her pack. At what she was carrying and roughly what it weighed. A professional reflex lasting half a second.

On the left side of the lane: a separate entrance. Not the shop door. A door with a hand-lettered label at eye height: *2F — by appointment.* The label was old. Someone had renewed it at some point, the newer ink slightly different in color from the aged original, both faded now to the same register.

She had no appointment. She went up.

* * *

The staircase was steep, the railing iron and cold. At the top: a landing, a door, a brass number plate. She knocked.

A pause that was not surprise. A considered pause. Someone deciding.

The door opened.

The woman who opened it was in her mid-forties, in clothes that were functional rather than casual — the particular working register of someone who had stopped distinguishing between professional and personal because the distinction had no use for her. She was compact, precise in her movements, with a stillness that came from a decision long ago about what was worth moving for and what wasn't. Reading glasses pushed up on her head. Eyes that had already taken a measure of Kirin before Kirin reached the top step.

She looked at Kirin's face.

Not the pack, not the jacket, not the shape of what was in it. Her face. The look had the quality of note-taking — recording, not evaluating.

She stepped back.

"Come in."

Her voice was level. Not flat — controlled. She did not say welcome. She said come in, which was an instruction, and Kirin came in.

The room was larger than the staircase suggested. A desk at the east wall with two screens, a corkboard above it dense with handwritten index references in at least three scripts, accumulated over time rather than organized at once — the older references faded and overlapping, the newer ones cleaner, the whole thing a palimpsest of years of inquiry. Shelves on three walls: printed reference materials in a consistent format, labeled document boxes with date-range notations, a row of sealed cases in a dark composite material she did not recognize. A clear space in the room's center — two chairs, a low table between them. The south window looked down onto the dyers' operation: the lengths of material visible from above, the steam column visible from here as it left the stack and rose past the window.

The woman sat behind the desk. She did not offer Kirin a chair. She sat and looked at her from across the room.

"Who sent you."

"My grandmother. Adaeze Okonkwo."

The pause was three full seconds. Not surprise — the specific non-surprise of someone absorbing news they had been preparing for and adjusting their operating state accordingly. She sat back, a very small movement.

"Sit down," she said.

Kirin sat. She put the atlas on the table.

"Dara," the woman said. Confirming that Kirin already knew.

"Yes," Kirin said.

Dara looked at the atlas on the table. She did not reach for it immediately. She looked at it the way you look at something you have been waiting for when it arrives and you are deciding whether to let yourself reach for it yet.

Then she picked it up, opened it, and began to read.

* * *

She did not read from the beginning.

She went directly to the regional population tables in the eastern sectors — the pages Kirin had spent two hours with in the archive's reading room. She cross-referenced two pages simultaneously with the ease of someone who had built a model of what this document should contain and was checking the model against what was actually here. She turned to the methodology section and read three paragraphs. She went to the aggregated totals on the final page and held there, the page flat under her hand.

Her face did not change. She read the way a person reads when confirmation brings neither relief nor satisfaction — only the closing of an open question.

She closed the atlas.

She set it on the desk and looked at Kirin.

"She's dead," she said.

Not a question.

"November," Kirin said. "Three weeks before I found the box."

"November." Dara removed her reading glasses from her head and set them precisely on the desk. "She was supposed to bring this herself. Last autumn — I had a specific window." She looked at the atlas. "When she didn't come, I put a watch on the corridors and waited." A pause. "Seven months."

"She sent a letter in Tamsat. I couldn't read it when she died."

"Can you now."

"Six of twelve letters rendered. Enough to know the shape."

Dara looked at her steadily. The note-taking quality again, more focused now. Recalibrating.

"How much of the log have you read," she said.

"Twelve years in," Kirin said. "I stopped at 2104 in the reading room and read more on the road. I'm into 2108."

Dara looked at her for a moment.

"Then you know what she was doing here."

"I know she kept a log and the log is operational. I know she appointed herself responsible for a set of tasks and carried them out for thirty-five years. I know she was tracking water management components in the corridor. I know she came to Meridian at least once — the log references a delivery to a Meridian contact, third quarter of partial atlas, 2113." She paused. "I'm inferring the rest."

"She came three times," Dara said. "2112, 2118, and 2122. Each time she brought a section of the population survey — regional, eastern sectors, compiled from the archive's records. Nothing complete. She was cautious about what she brought through in a single transit." She looked at the atlas on the desk. "Each section was keyed to a reference format that told me how it fit with the others. I built the file from pieces over ten years." She paused. "The atlas you brought is the complete document. All the pieces assembled. She must have done this at the archive — the last visit, September 2125." She was quiet for a moment. "She was in worse health than I'd realized. If she assembled the full atlas on that trip, she knew she wasn't making another one."

Kirin held still.

"The log entry for September 2125," she said. "She was checking whether the Curator was still on his route. The entry says going home was not the choice that week. She went to the archive instead."

Dara nodded, very slightly.

"She was closing accounts," she said. It was not a sentimental observation. It was a project management observation, and it landed the way those did.

Kirin looked at the atlas on the desk. At the cover in its scuffed protective sleeve, the handwritten notation in the lower corner in Adaeze's hand — the archive reference code that had told Kirin which section of the shelf to look in and had told Dara, she now understood, something about the document's provenance.

"The alert this morning," Kirin said. "The Rootstock Protocol query."

Dara was quiet for a moment, and in that moment Kirin could see her recalculating — deciding how much to confirm, and that she had decided yes.

"Third this season," Dara said. "Coming from an address in the east financial district. A firm I know by reputation, not by direct contact." She set her glasses on the desk. "They want to know which authentication keys are still active. What the Rootstock Protocol is compatible with in current infrastructure terms. What a working key might be made to do in a system that has evolved away from it." She looked at Kirin. "You understand what they want."

"EQU convertibility."

"Through the back door." Dara's voice was flat, without heat. "The Compact prohibits external exchange of equity units — EQU stays inside the Ledger, always. The prohibition has held for sixty years because the Ledger is the system and the system is the economy and the economy is not a fact about commerce but a fact about value, about what things are worth and why and to whom. You can't speculate what isn't on a market. You can't hollow out what isn't convertible."

"But if a pre-Compact protocol existed that the Ledger was designed to be compatible with," Kirin said.

"Then someone who held that protocol — someone with a working authentication key and the population data to demonstrate that the outside market was large enough to justify the political cost — could file a claim that the Compact would have to process. A legitimate claim, filed through a legitimate channel. The Compact would be forced to argue against its own structural architecture to deny it." Dara looked at the atlas. "The population data is what establishes the market size. A claim based on six million people looks very different to the Compact's review board than a claim based on six hundred thousand. Six million is a constituency. Six hundred thousand is a rounding error."

The atlas on the desk. The same atlas Kirin had been carrying for two weeks as an inheritance, as an instruction in Adaeze's handwriting, as the first of three concrete tasks. She looked at it.

"My grandmother knew this," she said.

"She knew it in 2112. She told me when she came the first time, and I am telling you now because you are clearly going to work it out from the log anyway and it is more efficient if I tell you." Dara's voice was not unkind. It was the voice of someone for whom efficiency was itself a form of respect. "The authentication key your grandmother held — the one she left you — is one of six original Rootstock keys from the protocol's registration. Two of the others are confirmed destroyed. One is unaccounted for, held by a party the network cannot locate. The remaining two are with the Outside's network in specific custody arrangements." She paused. "Your key is the only one in Compact-internal hands. If it moved to the EQU convertibility market, the claim becomes viable."

"The Curator," Kirin said.

"Yes." Dara looked at her without particular surprise that Kirin had reached this. "The Curator's network and the convertibility brokers are not the same entity. But they have a shared interest in the key's location. The Curator wants to know where the key is because if it moves to the convertibility market, the Curator's arrangement with the Compact's internal shadow — the agreement that keeps the empty zones' management quiet — becomes worthless. The convertibility play makes everything visible, and the Curator's value to the Compact shadow depends entirely on the quiet being maintained."

"So the Curator is hunting the key not to use it," Kirin said. "But to prevent someone else from using it."

"That is my assessment. Based on eleven years of watching the alerts and three conversations with your grandmother, who had seventeen more years of watching than I did." She looked at the desk. "Adaeze's position in Sable River served multiple purposes. Interior witness. Observer. Carrier of the key in a jurisdiction where the EQU prohibition was strongest and the convertibility play had the least traction. She was the key's anchor inside the system the key was designed to interact with. Moving it outside the system — to the corridor, to the Free Zones, to anywhere other than where it was — was the risk she was managing."

Kirin was quiet for a moment.

"And she's gone," she said.

"Yes."

"And I'm outside the system."

Dara looked at her steadily.

"You are currently outside the Compact's territorial border," she said. "That is not the same as outside the system. The system includes the corridor, the archive, this room. What the Compact calls outside is not actually outside." She paused. "But you cannot remain in transit indefinitely. And the key in motion is the key at risk."

Kirin looked at the window. Below it, the lane was fully in the morning's operation now — a second staff member visible through the dyers' ground floor, the material lengths moving as someone sorted through them. The ordinary work of a place doing what it did.

A man on the opposite side of the lane. Standing. Looking at a handheld device, making some kind of input or query. She watched him for a moment, and then he looked up — not broadly at the lane but at this window, at the second floor above the dyers. He looked for two seconds and looked back at his device.

"Dara," she said.

Dara was already moving. She came around the desk and stood at the window beside Kirin, close to the frame and not in the sight line of the lane below. She looked.

"I see him," she said.

She looked without moving for a count of ten. Then she went back to the desk.

"He's been on this lane three times in two weeks," she said. "Always different hours, never consecutive days. He's building a pattern of my routine without wanting me to notice the pattern." She sat. "He's not a problem today. He doesn't have what he needs yet."

"What does he need."

"Confirmation that the atlas came through. He can see someone entered this building. He can't see what they were carrying." She paused. "He'll query the market this afternoon. His firm will have a flag on my subscriber account. When I don't immediately sell or transmit anything new, he'll wait for the next visit." She looked at Kirin. "Which means you should not come back here."

Kirin looked at her.

"Then you take the atlas on those terms," she said.

"Yes. The atlas is safer here than in transit, and here is where it does what your grandmother brought it to do." Dara set her hands flat on the desk. "Which is not to sell it. Not to transmit it to the market. To hold it, specifically, so that the people who want it cannot confirm it exists." She paused. "The convertibility play requires the complete population data. Partial data is not enough — you cannot file a claim on a methodology that acknowledges six regions without accounting for the other twelve. Adaeze was bringing me pieces because pieces are not the document. The document is not the document until all the pieces are assembled and in someone's hands." She looked at Kirin. "Now the document is assembled and in someone's hands."

"And you hold it," Kirin said.

"I hold it for the purpose of keeping it out of the market. This is what I have been doing with the partial data for ten years." She paused. "It is not altruism. I have interests in the convertibility play not succeeding. The firms that would benefit from convertibility would benefit at the expense of firms like mine — we operate in the space between the systems, and if the systems merge, the space closes." She said this without embarrassment. "Your grandmother knew this. She chose me because she needed someone who would hold the document for reasons that did not depend on moral commitment alone."

Kirin looked at the atlas on the desk.

"She chose you because you couldn't afford to sell it," Kirin said.

"She chose me because I couldn't afford to sell it and because I was competent," Dara said. "The second part also matters." The corner of her mouth moved, very slightly, with the restrained amusement of someone who had just heard an accurate thing. "There is a price."

Kirin looked at her.

"In six months," Dara said. "A single message. From Sable River or from wherever you are by then. Confirming the key is not on the market. That is all I am asking."

It was not all she was asking. Kirin could see the structure of it clearly — one message, confirmed contact, a channel established, and the next six months after that would produce a reason for a second message and the six months after that a reason for a third. It was the same structure Adaeze had entered in 2112 when she came the first time and left with an agreement to bring a second section of the survey data. It was how networks were built between people who were too careful to build them quickly.

She had been looking at the shape of this for two weeks.

"Yes," she said.

Dara nodded once.

"Then we understand each other."

She picked up the atlas from the desk. She carried it to the shelf on the west wall, to a gap she had clearly left for it — the space was the right width, the right height. She slid it in.

"The man in the lane," Kirin said. "He'll see me leave."

"He will see someone who came in with a heavy pack and left without it leave this building." Dara looked at her over her shoulder. "He already knows the atlas exists. He doesn't know it's here, specifically. He'll make the inference in three days when his firm files a query and I don't respond to it. By that time, the decision about what to do with the inference is theirs, not mine." She turned back to the shelf. "And by that time, you should be well clear of Meridian."

She did not offer her hand. She went back to her desk and picked up her reading glasses.

"Take the ring road north," she said. "Not the lane."

Kirin stood.

"Thank you," she said.

"Go safely," Dara said. She was already reading.


* * *

Outside: the lane, the steam column, the man across the way.

She did not look at him. She turned right out of the side entrance, away from the dyers' ground floor, away from the direction he faced, and walked north toward the ring road at the pace of a person who knew where she was going. Which she did. The café was forty minutes, the route clear in her head, the city's geography settled since the night before. She had the atlas's weight gone from her pack and the pack was lighter for it but she was not lighter — the opposite of lighter. The weight of what Dara had told her had displaced the weight of what she'd been carrying, and it was in a different place.

The Compact prohibited external exchange of equity units. This was not a political position she had been raised to hold — it was the structure of how money worked, which meant it was the structure of how everything worked. EQU stayed inside the Ledger because the Ledger was the system and the system was the economy and the economy was not a fact about commerce but a fact about value, about what things were worth and why and to whom. You did not wonder whether to be inside it any more than you wondered whether to be inside gravity.

Adaeze had spent thirty-five years inside it, specifically, and the key to why she had done that was the EQU convertibility play. Not a policy disagreement. A specific threat with a specific mechanism: a working authentication key, a complete population dataset, a legal filing through a channel the Compact could not dismiss. Three things. For thirty-five years Adaeze had held one of them and made sure the others stayed separated.

She was not holding it anymore.

Kirin put her hand in her jacket pocket. The key was cold, the way it had been cold since they crossed into Meridian — no corridor nodes, no Rootstock-compatible infrastructure in the city's grid, no language spoken here that it could respond to. It was dormant in the way it had been dormant in Sable River for all of her life. Not dead. Waiting for something that spoke to it.

She took her hand out of her pocket.

* * *

The café on the north ring road was exactly what Dez had described: an open counter facing the street, stools on the pavement side, transit post adjacent with its route-schedule board updating on a cycle. She got there thirty minutes before Dez was due and ordered something hot and sat at the far end of the counter where she could see both the ring road traffic and the approach from the south.

She opened the log.

She was past the entries she'd been reading in the morning — through 2108, into 2109, then 2110. She was looking for 2112. The year Adaeze had made the first delivery to Dara.

She found it on the seventh page from where she was. A transit entry in mid-April, Salt to the southern corridor, cross into the transitional zone, arrive at an unmapped waypoint. Then: a four-line entry.

*April 14, 2112. Meridian delivery, first quarter of partial atlas, eastern sector population survey. Contact confirmed receipt. Established terms for subsequent deliveries — quarterly partial data, three years minimum, extended by mutual agreement. Contact is competent. The hold is viable.*

That was all. Four lines. No name. No description of the meeting. No observation about the city or the person she had met or what it had cost her to get there and back. Adaeze did not traffic in those observations when the observation was about an active operation.

Kirin turned forward two more pages. June 2113.

*Transit interrupted at mile forty-one by new Curator equipment. Not personal — a general installation on the relay post. Re-routed via Naila's secondary approach. Added two days to the return. The hold requires more people than there are. Find them or watch it fail.*

She held the log open to that page.

*The hold requires more people than there are. Find them or watch it fail.*

Not addressed to Kirin. Written in 2113, which was thirteen years before Kirin found the box. Written to the log's record, to no one, to the particular empty air you wrote into when you were working something through. Adaeze at forty-two, eight years into the Sable River placement, tracking the corridor's new installations, re-routing through Naila's backup path, arriving home two days late to an apartment with herb pots on the window ledge, sitting down to write what she'd observed.

She had not found more people. Or she had found the people she found — Naila, Dez, Dara, the archive's network — and had carried the hold for another thirteen years with the people available rather than the people needed. The hold was working when she died. She had built it to work with what she had.

Kirin closed the log.

She picked up her cup and looked at the ring road traffic — the vehicles at regulated speed, the transit board updating its cycle. A couple came out of the building across the road with a child between them who had opinions about the direction they were walking, opinions expressed through the medium of resistance and eventually through the medium of being carried, the child apparently unsurprised and the couple apparently experienced.

She watched them and found herself smiling, which surprised her. She had not done that in a while and had not expected to do it here.

Dez arrived twenty-three minutes later, coming from the south. She had covered distance quickly — the quality of someone who had done more walking in the last three hours than the distance suggested. She sat on the stool beside Kirin and ordered something from the counter without looking at the menu.

"How did it go," she said.

"Done," Kirin said.

Dez looked at her. Taking stock.

"Good," she said.

"And your contact."

The counter operator set down a cup. Dez drank from it without looking at it.

"There have been two load-cycle events on the eastern junction components in the last eighteen months," she said. "Sectors six through eight. The components that match the failure-mode profile in the archive's infrastructure report. No fractures yet. But the progression rate is running faster than the twelve-year-old report modeled." She set down the cup. "The twelve-year-old report gave a conservative failure window of fifteen to twenty years from its writing date. My contact's current projection is eighteen months to three years. The high-confidence number is two and a half years. The short number is eighteen months if the next winter runs heavy."

Kirin sat with this.

The twelve-year-old report in its acid-free box in the archive. The same report she was carrying as a photograph in two separate places in her pack. Addressed to the Compact's infrastructure review board, no record of a response. Adaeze tracking the component ratings since 2098, logging the decline from marginal to flagged-for-replacement across six years of entries. The Compact not responding.

"And the Compact's infrastructure board," she said.

"Still no active replacement contract for these components," Dez said. "My contact tracks the board's maintenance schedules. The eastern sectors haven't been on the work list in four years." She looked at the ring road traffic without expression. "The sectors are outside the Compact's current active mapping area. They were written off in 2090. Infrastructure that isn't mapped doesn't generate maintenance work orders."

"Six to eleven million people," Kirin said.

"Yes."

The couple with the child had reached the transit post and were now consulting the route schedule with the focused disagreement of two people who had not fully discussed the plan. The child had relocated to the ground and was examining something in the gap between the post's base and the pavement with the complete absorption of a person who has found a thing they find more interesting than adults.

"Who is your contact," Kirin said.

Dez was quiet.

"I know you have reasons," Kirin said. "I'm asking what they are."

The cup sat on the counter. Dez looked at it.

"The contact has a family," she said. "Two young children. Inside the Compact — a Keeper-class household, Compact city, eastern agricultural district. The contact left the Compact six years ago to do the work they're doing. The family stayed." She paused. "If I give you the name and something happens with it — if it moves through a channel I don't control, if the Curator's network gets it, if the Compact's internal shadow adds it to a watch list — the family gets the consequences." She looked at Kirin. "The contact's work is worth the risk the contact chose to take. The family didn't choose it."

Kirin held that.

"That's a reason," she said.

"It is the reason." Dez picked up her cup. "I understand that you just spent three hours giving up your name and your Sable River affiliation to a capability broker in the east financial district's sight line. That is not the same thing."

"I know it isn't," Kirin said.

"The terms of our alliance allow for things we don't share." Dez looked at her with the flat, direct quality she used when she was stating something she had thought through. "I've been in the corridor eight years. I have contacts I protect. You've been in the corridor two months. You have a key and a log and a set of instructions. What I have is the infrastructure of the next part of the route."

"I know," Kirin said.

"Then we're all right."

"We're all right." She looked at her cup. "The timeline."

"The timeline moved." Dez set down her cup. "Eighteen months to three years is not abstract. It is specific enough to plan around. The infrastructure report you're carrying — photographed, in your pack — needs to reach Brigid at the CapEx Board not as a historical curiosity but as a live operational finding." She paused. "Does your supervisor know what she's waiting for."

"I don't know what she knows." Kirin thought of Brigid's face at the CapEx Board — the careful voice, the held position, three of three returned. "She knows something. She's been waiting for something. Whether she knows it's the infrastructure report or whether she's waiting for something she can't fully name — I don't know." She looked at the ring road. "The log will tell me, eventually. I'm not there yet."

"How long until you find Brigid's name in the log."

"She joined the Board seven years ago. The log's well-organized. I'll find her entry in the next week of reading."

"Then read faster," Dez said. It was not unkind. It was calibrated.

* * *

They walked back through the Trade District as the afternoon light went.

The streets were busier than the morning — the particular density of a city in its afternoon register, the storefronts that had been open for work now open for customers and the distinction between the two categories blurring because in the Trade District it was always both. She walked through it without stopping. She filed what was useful.

At the lodging, the manager was no longer reading. He was doing something at the counter she couldn't identify from across the room — small focused work, his hands moving with the precision of a person who had done this specific task many times. He did not look up.

She went up to the room.

The painted-shut window let in the ambient city light, the Trade District's uncoordinated scatter of temperatures and sources coming through the glass in the diffuse way of a room with no direct view. She sat on the bench and opened the log.

She was in 2113. She read forward.

The entries from 2113 were, on balance, thinner than the years around them. There was the transit entry she had read at the café — the re-route through Naila's backup path, the two days added, and the line about the hold requiring more people. There were three more entries through the autumn of 2113, shorter, covering corridor maintenance observations and a Commons health aide schedule adjustment at the Sable River clinic. Nothing named in terms that would help her.

Then 2114 opened and the entries lengthened again.

She read forward at pace, tracking for names, for any mention of the CapEx Board or a Foundation Assessor or a person at the Sable River governance structures. She found a Board reference in 2115 — *Territorial Board is assigning a new senior assessor for the eastern agricultural review. Not relevant to current operations* — which was not Brigid. She read through 2116, 2117.

In 2118: the second Meridian delivery. Identical structure to the first entry, three and a half lines, no name, just *Contact confirmed receipt. The hold is holding.* And then, two lines below: *Tomas Bergström saw me at the transit post in Kesmark. Did not approach. He knew what I was doing there. He knows the corridor. We did not speak.*

She stopped at that.

Tomas. Who had packed her bag for the journey and told her to buy salt and whose mother had passed through the corridor in 2094. Who had a backchannel to corridor people in the way he had a backchannel to a lot of things — not because he sought it out but because he was the person people told things to, and he remembered them, and they all knew he remembered them.

*He knew what I was doing there. He knows the corridor. We did not speak.*

She read that three times and moved on.

* * *

The Trade District at night was louder than it had been the previous evening. Or she was more awake to it.

She could hear the level through the wall — not individual sounds but the collective quality of a working night, the particular sustained note of many people in close proximity managing their needs and their negotiations simultaneously. The Compact's Sable River was quieter at this hour — not because its people were less alive but because the city's infrastructure had been designed to separate the commercial from the residential, the work from the rest. Here there was less separation. The dyers' operation was three blocks east and she could not hear it but she had smelled it when she opened the window briefly and the smell had been in the room since.

Dez was asleep or was managing to seem asleep with the same efficiency she managed everything else. Her breathing was even. Her pack was against the wall in the arrangement she always used — specific weight placement, access side out.

Kirin read.

She was into 2119, looking for anything that would give her Brigid's name. She found instead a detailed entry about a Compact internal-security movement in sector forty — the Curator's agents running an unusual pattern, two-day rotation rather than the standard four, which Adaeze had noted as significant without explaining why. Then a shorter entry about the herb pots: *The south-facing ones are doing better than expected this year. The north-facing ones lost the Rootstock variety and I have not replaced it. Not sure I should. The practice should be in the corridor, not in the apartment.*

She held that entry for a moment.

Then forward. 2120, 2121. The third Meridian delivery in 2122, same structure, same terse format. *The hold is holding.* The corridor entries growing more detailed in these years — the Curator's expanding activity, the changes in faction management at the Larch depot, Naila's way-station establishing itself as the reliable fixed point in a shifting system.

She found Brigid's name in November 2122.

*Foundation Assessor Brigid Aelfgar has taken senior position at the Sable River CapEx Board. She is a Rootstock-adjacent appointment — I don't know from where, but the pattern is clear. Someone placed her the way I was placed, in a role adjacent to mine. She may be the Compact-internal contact the network has been building toward. I will not approach her. If the contact is real, she will find me.*

Kirin set the log on the bench.

She sat with that for a long time.

Brigid Aelfgar, who had held Kirin's station with the patience of someone who knew what they were waiting for. Who had said, on Kirin's first day back at the Board after the leave request was approved, with the particular careful voice: *Three of three have returned. Take what time you need.* Not *I hope you find what you're looking for* or *be careful out there* — the things a supervisor said to a grieving employee taking indefinite leave. Three of three have returned. A statement about prior outcomes, offered as comfort and now visible as something else entirely.

*If the contact is real, she will find me. — A.O., November 2122.*

She had died in November 2125, exactly three years later. Whether Brigid had found her, or found her first, the log would eventually say.

She picked the log back up.

She read until the building's sounds settled and the city's night register simplified into the deep sustained baseline. She read past midnight, past the point where she was tracking narrative and into the point where she was tracking her grandmother — not the events but the quality of the record, the way the entries had changed in the last five years before Adaeze died. They were not shorter. They were more compressed. The same amount of information in fewer words. The discipline of someone learning to economize differently, not because the discipline had changed but because something else had.

She found the February 2126 entry — the last one. She had read it in the archive, read it aloud to Dez at the reading table in the basement. She read it again now, in a lodging room in Meridian, three weeks further into what the entry had set in motion.

*The road is long, but there is water at the end of it.*

She closed the log and set it on the bench.

The key was in her jacket pocket, on the hook behind the door. She could feel it from here — or she could feel the knowledge of it, which was close enough to the same thing. Cold, dormant, waiting for something that spoke its language. It had been dormant since they crossed into Meridian's grid. It would be dormant until the right room.

Tomorrow she would need to find Dez's third lead — the missing carrier Yusra had mentioned at the Larch depot, the one who had gone east and not returned. The thread she'd been carrying since the corridor without knowing what to do with it. Yusra had said one carrier missing east. The archive's steward had said the previous steward went west without arranging a handover. Those were not necessarily the same person, but she had been running the accounts since the archive and the numbers were close enough to require following.

The hold requires more people than there are.

She was not more people. She was one person with a key and an empty pack and a log she had not finished reading and a set of terms with a capability broker who would be fielding market queries about her visit within the next seventy-two hours. She was one person who had just learned, in the specific flat voice of a dead woman's working notes, that her grandmother had spent thirty-five years doing this work with fewer people than it required and had kept the hold anyway.

She looked at the window.

The city's light came through the glass in that diffuse, sourceless way — the Trade District's uncoordinated scatter of temperatures, the ambient warmth of a place where many people lived in close proximity and generated light as a byproduct of everything they were doing. She could see the glass itself. She could see the city beyond it, imprecise, a gathered impression of light and movement. She could see both at once, depending on where she let her focus settle.

She let it settle on what was further away.

She put the log in her pack. She turned off the bench lamp. She lay back and looked at the ceiling and listened to the city doing what it did, and after a while, she slept.
