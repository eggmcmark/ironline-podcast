# Episode 10: "Adaeze's People"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin reaches the plateau settlement, meets the people Adaeze's world was built around, and discovers what her grandmother's final instruction required.*

---

The relay arrived on the fourth morning of the week's drainage survey.

Rua was in the lower drainage channel on the western slope, knees in the mud, probe in the near-saturated soil. The section had been flagging as marginal since last winter, and the winter before that, which meant either the rating methodology was incorrect or the soil improvement work from three years ago had not held. She was determining which. The distinction mattered because if the methodology was wrong she would need to re-rate fourteen similar sections across the basin's watershed, and if the work had not held she would need to understand why before repeating it.

She moved the probe thirty centimeters along the channel. Read. Thirty more. Read.

The runner found her by the sound of the probe striking stone — a particular sound, the probe hitting underlying substrate before the expected depth, which was data in itself. The runner was eighteen and had been doing this route for three years and knew enough to wait at the channel's edge rather than climbing down.

"Relay from Ade at the second way-station. Marked priority standard."

Rua looked up.

"Set it on the bank."

She took three more readings, climbed out, and read it with mud still drying on her hands.

A bearer. Okonkwo-line referral. Compact origin. In transit from the second way-station, due the western descent in two to three days depending on pace. Key authenticated at the maritime facility and again at the way-station with increased register at the second site. No urgency.

A line below Ade's hand, different writing, smaller. *Find me at Rua's if you come before I leave. — Kirin S.*

She stood on the slope with the relay folded in one hand, looking at that additional line. The runner had already gone.

She had been holding Nnedi's key for two months, one week, and four days. She kept it in the survey case's sealed inner compartment — not the council's shared storage, not her own cabinet at home, but the field case she carried every working day because the drainage survey had been ongoing since she took the infrastructure lead position six years ago and showed no signs of becoming the kind of completed project that would not need her to be on the western slope with mud on her knees. The case went with her. The key went with the case. This was a practical arrangement with an impractical premise she had not explained to the council, because Nnedi had told her not to show it until after the meeting, and Nnedi had been correct about enough things during the twenty years Rua had worked beside her that the instruction's validity extended past her ability to understand its purpose.

She folded the relay into her jacket.

Two more days on the western slope before she could complete the soil improvement assessment and update the basin drainage profile. The relay said two to three days for the bearer to reach the western descent.

She went back to the probe.

* * *

The third morning out of the second way-station, the ridge appeared.

She had been watching for it since the scrub line thinned on the previous afternoon — the gradual denudation of vegetation that happened at elevation, the way the low-growing species gave way to scattered stone and sparse grass and then the specific profile of the ridge that Pit had described. She found it to the north: a long horizontal interruption of sky, and along it two of the three named features visible from this angle. On course.

The third morning she woke in a hollow under the ridge's western face where the stone had fractured in some past century in a way that left a protected cavity. Dry. The wind from the northwest becoming sound without weight when it hit the stone's curve. Cold in the specific way of elevation: not damp cold, not wind cold, but clean cold, a temperature that had no interest in keeping her warm or letting her freeze. She dressed for neutral conditions. She ate standing. She climbed.

The first feature was a stone formation the route called the elbow — Pit's word, not a cartographic designation. The stone bent in a configuration that resembled a joint if you were the kind of person who named rock formations for body parts, and the people who had mapped this route apparently were. She found it forty minutes into the morning's climb. She took a bearing off the formation's western face to confirm the second feature's direction, because Pit had said the notch was not visible until sixty meters out and the ground between the two points was loose.

The ground between the elbow and the notch was loose.

She picked across it with the specific attention of someone who had been learning, for two months, that the consequence of a twisted ankle was not a short walk to the nearest clinic. This calculation had changed the way she moved in terrain. She no longer walked through landscape; she negotiated with it. Every step was an assessment. The assessment had become automatic.

At fifty meters, not sixty: the notch. A gap where a softer stratum had worn away through whatever winters this ridge had seen, leaving a window through the stone. Wind came through it with the sound and speed of compression — the same air volume, smaller opening, the physics of it audible. She walked into the gap and felt the temperature change, the direct wind finding her for a moment, and then she was through.

The basin.

She stopped.

Wide, protected on three sides by the plateau's eastern face and the ridge's curve — a sheltered valley below her, its floor four hundred meters down. She was on the western rim looking east and down at land in its winter-end state. Managed fields, post-harvest, the turned soil dark against the dry stalks. Across the valley, on the slope opposite, a water management installation she could trace from its head-works reservoir downhill through the distribution line to the field network below. Gravity-fed. The infrastructure choice of a community that had been here long enough to invest in something that would last.

And the structures. Stone-built, low, arranged around a central gathering space in a configuration that was not the second way-station's organic accumulation. Something with a different origin: a layout that had been thought about before it was built, the kind of settlement geometry that required either a single strong vision or a long conversation before the first foundation stone went in. She could see the logic of it from up here. The gathering space oriented for sun. Individual units distributed for morning light. Agricultural storage at the downhill edge where the road in and out served it without cross-traffic.

Someone had planned this. Forty years ago, or thereabouts. Modified since — additions that didn't fully match the original geometry, a newer building at the south end that had been placed for current needs rather than the original plan's logic — but the structure of the original idea still legible in the arrangement. The way a thing held the mark of the thinking that first made it.

She found the descent path.

The western slope had been walked for decades. She could read the generations of use: the path deepened in the soft sections by accumulated feet, stone steps set where the gradient required them by someone who had thought about the specific angle of a loaded descent. The steps had been cut at least twice — she could see the original cut and the later re-cut where the original had worn, and in the re-cut a different profile, steeper rise, less width. Whoever re-cut them was taller than whoever first cut them, or in more of a hurry, or both.

She counted the steps. Not for any reason. Counting was a way of being present to a thing without trying to translate it.

An hour and a half of controlled downhill work. At the bottom: a flat section twenty meters long, a gate in a post-and-wire fence, a woman standing at the gate. Mud on her knees, a probe in a carry loop on her field case. She looked at Kirin with the focused attention of someone who had been given a description and was calibrating whether the person who had arrived matched it.

"Bearer," the woman said.

"Yes."

"I'm Rua." She looked at Kirin's jacket pocket. "The key. I can tell the shape of it through the fabric."

"It's there."

"Come through." She opened the gate. "I have something to show you. It can wait until after you've eaten, or it can happen now."

"Now," Kirin said.

Rua latched the gate behind them and walked without explaining.

* * *

Rua's house was close to the western fence by practical necessity. When she had accepted the infrastructure lead position six years ago, the available housing had been in the western quadrant, where she spent most of her working hours, and proximity had been the only variable she had controlled in the assignment. The house reflected her work: a table that took up most of the interior, drainage profiles arranged in a system whose logic was clear without being labeled, a partial map of the basin's eastern watershed tacked to the wall in sections as she completed each one. Personal effects present but not displayed. Not a home that had been arranged for visitors.

She went to the table. Opened the survey case's sealed inner compartment.

She set the key on the table.

Different from Kirin's in every way except one. Same material — or near-same, that particular density-to-mass quality that made the card in her pocket feel heavier than its size, the same color temperature that was not quite metal and not quite anything in the Compact's materials catalog. But this one was disc-shaped. Smaller. The contact pattern on its back face was a different configuration — a radial arrangement where hers was a grid, the contact points at different positions. Same protocol family. Different implementation.

Kirin looked at it without touching it.

She had known since Marek at the transit facility that Nnedi's key existed and was a different form factor. She had been carrying the knowledge of it for two months without a picture of what it looked like. Here was the picture.

"Nnedi gave it to me three weeks before she died," Rua said. "She said: hold this until the bearer with the other key arrives. She described the form factor of your key — what to look for, how the rivet configuration would be different from this one. She told me not to bring it to the council until after the meeting." She paused. "I have been following the instruction because she gave it and I trusted her judgment. I have not understood the purpose."

"Do you want to?"

Rua looked at her. "Show me."

Kirin set her key on the table beside the disc. Both keys on the same work surface, twelve centimeters apart.

The change was immediate and quiet.

Her key cycled — the authentication warmth running through the same sequence she had felt at the transit facility's witness board, at the reading room's terminal, but different in character. Faster. More complete. The cycle ran through and the warmth settled at a register higher than it had been at the second way-station, which had been higher than the transit facility, which had been higher than the managed corridor. Not accumulating toward something. Arriving at something. The specific state of a hardware token that has reached the conditions it was calibrated for and is now operating fully.

The disc did the same thing. She could not feel the disc as she felt her key, but she could see Rua's expression change: she was watching the disc and something about it had shifted in a way she had not anticipated.

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

"Authentication cycle," Kirin said. She kept her voice level. "The protocol both keys belong to required two witnesses. One appointed from inside the Compact — the interior witness. One appointed from the founding settlement. My key holds the interior appointment. Adaeze's appointment, which passed to me when I took the key. Your key holds the founding appointment. Nnedi's." She looked at the disc. "Each key can authenticate its own appointment independently. Together, in proximity, they complete a joint handshake that neither can complete alone. The protocol was designed for two keys in the same room. The two keys have not been in the same room since 1978."

"What does the handshake do," Rua said.

Kirin was quiet for a moment.

"The Rootstock Protocol was built for a specific function," she said. "An equity claim filed with two authenticated witnesses — one interior to whatever system the claim would be processed by, one exterior, representing the people the claim was on behalf of. The claim was filed before the Compact was the system. The Compact became the system. The two keys have been on opposite sides of the Compact's boundary ever since." She looked at both keys on the table. "When the two witnesses are present together and the handshake completes, the claim moves from registered-but-dormant to active. It's ready for processing."

"Processing by whom," Rua said.

"That's the question the next several months are going to answer," Kirin said.

Rua looked at both keys on the table for a long moment.

"Thirty-five years," she said. "Your grandmother."

"Thirty-five years inside the Compact to anchor the interior appointment in a place the Compact couldn't challenge. And Nnedi, forty years here, holding the founding appointment outside the Compact's reach." She paused. "They built the conditions from opposite ends."

Rua said nothing for a moment. She looked at the disc.

"Nnedi did not tell me any of this," she said. "She told me to hold the key and wait for the bearer. She told me to bring both keys to the council when the bearer came." She looked at Kirin. "She did not tell me what would happen."

"She probably didn't know the full mechanism either," Kirin said. "She knew her piece of it."

Rua looked at the table. Then she picked up the disc and held it in her palm, and held out her other hand for Kirin's key. She set both in the survey case's inner compartment, the disc and the card side by side, and closed it.

"Eat first," she said. "The kitchen opens in an hour. The council meets tonight." She latched the compartment. "You should be rested for it."

She went out.

Kirin looked at the closed case on the table.

Both keys in the dark, together, in the same small space for the first time in forty-eight years.


* * *

The settlement was mid-morning operational when she came out of Rua's house.

People crossing the central gathering space with the purposeful efficiency of a community that had organized its mornings around collective work before individual preference. The gathering space was stone-paved, worn smooth by decades of feet, with a fire pit at its center that had gone cold this morning — still smoking, just barely, the kind of banked fire that would restart fast if the temperature dropped. The stone of the surrounding buildings was a different color from the path stone: local material, laid in a style she hadn't seen in the corridor, the joints finished in a way that was neither Compact-standard nor the improvised style she'd found in the managed sector's infrastructure.

Third kind. The Outside had been building its own way for four decades without reference to either system it was surrounded by, and the building showed it.

She found the kitchen without being directed. Smell, mostly. It was a larger room than the second way-station's — higher ceiling, longer tables, a window on the east wall that the morning sun was still using. She ate without asking what she was eating, because by now she recognized the practice of eating without demanding explanation, and because the food was warm and substantial and she had been walking for four days.

At the table: a woman in her late fifties eating with the deliberate patience of someone who had a great deal to do today and was refusing to rush this part of it. She looked at Kirin without friendliness or its opposite.

"The bearer," she said.

"Yes."

"Veda." She looked at her plate. "I knew Adaeze Okonkwo."

Kirin waited.

"She came here four times in thirty years," Veda said. "Three times she asked about the plateau's water management and the corridor junction components. Once she asked about Nnedi." She ate. "She did not ask about the settlement. She did not ask whether we were well or whether the community was stable or whether the children were learning. She asked about the things she came to assess." She looked at Kirin. "I'm not telling you she was unkind. I'm telling you what she was interested in."

"I know what she was interested in," Kirin said.

"Do you." Veda set down her utensil. "Then you know she was interested in making the Compact aware of us. In inserting a witness into the Compact's system who could carry information back and forth. Who could eventually — " she stopped. Picked up the utensil. "Who could eventually make us legible to a system that has been willfully ignoring us for thirty-six years."

"Is that not what you want," Kirin said.

"It depends on what legibility costs," Veda said. "And who controls the terms of it." She looked at her plate. "We have been building something here that does not need the Compact to validate it. Four decades. Seven hundred people, now, in this basin alone. Water management that works without a regional CapEx Board telling us what the specs should be. A community governance structure that takes longer than the Compact's mediation process and produces better outcomes than the Compact's mediation process. We did not build this by asking the Compact to see us. We built it by not needing them to."

"And you think Adaeze's work undermines that," Kirin said.

"I think inserting a witness inside the Compact's system to eventually file an equity claim through the Compact's protocol — " she stopped again. "I think that makes us subject to the Compact's process. For the first time. By choice." She looked at Kirin. "We have never been subject to the Compact's process. That has cost us things. It has also been the condition of everything we built."

Kirin ate. The food was something root-based, roasted, with a grain she didn't have a name for.

"What does Adaeze's plan cost you," she said. "Specifically."

"Specifically." Veda was quiet for a moment. "If the equity claim is filed and processed through the Compact's Rootstock Protocol mechanism, the Outside settlements become a recognized category in the Compact's Value Ledger. Not registered. Not enrolled. Recognized. As a class." She ate. "The Compact then has grounds to offer Commons access to recognized non-Compact settlements. Healthcare. Infrastructure support. Education certification." She looked at Kirin. "Some people here will take that. Some will not. The community will divide on it. We have been unified by our independence. We will not be unified by the option of dependence."

"That's a real concern," Kirin said.

"Yes." Veda picked up her cup. "Your grandmother's concern was that the eastern junction components would fail before there were enough people in the right positions to respond to it. My concern is that the response will cost us something we cannot recover." She drank. "Both concerns are correct. That's the difficulty."

She stood, collected her plate, and left without ceremony.

Kirin sat at the table.

Not a warm figure, Saren had said. The actual version was useful and occasionally irritating.

She had not expected her grandmother's ideological opponent to be this coherent.

* * *

She found the building from the photograph in the late morning, without looking for it.

She had been walking the settlement's perimeter — not to assess it, or not only to assess it; walking for the purpose of being in a place long enough for it to become three-dimensional. She went south past the storage buildings, east along the field fence, and came around a corner of the gathered structures and stopped.

She recognized it before she understood why.

A low stone building, original structure, the same corner profile as in the photograph. Fifty-nine years of weathering had darkened the stone and done things to the roofline that the original builders would not have planned for, but the proportions were the same, the window placement, the specific angle of the south-facing wall that caught winter sun in a way the builder had intended. She had looked at the photograph enough times to have the geometry of it in her head without knowing she had it there.

"The founding building," said a voice behind her.

She turned. A young woman in her late twenties, carrying two seed trays. Not stopping, exactly — slowing, to see what Kirin was looking at.

"You recognized it," the young woman said.

"From a photograph."

"My grandmother's." She set the trays down on the field gate's flat post. "I'm Eme. Nnedi's granddaughter." She said it without pause for its effect. A fact, not an introduction. "I've seen people stand there and recognize it from photographs before. It doesn't happen often."

"I've been carrying the photograph for two months," Kirin said.

Eme looked at the building.

"It's storage now," she said. "Root cellar in the lower level — the floor is below grade on the north side, runs cold in summer. The upper level is where we keep the fiber processing equipment that nobody has fixed yet because the person who knew how to fix it died and we haven't found the right skill in anyone currently on the settlement." She looked at Kirin. "It was the gathering place in the founding period. Nnedi's family built it in 2067. The settlement grew around it." She picked up the trays. "There's a spot along the east wall where the original mortar shows — the later repairs are a different mix. You can see the seam."

She went through the gate and into the field without ceremony.

Kirin stood in front of the building.

In the photograph: Adaeze at twenty-seven, radiant in the specific way of a young person who does not yet know what the next thirty years will require. And beside her, leaning against this wall, a child of seven with her arms folded over her chest and a look of patient tolerance for the act of being photographed. The building behind them: new, the mortar still pale.

She found the seam Eme had described. The original mortar was a pinkish gray, slightly rough. The repair mix was darker and smooth. A seam running from the foundation to the roofline, a meter wide in the original and two in the repaired section, recording a crack that must have been substantial. The crack had been fixed and the building had continued.

She put her hand on the original section.

Fifty-nine years of weather and use and the settlement growing up around it. Storage now. Fiber equipment that hadn't been fixed yet. A root cellar that ran cold in summer because the floor was below grade.

Her grandmother had been twenty-seven here. She had walked into the Compact sixty years ago and spent thirty-five years of it carrying a key that would eventually bring someone back to this wall.

She stood there for a while.

The key was in Rua's survey case. Both keys were. She could not feel it from here.

She went to find the gathering space.

* * *

The council met in the evening in the main building's central room — a high-ceilinged space with benches arranged in a shallow arc, a table at the open end. Six people at the table, Rua among them, with the survey case set on the floor beside her chair. Thirty-some others on the benches, plus people standing at the back and along the walls. Not a full settlement assembly — a working council session with an open gallery.

She sat in the gallery.

The council handled three items before hers. A proposal from a household in the northeast quadrant to expand their composting installation to a scale that would require a formal allocation from the common land boundary — reviewed, adjusted, approved with a modification she didn't fully follow involving water runoff calculations. A complaint from the eastern field crew about the seed stock rotation schedule, which resulted in a fifteen-minute working discussion that ended without resolution and with a note for the next session. A relay from a neighboring settlement about road maintenance on the shared approach, which Rua handled in four sentences that committed to a date and nothing else.

Then her.

Rua opened the survey case.

She set both keys on the table.

The room's attention organized itself around the objects without comment. Thirty-some people looking at a disc and a card, different form factors, the same material quality in both.

Rua described what had happened in her house that morning in the precise language of someone who had decided to be accurate rather than comprehensible. The joint handshake. The register change. The protocol condition. She did not editorialize. She gave the facts.

Then she looked at Kirin in the gallery.

"The bearer can speak to the documents," she said.

Kirin stood.

She had been speaking to rooms of unfamiliar people since Kesmark. She had been learning, in increments, that the skill was not warmth or authority — it was specificity. People trusted what was specific. Vague statements required the listener to fill in what they needed to hear. Specific statements gave the listener something to hold.

She told them about the Tamsat letters. Twelve administrative documents, formal appointment language, the key-bound structure of the appointments — the appointment travels with the key, not with the person. She told them about the joint handshake's protocol function: not an activation, but a state change, from registered-but-dormant to active. She told them about the equity claim, and the EQU-convertibility play, and what the difference was between those two things. She did not talk about Adaeze. The room did not need to know who had carried this for thirty-five years. They needed to know what it was.

It took twelve minutes.

The questions were practical. One council member — the one who had handled the road maintenance relay — asked about the processing timeline for an active Rootstock Protocol claim. She said she didn't know. He looked at the man to his left, who also said he didn't know. They wrote it down.

Veda was in the gallery. She did not ask a question. She listened with the quality of a person who had already done her speaking and was now tracking whether the public version matched the private version.

One of the standing people near the back asked whether the claim's processing required the Outside settlements to identify themselves in the Compact's system.

Kirin said: "A Rootstock Protocol active claim requires the filing party to present documentation. The documentation would include population data — the atlas assembled from this network's own census records. The settlements themselves would not be individually named in the initial filing. The claim is for a category. What becomes visible after the filing is something the settlements would have negotiated."

She did not know if this was exactly right. She said it as carefully as she could.

The question did not produce a follow-up.

Rua let the room be quiet for a moment. Then: "The bearer holds the interior appointment. The appointment is key-bound. The joint authentication is now active as of this morning." She looked at Kirin. "The community wants to ask: does the bearer intend to continue holding the appointment?"

The room was quiet.

Kirin looked at the table. At both keys on it.

"I need to think about what it means," she said. "I know what my grandmother did. I know how long she did it. I know what it cost." She looked at Rua. "I need three days."

Rua looked at the council on either side of her. The looks that passed between them had the specific quality of a community that had been doing collective decision-making for forty years and had developed a private language for it.

"Three days," Rua said. "The route west opens on the fourth morning. The snow on the eastern slope section clears by then." She picked up both keys and placed them back in the survey case. "We'll meet again on the third evening."

The council moved to the next item on the list.

Kirin went back to her borrowed room.


* * *

The first full day she spent in the settlement's rhythm.

Not by design. She woke at the same hour the settlement woke and went to the kitchen and ate what the kitchen provided and was given a work task by a person who assumed, correctly, that she would prefer to not be idle. The task was fence repair on the north field boundary: replacing posts where the winter had shifted the substrate, re-tensioning wire, a job that required no explanation and could be done in company or alone. She did it in company — two people from the north field crew, a man and a woman in their forties, who worked with the efficient quiet of people who had been doing this together for years and did not need to narrate it. They included her in the work without treating her as a guest. She appreciated this.

In the afternoon she read the log.

She was in 2125 now. Adaeze at seventy-four. The entries were shorter than ever — not weakness, the way the handwriting was still clear and level, but compression. The preamble had dropped out of her practice entirely. Each entry was what the entry was.

March 2125: a note on the Curator's patrol schedule showing an expansion of the eastern survey zone. No comment on what this meant. It was data; she filed it as data.

April 2125: a brief notation on the corridor approaching Kesmark, a change in the processing station's staff composition, the new assessor's name. No comment on whether this was good or bad.

May 2125, the last water junction tracking entry — sector six, eastern approach, rated as below specification, decline rate at forty-two percent against the 2098 baseline. The same entry, the same format, the same location, twenty-seventh consecutive year. No response from the Compact infrastructure board in any of the twenty-seven years. No active work order. No new inspection scheduled.

She held the log and looked at that entry.

Twenty-seven consecutive years of observation. A single component failing at a rate that was consistent, predictable, and documented in a system that no one in the Compact was looking at. Not because the system was hidden — the Compact's infrastructure board had access to the survey data, theoretically; the Rootstock network had been filing copies with the relevant Compact monitoring systems since 2104. The Compact's monitoring systems were not reading them. The empty zones were empty. What happened in them was not a concern requiring active response.

Six to eleven million people. The eastern junction sector serving the basin's water management. The component in Adaeze's 2125 tracking entry: the same component Dez's infrastructure contact had given the eighteen-month projection for.

She put the log away and looked at the ceiling of the borrowed room.

The ceiling was low stone, smooth with age, sealed with a clay compound she didn't recognize. Third kind. Someone had built this room a long time ago and the room was still in good condition.

* * *

The second day, Rua took her to the junction.

They left at first light. A two-hour walk east on a route that ran along the basin's northern face and then dropped into a gully between two ridgelines. Rua walked with the pace of someone who made this route regularly and had stopped finding it interesting as scenery. She gave the route's practical information — where the footing was tricky, where to cross the drainage channel before the gully — and nothing else. They talked about the soil improvement assessment. Rua explained what she was looking for and why it mattered and what she would do if the work had not held. She explained it with the specificity of a person who found the work itself interesting enough to describe accurately. Kirin listened.

The junction was at the bottom of the gully. A concrete housing, original construction from the 2060s or 70s, with external inspection ports at three points and a pressure gauge on the eastern face that was still functional — she could see the needle holding position. The component itself was inside the housing, accessible from the lower inspection port with a quarter-turn on the port fastener.

Rua opened the port.

"The component is a flexible membrane system," she said. "High-pressure water management junction. This one was rated for sixty years at design specification. It is now approaching sixty years at below-specification conditions — the soil compression on the eastern face has been increasing the lateral stress on the housing, and the membrane has been compensating for that stress by flexing beyond its design parameter." She crouched at the port and pointed. "You can see the deformation in the membrane surface here."

Kirin crouched beside her and looked.

The membrane was visible at the back of the port — a curved surface, gray-white, with a quality she had been trained to read. She read it. The material was fatigued: a pattern of micro-creasing in the surface that was not yet cracking but was the stage before cracking, the specific stress signature of a material that had been asked to do more than it was built for over a long period of time. She had seen this signature in submitted materials at the CapEx Board. Not often, because the Compact's procurement system required replacement before material reached this condition. When she had seen it, the material had been removed from service.

This material had been in service for three more years past what Adaeze's tracking had rated as the outer limit of its design life.

"When this fails," she said.

"The housing will lose pressure integrity," Rua said. "The junction serves three distribution lines. One runs to the basin's main irrigation system. One runs to the settlement's domestic water supply. One runs north to the secondary fields." She stood. "The domestic supply has a storage tank — forty-eight hours of capacity at normal use. The irrigation system has no storage capacity; it runs direct from the junction. The secondary fields have a spring-fed backup that covers approximately thirty percent of need in dry months." She closed the port. "When this fails, we lose the main irrigation supply. The basin's managed agriculture — the forty-hectare plot — depends on the junction for sixty percent of its water input during the growing season." She looked at the housing. "The growing season begins in six weeks."

Kirin looked at the concrete housing. The pressure gauge needle, holding.

"What does replacement require," she said.

"We have been looking at replacement for four years," Rua said. "The membrane specification is not a type we can fabricate locally. The joining technology on the new housing — whatever we put in to replace this — requires a skill we do not currently have on the settlement and cannot develop in six weeks." She picked up the probe case. "We have been in contact with two neighboring settlements about a shared replacement project. The project requires materials we can source through the Outside network, but the materials take eight to twelve weeks for delivery from the supply network's current inventory locations." She paused. "The component will fail before the replacement arrives. We have been managing the risk by reducing the load on the junction — we are running the tertiary agricultural plots on spring backup and reserving the junction's output for the primary basin fields and the domestic supply. This buys time. It does not fix the problem."

"The Compact's infrastructure board has the same rating data," Kirin said. "They've had it for twenty-seven years."

"They have it," Rua said. "They are not reading it." She started back up the gully. "This junction serves a zone the Compact considers empty. The monitoring is automated — it files reports to systems that are not actively staffed for these sectors. No one has flagged it for inspection because no one has a reason to look for it."

Kirin followed.

"The infrastructure report in the archive," she said. "Adaeze's second instruction. The one that goes to Brigid Aelfgar, in Sable River."

"Yes."

"That's what the report is for."

"That is what I have inferred," Rua said. "From what Nnedi told me about the instruction and what the timing of the junction's condition implies." She walked. "The infrastructure report will not fix the junction. By the time a Compact infrastructure board could initiate a response process, the junction will have already failed. What the report does is put the Compact's infrastructure board in the position of having been formally notified of a known failure in a zone they have classified as empty — and the failure will affect a population they have classified as not present." She glanced at Kirin. "The claim changes meaning when the infrastructure crisis has already happened. When the water system fails and six million people need a response the Compact has to provide because they are now recognized. That is different from an abstract equity claim in a document."

They climbed.

Kirin thought about Brigid Aelfgar, in Sable River, who had been waiting since at least 2122 for a contact that had never come. A Rootstock-adjacent placement, Adaeze's log had said. Someone placed her the way I was placed, in a role adjacent to mine. Adaeze did not approach her. Brigid has been waiting.

She had wondered, since finding that entry, why Adaeze had not approached her. She thought she was beginning to understand. You did not give this to someone until the conditions were in place. You did not give it early, because early was too soon, because the response required the crisis to be real rather than projected. Brigid had been waiting in the right position. When Kirin arrived with the infrastructure report, Brigid would have the grounds to act that she hadn't had before.

Timing. Thirty-five years of timing.

She walked.

* * *

Berin Kosta arrived on the third afternoon, seven days after Ade had sent him the relay.

He came up the western descent with a pack that was significantly lighter than Kirin's had been — the pack of a person who had spent eleven months inside the Outside and had learned what the network could provide and what it couldn't, and had stopped carrying the second category. He was in his thirties, Compact-trained in the way that showed in small things: the way he organized his gear at the gate, the particular posture he adopted when he was processing something new. But the eleven months had worked on these things. The processing posture had become something less formal, less about positioning himself relative to an assessment.

He looked at her and said: "You're Kirin."

"Yes."

"The key authenticated at the way-station," he said. "Ade's relay said increased register. The whole network talked about it." He looked at the settlement around them, then back at her. "Berin. I've been at sector twelve, eleven months. The relay said I should find you at Rua's."

"You moved fast," she said. "The relay said one week."

"I finished the section I was on." He looked at the building she was standing in front of. "Two days of walking at a decent pace is two days. I had a decent pace." He paused. "There's a junction on the east approach that I wanted to look at on the way. The notation in the archive documents it as a critical infrastructure point."

"Sector six," she said.

"Sector six." He looked at her. "The archive has twenty-seven years of condition ratings on that junction. Consistent below-spec, decline rate accelerating over the last five years." He paused. "How bad is it in person."

"I saw it this morning," she said. "Membrane fatigue, third-stage stress signature. Sixty percent probability of failure within the current growing season."

He was quiet for a moment. "The archive document rated it at six months, as of the most recent entry. That was eight months ago."

"The projection has moved," she said. "Dez's contact in the corridor gave eighteen months, two months ago. Rua's local assessment gives six to eight weeks before the growing season creates load conditions the membrane won't sustain." She paused. "The timeline is collapsing faster than the models."

He looked at her.

"Then the report needs to move," he said.

"Yes."

"Where is it going."

"Sable River," she said. "A contact at the CapEx Board. Named in Adaeze's log."

He nodded. He asked no further questions about the contact. He had been in the Outside long enough to understand the economy of named contacts.

"What's the crossing timeline," he said.

"Route west opens in the morning. Fourth day from today." She looked at him. "Why."

"I came to help if there's something I can do," he said. "I have a set of route notes from the archive documents — junctions, maintenance points, population nodes — that nobody on this side has in organized form. And I've been in the corridor before." He shrugged — the particular shrug of a person who was not asking for anything but was available. "What the next four months look like, I don't know. But I'm not useful to the archive while the section is midway through. I'm more useful on the road."

She looked at him for a moment.

"Come meet Rua," she said. "She's the council infrastructure lead. She'll have questions about your archive notes."

He picked up his pack and followed her.

* * *

The relay came in on the third evening, two hours before the council meeting.

Rua set it in front of Kirin at the work table without comment. Kirin read it. It was from the Outside network's infrastructure monitoring chain — not a communication between people, but a forwarded sensor report from the eastern sector automated monitoring system. The kind of relay that the network flagged automatically when a reading crossed a threshold.

The threshold crossed: the sector six eastern junction had registered a pressure drop in its primary housing — point seven percent over the last eighteen hours. Not a catastrophic drop. A rate of change.

At point seven percent per eighteen hours, the housing would reach critical failure pressure in fourteen to twenty-one days.

Not weeks. Not a model. A measurement.

She read it twice. Then she set it on the table and looked at it.

She had been carrying the concept of the junction failure for two months — as a number in a report, as Dez's revised projection, as Rua's qualitative assessment, as the twenty-seven years of entries in Adaeze's log. She had been to the junction itself that morning and seen the fatigue signature in the membrane. She had read this as a real thing.

The relay made it a different kind of real. Not forecast. Happening.

Fourteen to twenty-one days.

The growing season began in six weeks. The junction would fail two to three weeks before the growing season. The irrigation system would go down before the crops that depended on it went in. Not during the growing season. Before it. The basin's forty-hectare managed plot would go into the growing season with sixty percent of its water supply missing.

Seven hundred people in this basin. Six to eleven million people across the Outside's settlements, many of them on the same infrastructure network, many of them with similar junctions in similar condition.

She put the relay down on the table.

Rua was watching her.

"The council meeting is in two hours," Rua said. "The timing of the relay is — " she stopped. "It does not change what I asked you three days ago."

"No," Kirin said. "It doesn't change what you asked. It changes my calculation about what the answer costs if I say no."

Rua looked at her with the quality of someone who considered that a fair assessment.

"Fourteen to twenty-one days," Kirin said. "That's the rate of change in the housing pressure."

"I read the relay."

"Can the replacement project be moved up."

"No. The materials are in transit. Eight to twelve weeks, we were told six weeks ago. They will not arrive before the failure." Rua sat. "We will manage the failure when it comes. We have managed failures before. We know what the emergency protocols are. We will survive the growing season with the spring backup and reserve stores and reduced planting." She looked at the table. "The question is whether we survive it, or whether we survive it and have grounds to make a Compact infrastructure board respond. Those are different outcomes."

"If the claim is active," Kirin said.

"If the claim is active and the Compact's infrastructure board has been formally notified of the junction's condition," Rua said, "then when the failure happens, it happens to a recognized population in a formally documented crisis. The Compact cannot process the claim and then ignore the infrastructure consequence of the claim. They are bound by their own protocol." She paused. "Whether they honor that binding is a different question. But they are bound."

"And if the claim is not active," Kirin said.

"Then the junction fails. We manage. The Compact's infrastructure board remains unnotified and unstimulated. And the seven other junctions in similar condition across the eastern sector — most of which are not within a settlement's immediate maintenance reach — fail at their own pace. Without response."

Kirin sat with this.

Through the house's window: the gathering space in the evening light. People coming toward the main building from different directions, the settling movement of a community organizing itself for a meeting. Berin Kosta was somewhere in there — she had last seen him with Rua at the drainage profiles, going through his archive notes with the specific attention of a person who had organized the same data and wanted to see what the local version showed differently.

Eme was somewhere in there, doing whatever work she did after the day's field tasks.

Veda would be in the gallery.

She picked up the relay.

"I'll give you an answer at the meeting," she said.

Rua looked at her.

"The three days was a courtesy," she said. "You have been making this decision since you found the key in your grandmother's apartment."

Kirin looked at the relay in her hand. A sensor reading. A pressure drop. A number.

"Yes," she said.

* * *

The council meeting ended late.

She walked back to the borrowed room in the dark, through the gathering space, past the fire pit where someone had built the evening's fire and it was burning now — the specific orange of dry wood, the smell of it crossing the stone-paved space at this temperature and this air quality in a way that was particular to this elevation, this basin, this specific combination of the settlement's geography. She had been here three days and she could already tell this smell from the second way-station's fire, from the corridor's managed sector's burns, from anything she had known before.

She sat on the bed.

The survey case was on the floor at the foot of the bed. Rua had given it to her after the meeting — both keys inside, the disc and the card, in the sealed compartment. *They stay together,* Rua had said. *That's the protocol. Now that the handshake has completed, the keys need to stay in proximity until the filing.* She had said it with the flat specificity of someone conveying technical information, not making a request.

She opened the compartment.

Both keys on her palm.

The disc was warmer than the card. She had not noticed this before — she had only held the card alone. With both, she could feel the difference. The disc ran at a slightly higher register, the way a mechanism ran hotter when it was doing more work. Which made sense: the founding appointment was the older one, the original claim, the one the protocol had been built to serve. The interior appointment had been added later, in the 2080s, as a mechanism for bringing the claim across the Compact's boundary at the right moment.

She held both and felt the warmth of each.

Adaeze had held this card for thirty-five years.

Nnedi had held the disc for forty.

They had never been in the same room.

She closed her hand around both.

Tomorrow morning: the route west. The four-day walk back to the second way-station. The corridor, the managed sector, the coast. The maritime transit facility. The Compact's side of the boundary. Sable River. Brigid Aelfgar at the CapEx Board, who had been waiting since 2122 with whatever patience she had left. The infrastructure report in Kirin's sealed sleeve, undelivered, the second of Adaeze's three instructions.

The third instruction: take the key to the plateau and let what happens happen.

She had. And what had happened was: a sensor reading on a third evening in a borrowed room, and a decision she had been making since her grandmother's apartment in Sable River, which was only now finding its final shape.

The fire in the gathering space outside was still going. She could see the light of it under the door, the orange flicker on the stone floor.

Berin Kosta was in the storage unit two rooms north. He had not offered again to come with her. He had asked Rua three specific questions about the junction replacement project and offered three specific items from his archive notes that were relevant to the replacement materials' sourcing. He would have his own decision to make in the morning.

Somewhere in the eastern sector, the junction housing was registering point seven percent pressure drop every eighteen hours.

She opened her hand.

Both keys on her palm, warm, the disc and the card, the disc a little warmer, both running at the state they had arrived at when they first touched the same surface — the settled, active, continuous warmth of a system that was in the condition it was built for.

She put them in her jacket pocket, both together.

She lay down on the bed.

Outside: the basin, the managed fields in the dark, the water management line she could not see but knew was there, carrying what it could carry for as long as it could carry it. The junction at the bottom of the gully, the pressure gauge needle holding, holding, not yet at the threshold, the membrane flexing in its slow and irreversible way toward the point where it would flex no further.

Fourteen to twenty-one days.

The road was long. There was water at the end of it.

She slept.
