# Writer Notes — Episode 10: "Adaeze's People"

## What I Was Going For

The episode's engine is the handshake — not the crossing, not the discovery, but the specific moment when two hardware tokens that have been separated for forty-eight years complete the authentication cycle they were built for. I wanted that moment to be quiet, grounded, and completely non-magical. Kirin reads it as a materials analyst: protocol behavior, hardware state, the specific warmth of a system arriving at the condition it was designed for. The emotion comes through restraint.

The ensemble pressure from the instructions required three distinct POV-adjacent perspectives in one episode: Rua (cold open — practical, muddy, an infrastructure person who has been carrying an unexplained object for two months because someone she trusted told her to), Veda (Adaeze's ideological opponent — the character who articulates the real cost of legibility and can't be dismissed), and Eme (Nnedi's granddaughter — the youngest generation of the founding line, completely unromantic about the founding building). I avoided giving any of them epigrams. Veda in particular was a risk — she's articulating a real political position — but I tried to keep her in argument mode rather than wisdom-dispensing mode.

## The Junction Scene

Taking Kirin to the junction physically was important. She has been reading data about this component for two months. Actually seeing the membrane fatigue signature in person — reading it the way she reads materials — closes the loop between her professional skill and the story's stakes. It also grounds the abstract infrastructure threat in a specific object that can be felt and pointed at. Rua and Kirin talk about the junction the way two infrastructure people would talk: in terms of failure modes, replacement timelines, and protocol obligations. Nobody delivers a speech about why it matters.

## The Relay

The sensor relay arriving on the third evening was the right turn — it changes the decision from abstract to time-constrained without being a cliffhanger. Kirin has been deciding since she found the key in Adaeze's apartment. The relay doesn't create the decision; it removes the last reason to defer it. "You have been making this decision since you found the key in your grandmother's apartment." That's Rua understanding something Kirin hasn't said out loud.

## Veda

Veda is the episode's sharpest scene. I kept it in the kitchen, short, and let her walk away without drama. Her argument — that working inside the Compact's system legitimizes the Compact's right to keep the Outside hidden — is correct. So is Adaeze's argument. The episode doesn't resolve this. It shouldn't.

## Berin Kosta

He arrives without fanfare. He's been inside long enough to know that his job on the road is to be useful without requiring explanation. His three questions to Rua about the replacement project are how I show that he's oriented himself properly: he didn't arrive to be part of the bearer's story, he arrived because the junction timing had moved and someone who had organized that data should be present. His decision to come west is unresolved — I left it open for Ep 11 to decide whether he stays on the plateau or joins the route.

## Veda's Concern for Ep 11-12

Her objection about legibility splitting the community is a real problem that Ep 11-12 will need to address. The filing, if it happens, doesn't just change the Outside's relationship with the Compact — it changes the Outside's internal relationship to its own independence. Some settlements will take Commons access. Some won't. That's the consequence of winning.

## Landing

I went with Kirin holding both keys in the dark, feeling the temperature difference between them — the disc slightly warmer, the founding appointment running hotter because it's older and has been doing more work. This is the last sensory image I wanted: two objects, two weights, both warm, both different, both hers now. Not a summary. Not a statement. A physical fact she's going to carry west.

## What I'm Worried About

The middle section (Veda / Eme / founding building) risks feeling like a greatest-hits of the outline's promised beats delivered efficiently. Eme is thinly drawn — she serves a function (showing Kirin the building) but doesn't have much interiority. If I had more room I'd give her a harder edge. As is, she's useful but barely present. Ep 13 or later could deepen her if the story returns to the plateau.
