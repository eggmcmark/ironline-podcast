# Writer Notes — Episode 3 (rewrite): "Salt"

*This script supersedes the v1 "The Frontier" episode (archived in this directory as `script-v1.md`, `metadata-v1.json`, `writer-notes-v1.md`).*

## Why the rewrite

User feedback on the v1 script and on Episodes 2 by reflection:

- The empty zone read as pastoral. Drones cutting lawns, deer in cafeterias — beautiful, melancholy, and weightless. There was no sense that it might bite.
- Every supporting character was warm, wise, and immediately helpful. Dez, Seun, Tov, Caramel — a community that welcomed a stranger and gave her supplies inside an hour. There was no friction, no negotiation, no cost.
- The authentication key was a paperweight. People kept saying it mattered. It didn't do anything.
- Stakes were absent. By the end of three episodes there was no antagonist presence, no risk that cost Kirin anything, no reveal that recontextualized what we thought the story was.
- Metaphors stacked. "Steady weather" / "second heartbeat" / "patient as the road" — three similes for the same image inside a paragraph.
- Thematic content was articulated by characters rather than shown. The "everyone is at the edge of the map" coda at the end of v1 ep3 stated the season's thesis as text.
- The interior structure was rhythmically repetitive. *She thought X. She thought Y. She thought Z.*

The v5 outline (updated with this rewrite) addresses the season-arc consequences: the antagonist is now planted in Ep 3, every episode has a grounded engine, and the Outside reveal becomes a sequence of recontextualizations rather than a single late-season door-opening. The writer prompt has new anti-slop discipline language to prevent regression.

## What I built into this episode

**The cold open.** Forty seconds of POV from an antagonist operative we never name, processing a body on the highway. The listener now knows three things Kirin doesn't: the chits are being hunted, there is an organized operation, and the dead woman Kirin will find at mile 22 is not the first. This is the Card-style setup — every later scene has an undercurrent of dramatic irony.

**Kesmark recast.** No longer a quaint last stop. A Foundation Assessor at the depot. A nervous shopkeeper who reads Kirin's pack. A child who casually parrots his father's empty-for-a-reason refrain (Compact paradigm blindness shown in two lines without thesis statement). Kirin lies competently for the first time in her life, and notices that she can.

**The drone follow.** A small, tense beat that does several things: it confirms the cold-open framing (someone is watching for her), it gives the maintenance grid a weight character it didn't have in v1, and it gives Kirin her first physiological panic in the open. The road-against-her-palm beat replaces the v1 "she thought... she thought..." pattern with a single concrete image.

**The body at mile 22.** This is the hinge of the first half. Stephenson-grade specificity in inventory: the Free Zone canvas pack with brass D-rings (callback to the cold open), the band of pale skin where a ring had been, the specific groove at the temple. Kirin does the wrong thing (takes the chit) and the right thing (covers the body) and runs. The chit itself is the proof that there are other carriers — the Adaeze "line" the operative referenced in the cold open.

**Salt and Naila.** The way-station replaces the v1 settlement. Salt is a hard transactional place, not a community. Naila Brace is built as an anti-Yoda: tired, competent, fee-collecting, deliberately not warm. She charges in salt because salt cannot be faked — a Stephensonian world detail that is also literally and thematically loaded (Kirin will pay in salt; Salt the place; Adaeze paid three pounds to ask a question; the ledger has a salt-rime on its spine). The "fence/hands" greeting and the bolt-from-outside on Kirin's door work as character moments and as economy worldbuilding without ever lecturing.

**Dez reinvented.** Same biography (former Free Zone logistics coordinator, eight years out here) but recast as a morally ambiguous operator with her own interests. Sharp, watchful, asks more questions than she answers, takes the meeting with Kirin in her own time. Naila explicitly tells Kirin not to fully trust her: *her interest does not require her to do you wrong this week, that is the most you are going to get from anybody in this corridor and it is also enough to live on.* That's the season's working alliance ethic, articulated by a character whose right to articulate it has been earned.

**Bex.** A Curator operative planted at Salt, bored Kirin's measure during dinner, came to her door at 4 a.m., did not enter, left to report. The "did not enter, that's information not theft" speech from Naila is doing the work that two earlier wise speeches in v1 used to do, but it's coming from someone whose worldview is operationally specific.

**The ledger and the recontextualization.** Adaeze Okonkwo — the pre-Compact name from Episode 2 — passed through Salt in September 2125, three nights, paid three pounds of salt for one question, got it right. Naila reads it aloud from the salt-cured leather book without turning the book toward Kirin. The Sable River elder-care facility had Adaeze on shift that month. The cover was deeper than we knew.

**The Curator and the curated grid.** This is the world reveal. The empty zone is not autonomous; it is curated, and it has been for at least fourteen years. Multiple factions. One is run by a person Naila will only refer to as the Curator. He is the first articulated antagonist in the series. He is the person Adaeze went east in September to confirm was still on his old route. He is also the person Bex reports to. We do not see him on screen. We get his reach through his operative's voice in the cold open and through the comm broadcast at the end.

**Adaeze's possible murder.** Naila stops short of saying it: *I think someone is a word that is hiding behind two more words, and I will not say what those two words are out loud in a building that has comms in it.* This reframes Episode 1's "died in her sleep at eighty-nine" into a question Kirin will carry for the rest of the season. It does not state the case. It opens it.

**The key, finally working.** The key warms and cools in handshake patterns once Kirin and Dez are on the unmapped maintenance road east of Salt. Kirin understands what's happening — it's a hardware authentication token doing what it was built to do, talking to something in the road. Stephensonian: the magic object is just a smartcard, and Kirin has the materials-analyst eye to see it as such.

**The landing.** No coda. No Kirin meditating on what it means. She watches the wing mirror because Dez told her to. The road goes on. Trust the listener.

## Voice and craft notes

- **Metaphor budget:** at most one per scene, grounded in Kirin's professional eye (sample at her assay station, the way she tested for the way light hit it). Most scenes have zero. The "second heartbeat" / "warm like a steadying hand" / "hands like steady weather" stack from v1 is gone.
- **Character voice differentiation:** the cold-open operative speaks in short declaratives and bureaucratic euphemism (*don't gild*). Naila is clipped and medical (*ulnar fracture* is the kind of word that would be in her mouth in a different scene). Dez is mid-rhythm, watchful, withholds. The shopkeeper's *there's another one* and the boy's *empty for a reason* are doing local-color work without pulling toward folksiness. None of them sounds like another.
- **Dialogue:** contractions; people interrupt themselves and one another; lies are told and observed; nobody delivers an epigram. Naila's longest speech (the Curator one) is structurally a briefing, not a monologue, and she explicitly refuses to give the next layer.
- **Show, don't tell:** Kirin never "realizes" anything in the prose. She notices things. The reader infers. The Compact paradigm blindness is shown by the boy at the cart and the manager's *from who?* — never stated.
- **No friendly strangers:** the depot manager, the shopkeeper, Naila, Dez, Bex — all transactional, suspicious, or careful. The two moments of warmth in the episode (Naila's tea, the bolt as kindness-and-notice) are both ambiguous and earned.

## Continuity callouts

- Adaeze pre-Compact name: **Adaeze Okonkwo** (consistent with Ep 2).
- Tomas's instruction to buy salt is called back without explanation; Tomas knew because Tomas reads. Reinforces his quiet competence from Ep 2.
- Amira's name almost slips out of Kirin's mouth at the depot — small confirmation that Kirin's backchannel architecture (Ep 2) is on her mind.
- The "three pounds of salt" Adaeze paid in September is the same payment unit Kirin paid; the episode uses salt as the world's verification currency the way the Compact uses EQU, without ever lecturing on the parallel.
- The first chit (from the dead Renata) bears one glyph from Adaeze's pre-Compact name in the same hand as the photograph back in Ep 1. Direct material continuity.

## What I'm setting up

- **The Curator** (named, off screen) is the first articulated antagonist. He is the v5 outline's Ep 11 reveal-figure (Ardent), here introduced earlier per the user's note about not backloading the threat.
- **Yusra at Larch depot** (named only) is the Ep 4 handoff, replacing Cleo from v1.
- **Naila's debt to Adaeze** is a thread — she will repay it in Ep 5 or Ep 12 per the v5 outline.
- **The handshake behavior of the key** is the new active object. Subsequent episodes can do specific things with it: it should let Kirin authenticate at certain interfaces, attract specific surveillance, fail in specific ways. It is no longer decorative.
- **The Curator's network** is now alive in the corridor. By Ep 4 they will have an active intercept on Kirin. Naila bought three days.
- **Adaeze's possible murder** is open. Episode 11's revelation about her cover-as-watch is now sharper.

## What I'm worried about

- **The cold-open POV shift** breaks the close-Kirin POV established in Ep 1 and partially broken in Ep 2 (Perun, Brigid). I think it earns its place — without the cold open the listener has no idea Kirin is being hunted, and the dramatic irony is the whole engine of the first half. But it's worth noting as a structural choice.
- **The Curator is unnamed and off screen.** This is deliberate (Stephensonian / Card-y restraint), but it does mean the antagonist's presence in this episode is mostly atmospheric. If a listener wants a face by Ep 3, they don't get one. They get a voice on a comm and a single first name (Bex) for an operative.
- **Two adjacent location-and-character introductions** (Salt + Naila + Dez + Bex) is a lot. I tried to keep each entrance lean — Naila gets her physical presence and rules in two short paragraphs; Dez gets her entrance in six lines and her interrogation of Kirin in another twenty. Bex is essentially a presence at the edge of three scenes. If the listener is tracking, this should land. If not, the audio's pacing will need to give Naila and Dez extra breath at their first lines.
- **Naila's Curator briefing** is the longest monologue in the episode. I tried to break it with Kirin's questions and Naila's refusals and the deliberate *I will not say what those two words are* moment. It still runs long. It is doing four jobs (introducing the Curator, recontextualizing the empty zone, recontextualizing Adaeze, setting up the next move). I think the trade is worth it. Worth a read-aloud check.
