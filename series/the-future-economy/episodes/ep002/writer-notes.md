# Writer Notes — Episode 2: "What She Left Behind"

## Creative Choices

**Opening POV shift to Perun.** Ep1 was a pure close-third on Kirin. Ep2 opens on Perun at the Archive at three in the morning, unable to stop himself from running Rootstock queries. The intent is to widen the show's POV aperture in a careful, scene-boundaried way — to signal that this is an ensemble story without abandoning Kirin as the primary consciousness. Perun's interiority also gives the listener a piece of information Kirin does not yet have: that Perun himself was one of the witnesses. The reveal is delivered via a prayer-like reading of three names with his own at the top. I wanted that moment to land before Kirin's day even starts.

**Brigid's scene.** Kirin's supervisor got a brief interiority pass because she's one of the "structures of the Compact seen from the inside" that this episode needs to show. Brigid is not the antagonist, not an obstacle, not a cartoon of institutional goodness — she's a Founder-class assessor with twenty-three years of experience in knowing when good analysts are leaving, and her response is to let Kirin go well. This is a deliberate counter to the narrative temptation to make institutional figures obstacles. The Compact is furniture; the people inside it are trying to live good lives. Brigid's final line about not discarding the surface is also a thematic set-up for Ep3 onward — the warning against treating the Compact as the enemy once Kirin starts to see its blind spots.

**The dinner scene as the ensemble centerpiece.** The user's standing note is that the world must feel wide and the cast must feel ensemble. Ep1 was intimate by design (a grieving woman alone with a found object). Ep2 had to open the social aperture. The dinner at Lian and Benno's is meant to do three things simultaneously: (1) show a normal Compact domestic evening in loving detail so the listener feels the texture of the life Kirin is leaving, (2) introduce four new characters (Lian, Benno, Rufus, Amira) plus Isa the toddler, each with distinct voice and rhythm, and (3) deliver the thematic hinge of the episode via the casual nod at Rufus's "empty for a reason." I wanted the nod to feel completely ordinary — not a villainous moment, not a stupid moment, just the kind of thing reasonable people say at dinner without noticing. The horror, from Kirin's new perspective, is that she has said it herself many times. Amira is deliberately the quietest character at the table because she has lived at the edge of the map and knows when to not talk; her offer of the second comm at the end is one of the episode's small graces.

**Reva at The Bench.** The outline called for a visit to an old-timers' social club with one woman who hints at pre-Compact settlements. Reva is that woman. I made her very old (late 90s) and from the plateau settlement itself — she was there as a visitor in 2081, which puts her in the right temporal window to have known Adaeze personally. I gave her a grace note about Adaeze's hands ("steady weather") that Mira will echo later, and I had her refuse to know the settlement's name — because her memory has let go of it, not because she's withholding. The line "you do not expose a choice" is load-bearing for the episode's emotional logic: it explains why Adaeze hid, why Reva never approached her across the harvest plaza, and why the Rootstock diaspora inside the Compact is invisible to itself. I had Reva name the settlement's founder (Nnedi) because Ep9 or Ep10 will need that name to be set up now.

**The Turn: Mira and the song.** I considered giving Kirin a full elder-care facility scene (the outline suggested it) but the word count was already near the upper bound after the Reva scene, and the Turn didn't need a full scene — it needed a pavement encounter. Mira comes out the side door, recognizes Kirin, and offers one specific detail: the song Adaeze sang to a dying man named Efe, in an unknown language, which translated as *the road is long, but there is water at the end of it.* That single line does the work. Kirin understands in the pavement that Adaeze was not hiding but on watch — that the entire small Keeper-class life was a post, and the box is the relief. This is the emotional reframe the episode has been building toward. The Mira encounter is also a seed: Efe stopped eating the day after Adaeze died. That's a thread to pick up later if the story wants it.

**The Landing: the handoff, not the departure.** I wanted the final image to be the key held up in the first true light of morning at Adaeze's garden window, not Kirin walking out the door of her own apartment. The key's warmth is the callback from Ep1; the naming of "Adaeze Okonkwo" aloud at the window is the recognition; the line "I have it now" is the handoff. The episode ends not on cliffhanger and not on departure but on acceptance — the watch changes hands. The actual leaving is one sentence at the very end.

**Tomas gets interior space.** In Ep1 Tomas was a voice on a phone call. Here he gets a full scene in his workshop. The decision to keep him practical and slightly dry (his "I'm going to worry about you every day" speech framed as information, not pleading) was a deliberate push away from the sentimental-best-friend trope. He's a craftsman thinking about kilns and dry wood. That's how he loves.

**Language and voice.** I tried to match Ep1's sentence rhythms closely — long occasional clauses for interiority, short breath points for dialogue and action, strategic repetitions ("empty for a reason"), atmospheric transitions. The Tomas workshop scene deliberately opens with the same sensory register as Ep1's CapEx Board morning scene (workshop smells, the plane, the hands). Reva's speech is syntactically slightly different — slower, more declarative, faintly foreign-inflected without being accented in a distracting way. Mira gets the plainest voice of anyone in the episode because that is the voice of people who have spent their lives holding strangers' hands.

## What I'm Setting Up

- **Adaeze Okonkwo** as canon — the pre-Compact name, the Igbo lineage. This should echo forward.
- **Tamsat** as the language of the letters and potentially of the plateau settlement. Perun reads it rustily, which means he can translate the letters off-screen during the journey (Ep3-4 potential).
- **Nnedi** as the settlement founder — name now dropped so it can surface again.
- **Perun's own history** as a Rootstock records specialist from the western depopulated zone — this gives him a stake in Kirin's journey and opens the possibility that he could be pulled back in later. He does NOT go with her in Ep3 (outline has Kirin crossing the frontier and meeting Dez). But he is no longer "just the archivist."
- **Efe and the song** as a grace note that can be picked up or dropped depending on later need.
- **Amira's backchannel** — she lived in the eastern reach, she has the short-range comm, and she is not a close friend. If Kirin needs a lifeline that isn't Tomas, Amira is positioned.
- **Brigid's "Held at supervisor discretion" reclassification** — three of three analysts returned. Sets up an unspoken expectation that Kirin will be the fourth, which we can play with if the story wants a return that subverts it.

## Worries

- **Length.** The script came in at 9,849 words, ~15% over the 8,500 upper bound. At ~180 wpm that is roughly 55 minutes of audio, versus Ep1's 38 minutes. The overage is mostly in sections A and B; section C was compressed deliberately. Options: (a) accept the longer episode as an artifact of this being the set-up episode before the journey begins, (b) trim Brigid's scene by 200 words, (c) trim the dinner scene by 400 words (it's the fattest block), (d) trim Reva's scene which I think is already tight. My honest read is that the dinner and Brigid scenes could both be tightened without losing anything essential. But the user asked for highlights before audio, so the decision is theirs.
- **POV shifts to Perun and Brigid.** Ep1 was pure Kirin close-third. Opening Ep2 on Perun is a structural break from that. I believe it's the right call given the standing ensemble directive, but it is a choice that should be noticed and either endorsed or reversed going forward. If the user prefers strict Kirin POV throughout Season 1, Perun's scene could be rewritten from Kirin's imagination ("she knew he would not have gone home") or simply cut.
- **Mira is a very small character doing a very large amount of emotional work.** The Turn rides on her single speech. I think the line about the song lands — but if it feels too neat, Mira's speech could be stretched into a proper scene inside the facility instead of a pavement encounter, at the cost of another 500+ words.
- **The episode is heavily weighted toward conversation.** Ep1 had several long passages of Kirin alone with objects. Ep2 is mostly dialogue scenes. This reflects the nature of the story (she's gathering information from people) but it means the sensory texture is carried more by the settings than by Kirin's interior relationship with things. Ep3 will need to swing the pendulum back toward objects and environment as Kirin leaves the city.
- **The "watch" metaphor in the Turn** ("Adaeze was on watch; the box is the relief") is possibly too articulate for a moment that should feel felt rather than reasoned. I tried to ground it in Kirin's materials-analyst specificity (reading a substrate) but a read-through may still find it pushing into thesis territory. Worth examining at review.
