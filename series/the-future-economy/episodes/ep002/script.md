# Episode 2: "What She Left Behind"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin digs into her grandmother's past and finds a life carefully erased. One long day in Sable River, and then the leaving.*

---

Perun had not gone home.

The Archive at three in the morning had a particular sound — not silence, because the environmental systems were always on, maintaining humidity at forty percent and temperature at eighteen degrees for the sake of paper and data cores and the thin skins of pre-Compact films that nobody asked to see anymore. It was the sound of a building holding its breath for things nobody remembered. Perun found it restful, usually. Tonight it was something else. Tonight it was the sound of a room that had been waiting for something, and the something had finally come, and now the room wanted to know what he was going to do about it.

He sat at the back workstation with the Rootstock reader plugged into a port he hadn't used in nine years. The reader was older than his apprentices. He'd built it himself in 2097, from a schematic handed to him in a stairwell by a woman whose name he had agreed never to write down. Three pieces of paper, three different kinds. He had burned two after memorizing them and kept the third because it was beautiful — a fine hand-drawn signal decoder, the lines precise enough to look printed until you held them close and saw the small tremors where the pen had lifted.

The reader's screen was amber. He'd wired it that way on purpose. Amber text on black was the color of transition-era displays, and transition-era displays were what he had learned to read in the years when the world was trying to decide what it would be. His eyes knew that color the way other people's eyes knew their mother's face.

He had been running queries against the Rootstock registry for four hours. The registry fragments the Archive held were not complete — nobody had a complete copy, not even the people who had built it, because building it had been the point and completeness had never been — but the fragments were substantial. Enough to confirm that the key the woman had brought him corresponded to a real claim. Enough to confirm that the claim was not small. And enough to confirm, if he was reading the provenance chain correctly, that the claim had been registered by someone whose name he had known once, a very long time ago, in a different life.

He had not told her the name.

He was not yet sure he would.

He lifted his hands from the workstation and held them in the amber light and watched them tremble — a fine, old-man tremble that had nothing to do with what he was seeing and everything to do with the eighty-seven years of being a body. Then he set them back on the keys and kept working. There was a protocol, from the old days. If a key surfaced, you were to verify. If you verified, you were to report — not to any authority, because the point of Rootstock had been that there were no authorities. You were to report to the next link in the chain. The next link in the chain, for this key, was a dead woman.

Perun scrolled back to the primary registration record. The fragment was partial, but the witness signatures were intact. Three of them. He read the names in the amber light, one at a time, like a man saying a prayer he had not said in decades.

The first name was his own.

* * *

Kirin had not slept. She knew this the way you know weather — not by checking but by feeling it on your skin. The apartment was the pale blue of an hour before dawn, the light coming up behind the eastern buildings without quite touching anything yet, and she was sitting at her kitchen table with the photograph in front of her and the letters fanned out to the left and the authentication key on her right, a small dark weight on the pale wood, still warm.

She had looked at the photograph so many times in the last four hours that the image had started to do the thing images do when you exhaust them — it had stopped being a picture and started being a door. She could no longer see it as a composition. She could only see it as a place she had not yet been, full of people she had not yet met, with her grandmother in the middle of it, holding a child who was not her mother, smiling the way Kirin had never seen her smile.

The face was the hardest part. Kirin had known this face for twenty-one years. She had seen it at every angle, in every light. She had watched it age. She had watched it pinched with worry when Kirin was sick as a child, and soft with tiredness on evenings when Adaeze had worked a double shift at the elder-care facility, and briefly wet with a kind of crying that was not for Kirin's benefit but was not hidden either, on the anniversaries of Kirin's parents' accident. She knew the geography of her grandmother's face better than any other object in the world.

And she had never seen it make this expression. The openness. The radiance. The head tilted slightly down toward the sleeping child, the mouth soft, the eyes looking at the camera with the particular ease of a person who has nothing to hide from the person holding the lens. It was not a performed smile. It was an un-performed one. A face at home.

Kirin picked up one of the letters, the one with the least fading. The paper was thicker than Compact stock — something with fiber in it, almost cloth. The script ran across the page in long, connected lines, with occasional breaks where the writer had lifted the pen to think. There were dots above some of the letters and a kind of curled mark above others, and the shape of the words was rhythmic, as if each sentence had been composed with its cadence in mind. Like something meant to be read aloud.

She held the letter to her nose, which was absurd and she knew it. It smelled like nothing. Paper and time and a faint ghost of whatever drawer Adaeze had kept them in. She put it down.

Outside, the building's plumbing made a single deep pop as the water pressure shifted for the morning — the autonomous systems bringing the network up to daytime flow. Somewhere above her, on a higher floor, a baby started crying and then stopped. The city was waking up. She was supposed to be at work in two hours.

She stood up. Her knees hurt from sitting. She walked to the window and stood in the cool blue light and looked out at Sable River waking — the slow rise of the workshop district lamps on the north side, the first courier bikes crossing Foundry Street in the slate-colored dawn, a woman below her window walking a dog that walked the way old dogs walk, with purpose and without hurry.

She thought: *I am going to go to work and finish the shift and then I am going to go back to the Archive.*

She thought: *I am going to pack.*

She did not yet think: *I am going to leave tomorrow.* But the decision was already there, unspoken, underneath the others, the way a riverbed is already under the river before the rain comes.

She went to shower. She put on her work clothes — the soft gray of a Keeper-class analyst, the Board patch on the sleeve. She put the key in the inner pocket of her jacket, the one she had used as a child for smooth stones and interesting leaves, and felt its warmth against her ribs. She put the photograph in the same pocket, wrapped in a scrap of the quilt she could not bring herself to donate.

The letters she left on the kitchen table, under a clean plate, because she did not yet know how to carry them.

* * *

Brigid Aelfgar watched Kirin Saleh come in that morning and knew Kirin was leaving.

It was nothing obvious. Kirin logged in at the correct time and pulled the first item in the queue — a dispute about a lumber grade submitted by a western Guild house, and under it a thornier item: a provenance challenge on a shipment of rare earths that had come across the Trade Board from a Free Zone broker in Meridian, routed through three intermediaries and a paper trail that Brigid had already flagged for manual assay. Kirin took both without comment. She worked the way she always worked: thoroughly, without wasted motion, her face calm.

But there was a stillness around her that Brigid had seen before in the analysts she had lost. Not defeat. The stillness of someone whose attention had reoriented onto a different axis, so that the year ahead no longer hung on the usual pegs.

Brigid finished her own morning queue and walked over to Kirin's station with two cups of tea.

"Chicory," Brigid said, setting one of the cups on Kirin's desk. "Not real."

"Thank you." Kirin took the cup with both hands the way people did when they were cold from the inside. She smiled at Brigid — a real smile, small. "I haven't had breakfast."

"I know." Brigid sat on the corner of the desk, which was a thing she did only rarely and which signaled to anyone watching that this was a conversation, not a check-in. "How are you."

"Fine."

"How are you really."

Kirin held the cup. Her eyes, Brigid noticed, were a little red. Not the red of crying. The red of not sleeping. Brigid had a decade on this analyst and knew the difference by now.

"I found something in Adaeze's apartment," Kirin said. "While I was sorting. It's complicated."

"Complicated how."

"Complicated enough that I'm going to need more leave."

Brigid set her cup down. She looked past Kirin to the service yard where two apprentices were unloading a shipment of roofing slate.

"How much more leave."

"I don't know. I think — at least a month. Maybe more."

"Where are you going."

Kirin hesitated.

"East," Kirin said finally. "Into the depopulated zone. There's something I need to confirm."

"Something you inherited."

"Something like that."

Brigid nodded. She picked up her cup again. The chicory was warm and bitter and exactly what it was.

"You know what I'm going to say," Brigid said.

"Don't go."

"No."

Kirin looked up from her cup.

"Go," Brigid said. "Come back. If you come back, your station will be here. If you don't come back, I will understand. I have been at this desk for twenty-three years and I have watched a great many promising analysts walk out of this building for reasons that looked insane to the rest of us at the time, and the ones who came back came back better, and the ones who didn't — well. Those are the ones I think about most. I don't regret any of them."

"Brigid."

"What."

"You're being too kind to me."

"I am being the exact correct amount of kind to you." Brigid stood up from the desk. "I have one request."

"Anything."

"Finish the queue today. The granite dispute resolution paperwork is due and I don't want to foist it on Petru, he's still learning the mediation format. Give me a full day's work and then go home and sleep and do whatever you need to do. Do not dramatize the leaving. If you are going to go, go the way you do everything else. Carefully. Without announcement."

Kirin nodded. Her throat was doing something. She drank the bad tea to cover it.

"And Kirin."

"Yes."

"Whatever you find out there — if you find anything at all — remember who taught you to read surfaces. She lived in that apartment for thirty-five years. Whatever is underneath, the surface was real too. Don't discard it when you find the rest."

Brigid walked back to her own desk. She opened the station record and reclassified Kirin's post as temporarily vacant, with a note in the disposition field that said, *Held at supervisor discretion.* Three times before. All three back.

She was hoping for a fourth.

* * *

The Archive was easier to find the second time. Kirin walked the same route she had walked yesterday evening — through the civic district, past the Guild tool-lending library with its rolling glass door propped open for the morning air, past the Commons recycling center where a line of Keeper-class householders were dropping off sorted metals, past the small square where a statue of a pre-Compact engineer stood in green-streaked bronze, turning her face toward a horizon that was now two hundred miles of fields.

Perun was waiting for her.

He was waiting in a way that was not obvious. He was at his workstation, wearing the same gray smock he had worn the day before, which meant he had not changed his clothes, which meant he had not gone home. But he was holding a cup of tea with both hands as if it were a warming stone, and when Kirin came in he looked up with an expression that was mostly exhausted and partly something else — something a little like recognition, as if, in the hours between her visits, she had become a different person to him.

"You found more," he said, without greeting.

"Letters and a photograph. The letters are in a script I don't recognize. The photograph is of my grandmother when she was young, holding a child. She's standing in front of a building I've never seen, in a landscape I've never seen, and the people around her are —" She stopped, because the word she wanted was *family* and she was not ready to say it out loud. "The people around her are not from anywhere I recognize."

She put the photograph on his desk. Then, with some hesitation, she put one of the letters next to it.

Perun looked at them for a long time. He did not touch them at first. He looked the way he had looked at the key the day before — like a man who recognized something and did not want to.

He picked up the letter. He read it. He set it down.

"This is Tamsat," he said.

"What's Tamsat."

"A reconstructed language. Not ancient — a twenty-first-century project. A group of linguists and the communities they were working with tried to build a common written form for a set of regional oral languages that were losing ground to the dominant ones. Tamsat was what they came up with. It was adopted, for a time, by several of the Rootstock settlements. It is not in the Compact's translation databases because it was never widely published and because the last community that used it as a daily written form was on a plateau in what used to be the southern Maghreb, and that community —" He paused. "That community either dispersed during the transition or did not. The Archive has no record of what happened to them. The Ledger has no record of them at all."

"You can read it."

"I can read some of it. My reading is rusty. I was taught it in the nineties by a woman named Ife, who was a linguist before she was anything else. She thought I should know." He looked up. His eyes were wet, which surprised Kirin — Perun had seemed like a man whose eyes would not do that. "I have not read Tamsat aloud in almost twenty years."

Kirin sat down slowly in the chair opposite his desk. The Archive around them was very quiet.

"What does the letter say."

"I am not going to read it to you all at once. Some of it is — some of it is for you. From her. I need to prepare them. Can you come back tomorrow evening."

"Yes."

"Then I will have them ready. Not translated — I am not good enough to translate them properly. But I will read them to you and tell you what the words mean, as best I can, and you can hold onto them." He paused. "There is something else."

He turned the workstation screen so she could see it. The amber text, the Rootstock registry, the partial fragment of a record. Kirin could not read it. She recognized the format from yesterday, but the actual content was a dense grid of identifiers and witness signatures and timestamp fields.

"I spent the night," Perun said, "looking for your grandmother's pre-Compact records. She is not in the Ledger before 2091. She is not in the Archive's transition-era registry. She is not in any of the fragmentary databases from the regional health authorities, which is where I found most of the pre-Compact identities I have traced for other people. She is nowhere."

"So she's a ghost."

"No. She is not a ghost. A ghost would be an absence. This is the opposite." He pointed at a line of text. "I found her in the Rootstock registry itself. Not by the name Saleh. Under another name. A witness signature on three different claim registrations between 2078 and 2086. The same hand, the same credential. It is clearly her — the verification code on the credential matches the key you brought me. No coincidence."

"What was the name."

Perun hesitated. Then he read it off the screen.

"Adaeze Okonkwo."

Kirin said the name out loud to herself, softly. *Adaeze Okonkwo.* The syllables sat strangely in her mouth — familiar in the first half, unfamiliar in the second, the last name longer and more rolling than Saleh, with a music to it that Saleh did not have. She thought: *this was my grandmother's name.* She thought: *this was the name she answered to when she was the person in the photograph.* She thought: *I do not know what to do with this.*

"Okonkwo is an Igbo name," Perun said gently. "A common one, historically. It means something like *pride* or *strength.* There was a large Igbo diaspora involved in some of the Rootstock communities in the mid-century — people whose families had emigrated generations earlier, and people who had come more recently. There were settlements that were predominantly West African in origin, and settlements that were mixed. Your grandmother was a witness on claim registrations associated with one of the mixed settlements. I do not yet know which one. The coordinates on your key may be related to it. They may not."

"Perun."

"Yes."

"Were you one of the witnesses."

He was quiet. He looked at his own hands, at the tea cup, at the amber screen. Then he looked at her.

"Yes," he said. "On the first of the three. In 2078. I was twenty-nine years old. I was a records specialist for a small community in what is now the western depopulated zone, and we did not yet know that the Compact was coming, or that when it came we would have to choose whether to join it. The woman who signed the claim that I witnessed was your grandmother. She was forty years old. She had just returned from a trip to one of the founding settlements with a registration document, and she needed a witness, and I was the records specialist, so I signed. I do not remember the claim itself in any detail. I remember her. I remember thinking that she had the steadiest hands of any person I had ever met."

Kirin did not speak. Perun did not, either, for a while.

"I came into the Compact in 2093," he said at last. "Two years after your grandmother appeared on the Ledger in Sable River. We did not know each other was here. The Compact is large. I was placed in the Archive because I knew pre-Compact formats, and she was placed in the Commons health service because she had nursing training, and we lived in the same city for thirty-three years and never spoke. I did not know she was here until yesterday, when you put the key on my desk. I recognized the credential. I did not recognize her name at first — Saleh. That was her husband's name, I assume."

"She married him in 2091. He died before I was born."

"Then she married him shortly after arriving. It is a common pattern. The transition made many new names."

Kirin looked at the screen, at the amber text she could not read. She looked at Perun, who had spent a third of a century archiving the records of a civilization that had not known he was one of the things in it. She looked at the photograph on the desk and thought about the woman in it, the one whose hands Perun remembered as steady.

"Why did she hide," Kirin said.

"I do not know." Perun's voice was very tired. "I have been asking myself this question since three in the morning, and I do not have an answer. But I will tell you what I suspect, and you can do with it what you want. I suspect that she did not come into the Compact to escape the Rootstock communities. I suspect that she came into the Compact to watch them — from here, from inside, in a place where she would not be noticed. I suspect that the box in her linen closet was not a secret she was keeping from you. I suspect it was a post she was holding, and she held it for thirty-five years, and when she died she passed it to you because you were the only one she trusted to understand what it was."

"I don't understand what it is."

"Not yet. But she raised you. And she taught you to read surfaces. And she knew what was in the box. And she left it to you anyway." He closed his eyes for a moment. "She was not a fool, your grandmother. She chose her witness."

Kirin put her hands flat on the desk to keep them still.

"I want to see the coordinates again," she said.

Perun turned the screen.

* * *

Tomas's workshop was in a converted tram barn on the south edge of the Guild district — a long brick building with clerestory windows that let in the kind of north light that woodworkers lied about needing when what they really meant was that they loved it. Four Guild carpenters shared the space. Tomas's bench was at the end closest to the big door, which he had chosen when the collective had drawn for positions because the door meant air movement and air movement meant faster drying for the hide glue he still used, and also because he liked watching the street while he planed.

Kirin had been coming here since they were eighteen, when Tomas had finished his first apprenticeship and moved into the collective as a junior. She had watched him grow into the work the way you watch a tree grow — slowly enough that the change was invisible on any given day, and then you looked up one afternoon and realized he was a different person than he had been, that his shoulders had reshaped themselves around the work, that his hands had stopped apologizing for their size. He was now thirty. His hair was pulled back with a leather cord. He was planing a length of white oak, taking shavings off the edge in long curls that smelled like the inside of a library and a kitchen at the same time.

He did not look up when she came in. He had heard her footsteps on the concrete floor and he knew them — she walked the same way as she had at fourteen, slightly pigeon-toed, her steps evenly spaced, a rhythm he would have recognized from across a warehouse.

"I finished the queue," she said. "Brigid gave me the leave."

"Good." He pushed the plane through a final stroke. The shaving curled up between his knuckles and hung there for a moment and then dropped onto the pile at his feet. He set the plane down on its side the way good carpenters do, not flat, because flat dulls the blade. "How long did Brigid take to say yes."

"Maybe a minute."

"Of course." He ran his thumb along the edge of the board, reading the surface the way he had taught her to read it when she was fifteen and thinking about becoming a carpenter herself, before the materials analyst track had pulled her in a different direction. "Brigid is the best supervisor in that building. I've been saying that for years. She's going to hate losing you."

"I'm not —"

"Kirin. Come on. You're losing you."

She did not argue. She walked over to the bench and sat on the stool he kept there for visitors — her stool, by now, shaped to her in ways he had never adjusted for anyone else. The workshop smelled like oak and linseed oil and the faint coppery note of the brazing station in the back, where someone in the collective was joining metal for an inlay.

"Perun told me her name," she said. "Her real one. Adaeze Okonkwo."

"Okonkwo." Tomas tasted it. "That's a good name."

"He was one of the witnesses. On a Rootstock registration from 2078. He signed his name next to hers when they were both twenty-something, and then they both came into the Compact and lived in the same city for thirty-three years without knowing it." She looked at the oak board. "I don't know what to do with that."

"You don't have to do anything with it. That's the thing about other people's lives. Sometimes they just are, and your job is to notice." He picked up a cloth and wiped the blade of the plane. "What do you need from me."

"A route. Supplies. The practical things. You said you'd read about the empty zones."

"I've read a lot about the empty zones." He set the plane on a shelf above his bench, next to a rack of chisels that were arranged by width with the obsessive precision of a man who believed that a messy bench was a disrespect to the tools. "I have a data core. I'll copy the relevant sections to a portable reader and give it to you tonight. Infrastructure maps, autonomous system status where it's known, water table reports from the last civilian survey — which was in 2099, so treat it as a starting point and not a guarantee. You'll want to filter everything, even what looks clean. The pipe networks are fine. The reservoirs are a coin flip. Trust nothing you haven't run through a filter at least once."

"I'll remember."

"There's also a community ledger — informal, not Compact — that tracks the scavenger settlements in the zones east of here. I don't have a copy. But Perun does. Ask him. Tell him I told you to ask. He might pretend not to know what you mean, and then he'll give it to you."

She looked at him. "How do you know that."

"Because Perun lent me the original data core on infrastructure maintenance. I told you about it. There are people in Sable River who know things about the empty zones, and most of them are in the Archive or the Guild halls. We know each other. Not socially. Just — we know." He smiled a small smile, the one he used when he was admitting to a thing he had not planned to admit. "You're not the first person to ask these questions. You're just the first person I know personally."

"Who else asks."

"People whose grandmothers leave them boxes. It happens more than you think." He leaned against the bench. The light through the clerestory windows had moved — the sun was higher now, and the long stripes of brightness on the concrete floor were shorter than they had been when she came in. "Kirin. I want to say something to you, and I want you to not argue with me about it."

"Okay."

"I am going to worry about you every day you are gone. I am going to think about you the way I think about a piece of wood drying in a kiln — trying to calculate whether the conditions are right, not being able to check, going crazy about it. This is not a reason to not go. This is just information. So that when you come back — and you are coming back, because that is the protocol — you will understand why I am going to be angry at you for the first six hours before I am relieved."

She laughed. It was a small wet laugh that she had been carrying in her throat since Brigid's desk.

"I understand."

"Good. Now come look at this joint. I'm worried about the fit."

She stood up and came to the bench and bent over the oak board with him, and he showed her the dovetail he was shaping for a blanket chest commissioned by a Commons family in the south district. The pins were thin — thinner than a production carpenter would have cut them, because thin pins were harder but they looked better, and the family had not asked for them but Tomas had made them anyway, because this was his way of being in the world.

"It's perfect," she said.

"It's close. It's not perfect." He squinted at it. "But it's honest. I can live with honest."

Kirin stood next to her oldest friend in a shop full of wood shavings and the smell of oil and watched him decide not to re-cut a joint that was nearly perfect, and she thought: *this is one of the things I am leaving.* And then she thought: *he will still be here when I get back.* And then she thought, because she had inherited Adaeze's severity of mind along with her softness: *if I get back.*

But she did not say it.

* * *

Lian Mwangi's apartment was on the fifth floor of a Keeper-class building in the north residential district. Kirin climbed the stairs through the smell of onions and turmeric and something with cardamom in it, and by the second landing she was walking slower, because she was trying to remember the sound of the stairwell.

Lian opened the door in an apron and pulled her into a hug that smelled like the cooking.

"You're early. Which is perfect because Benno is pretending he knows how to make the rice."

"I heard that," Benno called from the kitchen.

The living room was four steps from the door. Benno was on the floor with their eighteen-month-old son Isa, who was pushing a wooden truck across the rug with the seriousness of a man laying foundation. On the couch: Rufus, a Commons teacher Kirin had known since secondary school, and a woman Kirin did not know — Amira, Rufus's partner, introduced as a wind-turbine specialist from the Guild house on the east side.

Kirin sat with a glass of Lian's dark fruit wine and tried to pay attention. The conversation was about the new Commons clinic on the west side — whether the building committee had picked the right parcel, which was the kind of conversation Sable River people had the way other cities talked about weather. Amira had several opinions. She was practical and articulate and slightly dry, and Kirin liked her immediately.

They moved to the table. Lian brought out roasted carrots with black sesame, grilled squash, flatbreads warm from the oven. The rice was, as predicted, slightly too wet. Nobody mentioned it.

It was during the main course that Amira said it.

She had been telling a story about a repair she'd done last month at the far eastern reach of the Compact's territory. A scavenger community had shown up to watch her work — reasonable people, she said, guarded but reasonable. On the way home she'd had to route around a Chinese-state substation that wasn't on any Compact map and wasn't supposed to be there, and had politely not been there for as long as anyone could remember.

"I've always wondered," Lian said, passing the salad, "why anyone would live out there. What's the point. You're outside the Ledger. You're outside the Commons. You're basically choosing to not have a society."

"They have a society," Amira said. "It's just not ours."

"But there's nothing out there. It's empty."

"The empty zones are empty for a reason," Rufus said, and reached for his wine.

And everyone nodded.

It was the nod that did it. Kirin watched them — her friends, reasonable and kind and intelligent people she had known for most of her adult life — nod at a sentence that was not an argument but a door closing. *Empty for a reason.* She had said it herself, many times. She had said it without noticing that she was saying it. The Compact's idea of what was worth looking at had become so total that the unlooked-at places had stopped being places and started being *reasons.* A reason was a thing you used to not think about something.

She put her fork down.

"I'm going to go out there," she said.

The table went quiet the way tables do when someone says a thing the other people have to decide how to respond to, fast.

"Out where," Lian said.

"Into the depopulated zone. My grandmother left me something. I'll be gone at least a month. Maybe more."

"You're not serious," Benno said.

"I am."

Amira was watching her now with a different kind of attention — not the attention of a dinner-party friend, but the attention of a person who had lived at the edge of the map and knew what it cost to go past it. She did not speak yet. She was thinking.

"Alone?" Rufus asked.

"Tomas is helping me with supplies. I'll find a guide at the frontier."

Lian reached across the table and put her hand over Kirin's.

"Okay," Lian said.

"Okay?"

"Okay. You go. We'll be here. Isa will learn to say your name properly by the time you come back — he's still doing *Kee-rin* with a gap in the middle — and Benno will have burned down the kitchen twice, and the clinic thing will still be going nowhere, and Sable River will be Sable River. This is the deal with home. It stays."

Amira, finally: "When do you leave."

"Day after tomorrow."

"Come see me before you go. I have things. A water filter better than anything Tomas will get you. Solar cells. A point-to-point comm that still works outside the network. I have two. I don't need two."

"I can't —"

"You can. This is what happens at the edge of the map. People hand you things. I lived in the eastern reach for three years. Come for breakfast. I have some ideas about what you should not do out there."

Kirin blinked. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. Wait until I've finished lecturing you."

They finished the meal. The conversation moved around her carefully, the way you move around a thing that has become slightly fragile. At the door Lian held her for a long moment and said, *You come back,* and Kirin said, *I will,* and meant it as much as she was able to.

She walked home through streets that were amber-lit and half-empty. She thought about the nod. She thought about Amira, who had kept a second comm for years because some part of her had known somebody might need it. She thought about Isa pushing his truck across the rug, certain of his work.

The Compact was a good place. A genuinely good place. Full of people who loved each other and built things and did the right thing by instinct.

And it was a place that was sure the reason was a reason.

She could love it and still go.

* * *

The old-timers' social club was on Quarry Street, in a building that had been a pharmacy before it had been a stationer's before it had been a community space, and the sign above the door said simply *The Bench* in hand-painted letters that had been refreshed every five years or so by whichever member of the club still had a steady hand. Kirin had walked past it many times without paying attention. It was the kind of place she had assumed was for other people — older people, retired people, people whose relationship to their own time was different from hers.

She pushed the door open.

The room inside was long and low-ceilinged and full of a particular kind of light — lamplit, even in the afternoon, because the front windows were small and the club's members liked it that way. Tables were arranged around the walls, and in the center was a larger communal table covered in a rotation of board games — a chess set with pieces worn smooth by forty years of hands, a sprawling strategy game with wooden tiles, a backgammon board with the leather cup still shiny. The air smelled like tea and pipe tobacco, which was rare in the Compact — pipe tobacco was a Free Zone import, and most people could not afford it. Someone here could.

There were maybe twelve people in the room. They were all old. Their average age, Kirin guessed without wanting to guess, was north of eighty. They turned and looked at her when she came in, not unfriendly, just curious — a young woman in an analyst's jacket was not what they were used to seeing on a Wednesday afternoon.

"I'm looking for someone who remembers the transition," Kirin said, because she had decided on the walk over that she would be direct.

A man at a corner table laughed. "We all remember the transition. We're all the transition. Come in."

She came in. She walked past the chess set and the game table and sat down at the corner man's table, which was where the corner man had waved her to. He was thin and small and wore a sweater the color of old moss, and his hands were folded around a cup of tea that steamed gently.

"I'm Kirin."

"I'm Olu. This is Reva." He gestured at the woman across the table from him, who Kirin had not noticed until that moment, because Reva was very still. Reva was very old. Reva had skin the color of good walnut and a white cloud of hair and eyes that were the sharpest eyes Kirin had ever seen on a person who looked that frail. Reva was drinking something that was not tea. Reva was looking at Kirin the way Perun had looked at the key on his desk.

"Hello, Reva," Kirin said.

"Hello, daughter." Reva's voice was low and rough and carried a slight accent Kirin could not place. "What are you looking for."

"I — my grandmother died three weeks ago. I found something in her apartment. A pre-Compact artifact from a group called Rootstock. I've been told that some of the Rootstock communities didn't come into the Compact. I'm trying to find someone who remembers the ones that didn't."

Reva did not react immediately. She looked at Kirin for a long time. Olu, the man in the moss-colored sweater, made a small gesture with his cup — not quite a toast, more like an acknowledgment — and then stood up and said, "I am going to play some chess. You two will talk."

He left. It was done with a grace that suggested he had done it many times before, for many different conversations, and the club was the kind of place where people stood up and went to play chess when other people needed privacy.

Reva set down her glass.

"What was your grandmother's name."

Kirin hesitated. She was not sure how much to give. And then she decided: all of it.

"Adaeze Saleh. Before that, Adaeze Okonkwo."

Reva closed her eyes. She did not open them for a long moment.

When she opened them, they were wet.

"I knew her," Reva said. "A very long time ago. We were in a settlement together — not for long, only a season, but we were there. I was nineteen. She was a few years older. She was teaching me to keep a ledger."

Kirin felt the floor of her own life shift slightly, the way a ship's deck shifts in swell that has been building for a while and is only now reaching you.

"What kind of ledger."

"A good one." Reva smiled — a small private smile, for herself and not for Kirin. "The Rootstock kind. The kind where every entry is a thing you can hold. She had hands like steady weather. Nobody could forge an entry under her hands because the entry would know it was being forged. I learned a lot from her." She reached across the table and took Kirin's hand in both of hers. Her grip was unexpected — firm, warm, dry. "You have her hands, I can feel it. You are an analyst?"

"Materials."

"Of course. The thing she was always best at. Of course her granddaughter would go into surfaces." Reva squeezed Kirin's hand once and let go. "I am not going to tell you very much. Not because I do not want to. Because I do not know very much. I was in that settlement for one season in 2081, as a visitor from another place, and then I left, and I do not know what happened to her or to them. I came into the Compact in 2094 like most of us. I have been in Sable River for fifteen years."

"You knew she was here."

"I suspected. I saw her once, across a crowded plaza, at a harvest festival maybe eight years ago. I was not sure. I thought: *that could be her, or it could be a woman who reminds me of her.* I did not approach. If she had wanted to be found, she would have been findable. That is a thing we learned, those of us who came in. You do not expose a choice."

Kirin swallowed. "Do you know the name of the settlement."

"I knew it then. I have forgotten." Reva's eyes moved to the ceiling as if the name might be written there. "It was one of the plateau settlements. It was a mixed community — East African, West African, some Americans, a few people from further. They wrote in Tamsat and spoke three other languages. The founder was a woman whose name I remember because it was strange — *Nnedi,* I think, which is a version of the Igbo word for *mother.* That is all. Four hundred miles east of here, you said."

"The coordinates point there, yes."

"Then it is the plateau settlement, or its remains, or its children." Reva was quiet for a moment. "I will tell you one thing, daughter. If you go there, do not go expecting to find her. Expect to find what she left. There is a difference. You will find it because you are looking for her, but the thing you find will not be her. Do not be disappointed when it is not."

"I'm not expecting her."

"Yes you are." Reva smiled again — wider this time, warmer, the smile of a woman who had outlived every one of her mistakes and had found the warmth on the other side. "But that is fine. That is how you find the thing that is there. You go looking for something else."

* * *

Kirin walked home from The Bench through the long slow light of a Sable River late afternoon. The streets were still busy — apprentices heading to their evening shifts, a mother with two children arguing about who got to carry the bread, an old man walking a dog that was not as old as he was but was trying. The city went on. It did not know she was leaving. That was important, somehow — that it did not know, and would not notice for a while, and would be fine.

She passed the elder-care facility on the west end of the civic district, where Adaeze had worked for twenty years. The ground-floor windows were full of the soft evening light, and through them she could see the residents at their long supper table and the Ghost-class aides moving between them with practiced gentleness, serving a stew that steamed in the cooling air. Kirin stopped for a moment and watched. She thought about Reva's words — *she had hands like steady weather* — and she pictured her grandmother in this room, night after night for two decades, holding the hands of people who were forgetting their own names, singing to them softly in a language nobody else in Sable River recognized because it was not a language anybody in Sable River was looking for.

A woman came out of the side door of the facility, a Ghost-class aide in the pale blue smock of the night shift, and she saw Kirin standing on the pavement and paused.

"You're Adaeze Saleh's granddaughter."

"Yes."

"I thought so. You have her forehead." The aide was in her fifties, maybe. Her name tag read *Mira.* "We miss her. It's been three weeks and every night I still expect to see her at the long table. She would sit with Efe in the last bed by the window. She'd sing him something. He stopped being able to talk a year ago, but when she sang he would close his eyes and his mouth would move along with the song. The song was in a language none of us knew. I asked her once what it meant. She said it meant *the road is long, but there is water at the end of it.* I think about that almost every day."

Kirin did not trust her voice. She nodded.

Mira looked at her for another moment and then said, "He stopped eating the day after she died. We were not surprised. Sometimes people wait for each other. I hope that is not a strange thing to tell you. I thought you should know."

"Thank you," Kirin said. "That isn't strange."

Mira went back inside. The door closed softly behind her.

Kirin stood on the pavement. Through the window, the aides were bringing out a second pot of something, and the old people at the long table were bending toward it the way plants bend toward light. She put her hand against her chest and felt the key warm through the cloth.

*The road is long, but there is water at the end of it.*

She thought about hands like steady weather. She thought about a woman who had written ledger entries no one could forge, and then had come to a quiet city and spent thirty-five years writing ledger entries no one would think to audit, in the bodies of dying strangers. She thought about the garden window, and the seven meals in rotation, and the box at the back of the linen closet where the towels had been.

*Be patient,* Kirin thought, and did not finish the sentence, because the sentence had already finished itself.

She walked the rest of the way home through the gathering evening.

* * *

She packed the way she did everything. Methodically. Top down. A list in her head, a hand for each item, the slow steady rhythm of a person who had spent her professional life cataloging things that had to be true. The backpack Tomas had brought her an hour before — Guild canvas, reinforced at the seams, the kind of thing he made for people who meant to come back. The water filter from Amira. The solar cells and the point-to-point comm, wrapped separately. The data core with the infrastructure maps. A change of clothes, practical and unremarkable. A fire-starter, a knife, a small cook pot, dried food for ten days. A paper notebook and two pens — she did not yet know why she wanted paper, but she did. The letters, in a waxed envelope inside the waxed pouch Perun had given her when she had stopped at the Archive one final time at dusk and collected the ones he had translated for her, his voice rough as he read, his hands steadier than his voice.

The photograph she carried in the inner jacket pocket with the key.

When the bag was packed she sat at her kitchen table and wrote a short note to Brigid, and a shorter note to Lian, and a note to Tomas that was only three words. She put them in the building's mail slot. She did not sleep. She cleaned the apartment — wiped the counters, washed the single cup, put the letters' waxed pouch on the table where she would pick it up last. She watched the slow rise of the dawn through her eastern window, and when the first pale edge of it touched the sill she stood up and shouldered the bag and went out.

She stopped at Adaeze's apartment one last time. The building was still asleep. The key she had carried since she was twelve let her in without a sound. The rooms were empty now — the furniture remaining, the garden window bare, the specific silence of a place that had stopped being a life and had not yet been reassigned. She walked through it slowly. She stood in the garden window for a moment and looked at the spot where Adaeze's kitchen chair had sat, and she said the name *Adaeze Okonkwo* out loud, softly, as a kind of greeting.

Then she took the authentication key out of her jacket pocket and held it up in the first true light of the morning. Its warmth was steady against her palm — not rising, not fading, just present, like a heartbeat at the other end of a road.

She put it back in her pocket. She walked to the door. She turned once at the threshold and looked back at the small rooms where her grandmother had kept the watch for thirty-five years, and she said, quietly, *I have it now.*

Then she closed the door behind her and went east.
