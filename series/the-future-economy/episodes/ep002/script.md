# Episode 2: "What She Left Behind"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin investigates her grandmother's vanished history, asks the wrong questions in the right rooms, and makes peace with a life she's about to leave behind.*

---

Tomas worked the dovetail by feel, the way he'd learned to work everything by feel in the fourteen years since certification. Cherry smelled like almonds and autumn. It always did. He'd been Guild-certified since twenty-one and the smell of fresh cherry in the morning still produced in him a low-frequency pleasure he didn't have a name for, which was fine — he had work instead.

The commission was a bookcase. Three bays, floor-to-ceiling, fitted to an alcove in a Founder family's study in the north district. Precision job. The walls weren't plumb — they never were in a house that old — and the client had specified traditional joinery, no adhesives, wood-to-wood only, because that was what Founders specified when they wanted to signal they understood what they were paying for. Tomas didn't mind. He liked traditional joinery. The joint either fit or it didn't.

This one didn't fit.

He set down the chisel and looked at it. A quarter millimeter of gap on the inside shoulder — fine for function, maybe invisible to the eye, but he could feel the slight give when he test-fitted the piece, and it didn't belong. He'd cut it wrong because he'd been thinking about Kirin, and now he needed to start over from a new board, because you didn't patch a dovetail and call it honest work. The other thing about traditional joinery: it remembered its mistakes.

He pulled out a fresh board and set it in the vise.

His workshop was in the Guild district, first floor of what had been an elementary school before the demographic collapse. The rooms were just right for individual workshops — twenty-foot square, high north-facing windows that gave good diffuse light. Forty artisans in this building. Quiet in the mornings, accumulating noise through the day: mallets on chisels, the whir of a lathe two rooms over, occasional exchanges through partition walls about problems that required a second opinion. Kirin had visited this workshop so many times the potter down the hall called her "your analyst." Tomas didn't correct him.

He wondered if she was already at the Archive.

She'd sounded clear-headed on the phone last night. She always did. Kirin's voice stayed level when she was upset — most people mistook it for calm, but Tomas had known her since they were fourteen, sharing a bench in Foundation Studies, and he knew the difference between her calm voice and her level voice. Last night had been very level.

He started the new cut.

He believed her — that wasn't in question. Kirin didn't invent things. She reported what she found, with the specific flat certainty of a person who trusted evidence over interpretation. He believed the Archive assessment too, and Perun's reaction, and the coordinates. What he believed that Kirin hadn't said aloud was this: she was going east, and nothing he said would change it, and the correct question wasn't whether but what she needed.

He'd spent an hour last night with the infrastructure records. The depopulated zone east of the frontier was AI-maintained — Compact systems, running on original parameters from the 2090s, nobody had updated them in thirty years. If you knew how they worked, you could use them. If you didn't, you were trusting machines that were running smoothly and doing something completely different from what you assumed. He'd printed a route summary. He had a water filter he'd bought for a fieldwork project years ago and never used. He was putting together what she'd need, quietly, because she'd fight him if he offered and she'd accept once the offer was made.

He cut the new joint.

This one was right.

* * *

The workshop door opened at half past seven, before most of the other artisans arrived. Tomas recognized the footstep — a specific rhythm, measured but not slow, the walk of someone thinking about something other than walking.

"You didn't sleep," he said.

"I slept a little."

"How long is a little."

"Longer than nothing." She set her bag on the clear end of his bench and looked at the cherry boards. "What's this for?"

"Bookcase. North district Founder. Traditional joinery, all the way."

"Of course." The reflex, without weight. She picked up a board and ran her thumb along the grain. She always did this with materials that weren't her job.

"I went back to Adaeze's apartment this morning," she said. "Before I came here. I'd already cleared everything out. I just wanted to look at the space."

"And?"

"The light was right. The evening light comes in at a particular angle. She picked the apartment for that angle. Thirty-five years and I never asked her why she chose it."

Tomas made a sound that wasn't words but served the purpose.

"I need a route," she said.

"I know."

"I need to know what's passable east of the frontier."

"I pulled the infrastructure records last night." He reached for the printed summary on his tool shelf. "Main highway east-northeast. The AI maintenance systems have kept the primary arteries clear — road surface passable for foot or light vehicle. Transit stations at roughly hundred-mile intervals. Pre-Compact facilities, all automated. Power's on. Water systems running — not verified clean, which is why I've got a filter for you."

She looked at him.

"I was going to argue," she said.

"I know. Don't."

She didn't.

"The route summary covers the first two hundred miles with reasonable confidence," he continued. "After that, the maintenance grid changes. The systems are still active past two hundred but I don't have specific documentation on their parameters out there. You'll have to read what they're doing and trust your observations more than any map."

"That's all right." She looked at the printout. "I'm good at reading what things are doing."

"Different grammar out there."

"I know." She set the printout down. "I went back to the Archive before I came here. Perun had been working since before I left yesterday."

"What did he find?"

She told him. The Rootstock Registry. Forty-six communities registered in 2078. Thirty-nine with dissolution records — absorbed, or voluntarily wound down. Seven with no disposition record at all. And the coordinates corresponding to one of the seven. And the fragment of a name, partial, A. Something, recorded as community coordinator in 2061.

Tomas listened the way he worked: without interrupting, giving the information room. When she finished he was quiet for a moment.

"Are you going to tell Brigid where you're actually going?" he said.

"I'll say east, to investigate an estate matter. Which is true."

"How long are you asking for?"

"Four weeks." She paused. "I'm coming back, Tomas."

"I know you are." He went back to the vise, fitting the next piece. "Come Thursday. I'll have gear ready."

* * *

The Archive was already open when Kirin arrived at nine. Perun was at his desk, which she suspected was where he'd been since before she'd left. A printout lay across his workstation — densely formatted, the pre-Compact data she was starting to recognize, but more of it than yesterday. Spread across every flat surface in the small room in the careful non-random arrangement of a man reading across multiple documents and tracking intersections.

"The Rootstock Registry," he said, without looking up. "2078. Community registration records from the Sorting period." He turned a printout toward her. "Forty-six registered communities in this region as of that year. I've read about sixty percent of the total. The degradation is uneven — better in some sections."

Kirin sat down. The Archive's morning light was gray and even, falling through frosted glass onto shelves of things nobody asked for. The smell was specific: old media, environmental control, a faint metallic undertone of equipment running at steady state.

"How many can you account for?" she asked.

"Thirty-nine have disposition records. Most were absorbed into the Compact formally — deaccession documents showing the transition, equity transfer, member registration onto the Ledger. Six dissolved voluntarily before formalization. Seven have no record at all."

"And the coordinates."

"Consistent with the registered location of one of the seven." He pointed to an entry on the most legible page. "I can read about forty percent of this one. Founding date 2061 — which makes it one of the older communities, built almost thirty years before the Compact. Mixed-use settlement. Agricultural and manufacturing. An external relay node of some kind." He moved his finger to the signature line. "The coordinator's signature. Most of it corrupted. What's legible: A. Something. A name beginning with A."

Kirin looked at the fragment.

"In 2078," Perun said, "your grandmother would have been in her early forties. Old enough to have been present from the beginning. Old enough to be a founding-generation coordinator." He paused. "I can't confirm it's her. I can say the connection is consistent."

"What was the equity claim?"

"Substantial. Not a single property — a portfolio. Agricultural land, built infrastructure, productive capacity. The registration scale suggests a community that had been productive for a long time. The Rootstock Protocol calculated equity differently from EQU — I can't translate the values directly. But the scale is not trivial."

"Meaning there's something there."

"Meaning there was something registered as there in 2078. Whether it persists — I genuinely can't say. What I can say is that the key is real, the registration is valid, and no dissolution was ever recorded." He leaned back. "There are no dissolution records for those seven communities. Most Rootstock communities either formally dissolved or were absorbed, and there are records for both. Seven simply stop appearing. Not erased — just no longer tracked by any system with a reason to look."

Kirin looked at the printouts. Forty-six communities. Thirty-nine accounted for. Seven that had slipped below the waterline of every monitoring system and, apparently, kept going.

"The Compact's AI monitors Compact assets," she said. It wasn't a question.

"The Compact's AI monitors what it was built to monitor. What it wasn't built to see —" He gestured at the Archive around him. "Doesn't cease to exist."

She sat with this. Adaeze arriving in Sable River in 2089, one year before the Compact formalized in this territory. Building a small careful life. Keeping the key in a box for fifty years. Knowing, presumably, that the community it pointed to was still there.

"She knew," Kirin said.

"I think she kept it as a record," Perun said. "An acknowledgment. Whether she ever intended anyone to use it is something I can't know." He looked at her. "Go east. Find the ground truth. That's what the key was made for."

* * *

Brigid was at her desk when Kirin returned to the Board at half past eleven. She noted the return without comment and went back to her document. This was Brigid's way — she catalogued things without producing an event from them.

Kirin worked through the afternoon queue: a provenance dispute on structural timber, a new apprentice submission, two standard materials assessments. The apprentice submission was careful work, slightly over-polished — extra passes on everything, hiding uncertainty about joins that were actually fine. The instinct to be sure was good. Kirin noted it and moved on.

At two she filed the leave request. Four weeks, bereavement and discretionary. She copied Brigid and Hasse and left detailed notes in the queue management system. More detailed than strictly necessary, probably — but the notes were also a way of saying goodbye to each piece of work, and she didn't have a more honest way to do that.

Brigid stopped by at half past two.

"Leave approved," she said. "Estate business."

"Yes."

"How far east?"

Kirin considered. "Far enough to need four weeks."

Brigid looked at her with the specific quality of attention she brought to materials she suspected weren't what they claimed to be.

"Kirin. Have you read the historiography of the Sorting period? Pre-Compact communities in the depopulated zone?"

The question surprised her. "Some. Through the Archive."

"The gap in the historical record is known." Brigid said it evenly. "It's not a secret. It's just not relevant to our operations. The Compact's account of itself focuses on what came in during the Sorting, not what didn't." She looked at the queue display for a moment. "If what didn't come in turns out to be relevant — someone would need to do the work of establishing that. Documentation. Verified provenance. Real evidence." She looked back at Kirin. "Come back with something real. That's the only way it matters."

She moved on. Kirin watched her go. Brigid was a Foundation Assessor, trained to verify that what claimed to be real was real. She'd just told Kirin exactly what she'd need to bring home. As clearly as Brigid ever told anyone anything.

* * *

The dinner was at Roshan's apartment in the north district — the large one he'd earned two years ago when he completed his third Guild domain. Roshan had done it on purpose and on schedule. Three-domain certification meant Founder status meant a better housing allocation, and Roshan was the kind of person who planned things eight years out and felt gratified, not smug, when they arrived as planned. He was a structural engineer who approached his own life the way he approached load-bearing calculations: from first principles, with appropriate safety factors, correctly.

Seven of them. Roshan and his partner Hira, who coordinated regional food production. Jessa, a Commons secondary teacher with the specific energetic warmth of someone who spent her days in rooms full of fourteen-year-olds and had developed a talent for making everyone feel welcome. Preet and Calla, civil engineers, who had met as Guild apprentices and been inseparable since in the way of people who found in each other the combination of intelligence and reliability they'd been looking for without knowing that was what they wanted. And Dav, Roshan's neighbor, who had first shown up at one of these dinners as a casual guest, and then reappeared, and then became a fixture the way certain pieces of furniture do — through persistence and the absence of objection.

Kirin had been coming to these dinners for three years.

The food was good. Hira had cooked, which meant real thought in it — a spice blend from a Trade Board exchange that gave the rice a depth nobody could quite name. Hira wouldn't explain it. She found the mystery more interesting than the answer. Local wine. Honey from Foundry Street that someone had brought as an afterthought and turned out to be the best thing on the table.

The conversation moved the way these conversations moved: work, Guild politics, a debate about the Commons school curriculum. Jessa said the history section still taught the Compact as if it were inevitable. Three weeks on the transition period, fifteen on Compact institutions. "The students think it appeared from nothing," she said. Roshan said three weeks was enough for an economics survey. Kirin said it should include what didn't happen, not just what did, and Jessa gave her a grateful look, and the conversation moved on.

Preet and Calla were midway through a remediation project on the east district's water main infrastructure — pre-Compact pipes, original spec, in better shape than expected. "The autonomous systems have been doing the maintenance," Calla said. "Nobody told them to stop, so they didn't." This turned the conversation briefly to the depopulated zones and the strange fact of infrastructure maintained for no one — roads in good repair for empty territory, lights still working in abandoned schools. Everyone found this mildly remarkable and nobody pursued it further.

Adaeze came up over dessert. Of course she did — Kirin was three weeks out from the death, these were her people, and they were trying to give her space to bring it up. She brought it up. They said the right things. Jessa said she'd once seen Adaeze speak at a Commons event, local history, transition era, very calm about it, specific in a way that made you feel like you were hearing something true rather than something processed. Kirin said yes. She had been like that. She ate her honey and thought about a fragment of a name in a document from 2078.

"I'm taking leave," she said. "Four weeks. Estate matters — property questions from Adaeze's side that need to be checked in person."

"How far?" Preet asked.

"East. Into the depopulated zone. About four hundred miles."

A pause around the table. Mild surprise — the kind you get when someone says they're going somewhere vaguely inconvenient.

"That's a long way for estate business," Roshan said.

"The property is old. Pre-Compact registration. It doesn't resolve from here."

"The empty zones." Dav reached for the wine. "What's out there, really? I've always been vaguely curious — not curious enough to go, obviously, but curious." He smiled at this. The comfortable smile of a man for whom curiosity was a form of leisure. "I mean — the empty zones are empty for a reason, right? Everything people needed was here, or in the Free Zones. The land is just land."

Around the table: small nods. Roshan, who knew his structural loads. Hira, who thought carefully about agricultural systems. Jessa, who taught children how the world worked. Preet and Calla, who kept old pipes running correctly. Good people. Thoughtful people, most of them. Nodding.

The empty zones were empty for a reason.

"Maybe," Kirin said.

She ate the last of her honey. She looked at her friends — their easy certainty, the particular warmth of people who were right where they were supposed to be. The Compact was good at producing this feeling. It was one of the things it did well: gave you a world that made sense, a set of values that held together, a daily life that was genuinely worth living. You didn't end up needing to look outside it because inside it there was enough. Why would you go east? There was no reason to. No CapEx posting, no Guild project, no Commons need that pointed that direction. The empty zones were empty for a reason and the reason was that there was nothing out there worth going for.

She helped clean up. She said her goodbyes. She walked home through a night that smelled like the river, and she thought about what it would take to ask the question that nobody at that table had thought to ask. Not because they were incurious, exactly. Because curiosity required a gap in the current account of things, and the Compact's account of things was very nearly complete.

The empty zones. Empty for a reason.

But whose reason? And when?

* * *

The old-timers' social club met Tuesday mornings in a room at the south Commons center. Perun had mentioned it as a place where people who'd lived through the transition sometimes gathered. The room had metal folding chairs in a rough circle, institutional lighting, and the particular atmospheric density of a space where the same group of people had been meeting for a long time. Coffee. Handcream. The warmth of bodies that had been doing this for years.

Kirin arrived at nine. She was the youngest person in the room by forty years.

Fifteen or so. She introduced herself as Adaeze Saleh's granddaughter, trying to understand her grandmother's history before the Compact — specifically anything related to communities in the depopulated zones during the transition. Most of the group didn't know what she was asking about, or knew it abstractly. A man named Gerhard spoke at length about Guild formation in the early Compact years — interesting but not relevant. A woman in her eighties remembered her parents' involvement in an equity trial in the western district, nothing about the east. Two people thought they'd heard the word Rootstock but couldn't say from where or in what context.

The woman by the window had been quiet since Kirin came in. Small, white hair cropped close to her head, hands folded in her lap. She hadn't spoken during Kirin's question and hadn't nodded along with the others when they gave well-meaning but useless answers. She'd watched, with the specific quality of attention Kirin was starting to associate with people who had been around when things actually happened — the stillness of someone letting information arrive without hurrying it.

After the meeting broke up, this woman touched Kirin's arm.

"Come back tomorrow morning," she said. "I'm in the east building, third floor. My name is Vashti."

That was all.

* * *

Vashti's apartment smelled like dried herbs and old paper and something else — a warm resinous spice Kirin couldn't identify. Every surface was in use: stacked reference materials, handwritten notes in several different handwritings, a growing setup on the window ledge with plants that were clearly functional rather than decorative. Dark-leafed, unfamiliar, carrying a faint medicinal quality.

Vashti moved with the deliberateness of someone who had stopped wasting motion in her sixties and found it suited her. She poured tea from a clay pot, did not apologize for the apartment, and sat across from Kirin with her hands wrapped around her cup.

"Your grandmother," she said. "Adaeze Saleh."

"Yes."

"And before Saleh. Before the Compact name."

"I don't know what it was. There's no birth record before 2091."

Vashti nodded. "The community people who came in during the Sorting — some gave themselves Compact names. Married people with Compact surnames. Changed their documentation at registration. The Ledger needed a name it could classify. Their existing identities didn't fit the categories." She paused. "It was practical, not shameful. There was a lot of that during the transition — people arriving with histories the new system had no forms for, finding ways to make themselves legible."

"You knew people who did this?"

"I knew people who did many things during the Sorting. I was twenty-two in 2090. Free Zone-born, came to the Compact during formalization. I had my own reasons." She looked at her tea. "I knew the community people mostly from that period — they were deciding at the same time I was deciding. Different calculations, different conclusions. Some of them came in. Some didn't. The ones who didn't were already running something. The Compact was an option for them, not a necessity. That's a different position from the one most people were in."

"The Rootstock communities," Kirin said.

"Is that what the Archive calls them."

"It's what the protocol is called."

"We called them the builders." She said it without sentiment — just the word. "Not because of construction. Because they were building a system before the Compact had a name for what a system was. They started in the 2060s, in the chaos after the Wage Collapse, when the old economy had clearly failed and nobody was sure yet what came next. They were trying things. The Compact was also trying things, with more resources and more territory. The builders were smaller, further out, but they'd been at it longer."

"What were they building that was different?"

"They had the same foundation — real assets, verified value, physical work as the basis of equity. But they disagreed about the edges. What counted. The Compact defined value through the Ledger, which meant what you couldn't audit physically didn't count. The builders had a broader definition. Care, knowledge, cultural production, certain kinds of sustained innovation — they had ways of accounting for things the Ledger couldn't see." She looked at Kirin steadily. "They weren't wrong. They just weren't the ones who grew into the governing system. History belongs to whoever's still running the administration thirty years later."

Kirin brought out the photograph. She'd been carrying it in her bag since she found it. She put it on the table between them without saying anything.

Vashti looked at it. She looked at the building first — the curved walls, the roof following the contour of the land, the sense of something grown rather than placed. Then the landscape: dense and green and empty of any other structure to the horizon. Then the people, and the way they stood together. And then the woman in the center.

"Rammed earth construction," Vashti said. "Compressed stabilized adobe. They built low and curved for thermal mass — not as a style. An engineering choice. They were in zones without reliable energy infrastructure. The walls did the work."

"You've seen buildings like this."

"I've seen photographs." She traced the edge of the picture without touching the surface. "Is this who I think it is."

"My grandmother," Kirin said. "That's Adaeze, in the center."

Vashti was quiet for a moment. Something shifted in her expression — not surprise. Something settling into place after a long time in transit.

"I didn't know her personally," she said. "I knew of her. In 2090, among the people I knew who were connected to the communities — her name came up. Not her Compact name. A different name." She paused. "I won't say it. That's hers to give, not mine. But she was known. The person in this photograph was not a minor person in those communities."

"She spent thirty-five years as a quiet Commons health aide," Kirin said. "Two-room apartment. Seven meals on rotation. She didn't have overnight guests."

"Yes." Vashti said it with something like recognition. "That's what it takes to disappear into a new system. Not just a new name — a new scale. You have to become small and stay small, consistently, for years, until the small life is your actual life and not a performance." She picked up her tea. "Whether she succeeded at that, I couldn't say. But she clearly tried hard enough that you didn't know."

"What was she hiding from?"

"I don't know. I knew she existed. I didn't know her reasons." Vashti looked at the photograph again. "The child."

"I don't know who it is."

"No." She set the photograph down with care. "The language in the letters you mentioned — letters you couldn't read. If it's what I think, it's a constructed language. The builders developed it for internal communication in the early years, before they had encryption infrastructure. Something they could write in that wouldn't be legible outside. It's not in any Compact database because the Compact never had a reason to document it. I know of it. I can't read it."

"Would someone who's still out there know it?"

Vashti looked at her directly. "Almost certainly. If the communities are still there — and I believe some of them are — then the language is still in use. Probably evolved since the early days. But the root of it would be legible to anyone who grew up with it."

"Then I'll be able to get the letters translated."

"If you get out there. If they receive you." She paused. "They're not hostile. But they're not accustomed to visitors from inside the Compact. Your authentication key will help. It's a credential — it says your grandmother trusted you with this, which is a form of introduction." She thought for a moment. "Go as yourself. Don't pretend to be something you're not. They'll know the difference."

Kirin looked at the photograph between them. Adaeze, young and open, holding a child in front of a building that knew where it came from.

"Is there anything else I should know? Before I go?"

Vashti thought about it. Seriously, without performance. The same quality of attention she'd had in the Commons room yesterday — patient, not looking for a particular answer, willing to let the right one arrive.

"They've been building for sixty years," she said finally. "Whatever they've built — be patient with it. It doesn't move fast. It's not trying to impress you."

Be patient with what it asks of you.

"My grandmother wrote something similar," Kirin said.

"Your grandmother was a careful person." Vashti stood, carefully, signaling the end of the conversation. "Come back when you return. I want to know what you found."

Kirin walked out of the east building into the midmorning light. The Commons center was starting to fill with people — a noon meal queue forming at the main entrance, apprentices passing in groups, an elder-care worker she recognized from the west-district facility walking a resident in slow circles around the courtyard. The ordinary Tuesday of a city doing what it did.

She thought about what Vashti had said. Not just the information — the way she'd said it. The builders. Not the Rootstock people, not the pre-Compact independents, not the communities that didn't join. The builders. Said with the specific lack of judgment of someone who understood that what you called a thing shaped how you thought about it.

She thought about Dav at the dinner table, his comfortable incuriosity. The empty zones are empty for a reason. Said with the ease of someone who had never needed another answer. It wasn't stupidity. It was what the Compact produced in its best citizens: a complete enough account of the world that the gaps were invisible. You had to go looking for the edges to find them.

Adaeze had known the edges. She'd come from beyond them, spent thirty-five years inside the frame, and never once pointed at the edge and said: look. She'd put the key in a box and hidden it in a linen closet and left a note that said be patient with what it asks of you.

Kirin walked through the rest of the day — a Commons errands run, a final stop at the Archive to thank Perun and collect a copy of the Rootstock Registry fragment he'd made for her, printed on the same weatherproof paper Tomas used. The fragment joined the letters and the photograph in the cloth pouch. Evidence. Not complete, not enough to bring home to Brigid and call it real. But a start.

She went home and looked at the letters one more time. Twenty-three of them, laid out on her table. The constructed language, the script she couldn't read. She'd been carrying them for over a week and they were still closed to her, still just a beautiful surface with no way in.

She folded them carefully and put them in the pouch.

She'd have the translation before she came back. She'd decided this.

* * *

Kirin found Hasse in the assay room Thursday morning. He was running a spectrographic analysis on a floor tile, the comfortable posture of a person doing something they'd done a thousand times. He glanced up when she came in and did not seem surprised.

"I'm leaving Saturday," she said.

"Yes." He said it as if he'd expected this since the moment she walked in with the key. Which maybe he had. After forty-one years at the Board, Hasse had developed the evaluator's habit of saying nothing about things he'd already filed away.

He set the tile down and looked at her.

"The man who brought the other key," he said. "Forty years ago. I told you he didn't come back. I should be more precise." He folded his hands. "He didn't come back here. He went east, toward the coordinates the key pointed to. Six months later he sent a message — to his sister, not to the Board. She told me about it afterward. He said he'd decided to stay. He resigned his Board position. He gave up his Founder status, his Guild certifications, his Ledger holdings. He said he'd found something that made those things feel like instruments he no longer needed."

Kirin was quiet.

"His name was Davorin. Foundation Assessor, senior level. One of the best I knew. Not reckless. Not romantic. A careful man, very thorough, the kind who checked everything twice before concluding anything." He paused. "When a person like that gives up everything he's built and sends a message saying he found something worth it —" He picked up the tile again. "I'm not telling you this as a warning. I'm telling you it as evidence."

"Evidence of what?"

"That whatever is out there isn't nothing." He went back to the analysis. "Be careful. Come back."

She returned to her desk and cleared her queue for the day. She left notes on every open case — more detailed than strictly necessary. But the notes were a way of saying goodbye to each piece of work, and she didn't have a more honest way to do it.

* * *

Tomas had laid out the gear on his bench by Thursday afternoon. Organized the way he organized tools: by category and use, nothing excess, everything where it belonged.

A water filtration unit, compact, tested. A fold-flat thermal layer for cold nights. A solar collection film and power cell, modest but reliable. Three days of concentrated rations. The route summary, printed on weatherproof paper, with his handwriting in the margins — notes at each transit station, what the maintenance records suggested about water and power and shelter. Observations about which autonomous systems he'd found documentation on and which would require watching.

And a small piece of cherry wood.

Palm-sized, toggle-shaped. Flat on one end, curved on the other, drilled through the top for a cord. The grain ran lengthwise. The finish was so fine she could barely feel the transition from the wood to the air above it.

She picked it up.

"For the pack strap," he said. "The quick-release on the one you've got is worn. That fits the loop and holds better."

She turned it over in her hands. Almonds and autumn.

"This is from the bookcase boards."

"From the offcuts. There's always offcuts." He was looking at something on his bench, not at her.

"Tomas."

"The important thing is that it functions."

She put the toggle in her jacket pocket. He didn't watch her do it, being tactful in his way.

"I have questions about the transit stations," she said.

"Ask them."

She did. He answered all of them. The first station at a hundred miles was a pre-Compact maintenance hub — solar generation, water well, vehicle charging capacity, automated inventory of maintenance equipment. Whether there would be vehicles was likely, based on the hub's original function, but not confirmed. The second station at two hundred miles: he had a location, a power confirmation, and almost nothing else. Past two hundred miles his information became approximate.

"The AI systems in the depopulated zones," he said, "were built with adaptation parameters. Not fixed instructions. They've been adapting for sixty years. Most of what they do is still what they were built for. But they've been modifying their own routines based on what they've encountered. Nobody documented the modifications."

"You said novel," she said. "Earlier this week."

"Novel. The behavior is logical — it's just operating on a different set of observations than you have. Watch what the systems are doing. Don't assume you know what they were designed to do." He paused. "This is also good advice for meeting people."

She looked up from the route summary.

He almost smiled. "Go," he said. "Come back."

She hefted the pack. It sat right — the weight distributed well, which was his work, his understanding of how a body distributes load. She adjusted the strap and felt the cherry toggle settle into the loop. Solid. Precise. Something that knew where it came from.

"Every hundred miles," she said.

"Every hundred miles."

She walked out of his workshop into the afternoon. The Guild district was in its post-lunch rhythm, apprentices returning, afternoon production starting up. An ordinary Thursday. She walked through it toward home.

* * *

Packing was a decision-making exercise, which meant Kirin was good at it.

She spread everything on the floor and looked at it. The water filter. The thermal layer. The solar cell. The rations. The route summary. The authentication key, which didn't go in the pack — it went in the inner pocket of her jacket, where it had lived since the night she found it, against her ribs like a second heartbeat.

The letters and the photograph, wrapped in cloth, tucked against the pack's back panel.

Her portable assay kit: the field reader, three calibration standards, an analyzer the size of her palm. She held this for a moment. She was going somewhere the Ledger didn't reach and nobody was waiting for a verification report. But she was a materials analyst going to read surfaces she'd never read before, and arriving without her instruments felt like going in without her eyes. She packed it.

Clothes: two changes, practical. Boots worn in and trusted. A waterproof outer layer. The total was smaller than she'd expected. She'd been afraid of weight, had edited ruthlessly, and it turned out that what she actually needed didn't take up much room. What she'd packed from anxiety she put back on the shelf.

She walked the apartment once. The scuff by the door from the time she'd moved the kitchen table and miscalculated the clearance. The worn spot on the floor by the window where she stood when she was reading something she wanted to think about. Six years of her body's habits, pressed into the surfaces of the place. She hadn't noticed, until now, that a place could hold that.

She set the pack by the door.

* * *

Friday evening she went to Adaeze's apartment.

The housing authority had cleaned and repainted already — the wait list was long, they didn't waste time. White walls, bare shelves. The garden window ledge empty of pots. The creak of the third floorboard was still there when she stepped on it. Of course it was — the board was structural, not decorative. In the empty apartment the sound of it was very loud.

The evening light came in at the right angle.

Kirin stood in the middle of the empty room. The apartment had been stripped of everything Adaeze had brought to it, and without those things it was just space — well-proportioned, well-built, a good apartment that would fit whoever came next. The quality it had had for thirty-five years was gone. What remained was the architecture. The bones.

She had cried in this room exactly once, into the couch cushions, after a relationship ended badly, and Adaeze had sat beside her and said nothing, had just put one hand on her back and kept it there. She had eaten hundreds of meals at the table that was no longer here. She had told Adaeze about her first day at the Board in this room. She had sat on this floor at fourteen and done homework she didn't want to do, and Adaeze had sometimes sat with her and sometimes come back with tea and asked no questions.

She had told Adaeze, at twenty-six, that the Board work felt like reading other people's truth without making her own. And Adaeze had said: "What's the truth you want to make?"

She hadn't had an answer then.

She was starting to have one.

"I'm going," she said to the empty room.

The light moved.

She walked out and gave the key to the housing office on the ground floor. The person at the desk took it without looking up. The unit transferred. The thirty-five years of Adaeze's life at this address became a data point in the housing allocation system — accurate, complete, containing nothing at all about what had happened here.

Kirin went home and checked her pack one more time. Then she slept, and she actually slept, which surprised her.

* * *

Saturday. Four in the morning.

The transit hub was empty except for the CapEx Board displays cycling in amber — postings and responses, the quiet metabolism of the economy in its night mode. A structural assessment needed on the east river bridge. A Guild workshop seeking experienced glasswork apprentices. A homeowner in the south district asking for a materials evaluation on a suspected settling foundation. The economy turning over, identifying its needs, waiting for the hands that would meet them.

Kirin sat on a bench and read the postings. She'd been reading them for six years. Some of the posters she knew by name — the structural collective she'd worked with dozens of times, the Guild workshop where she'd done her Level Two certification review. The names of buildings, districts, project histories. The city's ongoing conversation with itself, in amber text on a dark background, at four in the morning when nobody was watching.

She'd filed her final case notes. Cleared her queue. Left a note for Brigid with instructions on two ongoing disputes, observations on in-progress assays, and a note about the new apprentice who'd over-polished her submission out of uncertainty about joins that were, in fact, correct: Her instinct to be sure is good. The extra passes are nerves, not weakness. Worth telling her so.

She'd been thorough, because that was how she did things, and because she wanted the work to continue correctly after she left, and because she understood — in the specific way you understand things when you're about to lose them — that this work mattered. That it had always mattered. That the daily labor of keeping the Compact's accounts honest was not a small thing, even if it sometimes felt like reading other people's truth.

The city was dark and very quiet. Somewhere in the workshop district, an autonomous maintenance unit made its rounds — she could hear the low hum of its motor on the pre-dawn street, checking the things it was designed to check. Running its parameters. Doing its work, whether anyone was watching or not.

She stood, put on the pack, and felt the weight settle into her shoulders. She reached for the strap adjustment and felt the cherry toggle in the loop — smooth, precise, warm from her pocket. Something that knew where it came from.

She turned east.

The city lights fell behind her. The road was good — it was always good, maintained because the systems had been told to maintain and had never received the counter-instruction. On the horizon, two hours before sunrise, no lights at all. Just the long dark and the empty, patient, not asking anything yet.

She walked into it.

The transit hub's amber displays cycled on behind her, postings and responses, need meeting skill, the Compact's quiet conversation with itself at four in the morning. She didn't look back. The work would be there when she returned. The city would be exactly where she left it.

Four hundred miles east, something her grandmother had known and hidden and kept for fifty years was waiting to be read.

She walked toward it the way she'd always worked — methodically, without hurrying, trusting that the surface would tell her what it was.
