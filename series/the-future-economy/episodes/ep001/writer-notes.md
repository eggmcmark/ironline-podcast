# Writer Notes — Episode 1: "The Okoye Commission"

## Creative Choices

**Opening**: Started with the dovetail, not Marcus. The joint is the first character we meet — then the hand feeling it. This establishes the show's hierarchy: craft first, then the craftsman. The Sheffield kitchen works as a contrast engine for the entire episode: perfect work, invisible to its owners.

**Ray as introducer**: Ray serves as the bridge between Marcus's current world and Nadia's. His voice (Bay Ridge Italian, trades shorthand) establishes the acoustic texture of Marcus's social world before we enter Nadia's intellectual one. The three-call pattern (ignore, ignore, answer) shows Marcus's relationship to interruption.

**The brownstone visit**: Structured as a reveal. We see the building's quality floor by floor before we see the kitchen's failure. This mirrors Marcus's own reading of a space — he reads load paths before he reads finishes. Nadia's nervousness (watching from the window, rapid speech, glasses on/off) is shown through Marcus's observation, not narrated.

**The fig tree**: This is the turn. Not the economics, not the spec — the fig tree. Wrapped in burlap, older than Marcus, planted (or inherited) by a woman he never met. It tells him this is a building where people gave a damn for a century. The kitchen he'd build would continue that line. This is the emotional logic of his "yes," and it precedes the economic logic.

**Danny as mirror**: Danny's skepticism is practical, not philosophical. He doesn't challenge the idea — he identifies it accurately ("if it goes down, you get a story"). He also names Marcus's core trait without Marcus being able to name it himself: "You're a romantic. You just do it with wood instead of flowers."

**The spec review at night**: This is the craft equivalent of a love scene. Marcus alone with the drawings, solving problems that no one asked him to solve, making the kitchen better in ways that will be invisible. The corner cabinet hinge, the window sill depth, the shelf bracket pitch — each is a real woodworking detail that reveals character.

**Elena**: She appears only at the margins of this episode — homework, phone call, sleep. But she's already sharp ("What percentage?"), already practical ("Then it's not dumb. It's just weird."), already the person who will build what Marcus can only imagine. Her "Good" at the end is the episode's landing.

## What I'm Setting Up

- The Sheffield kitchen as a recurring contrast to the Okoye kitchen (pay attention to this pair throughout Season 1)
- Marcus's relationship with his father (referenced but not shown — the deck builder who would say "That's not how it works")
- Danny's logistical mind vs. Marcus's craft mind (this partnership will matter when the work gets complex)
- Sofia as apprentice (she's learning; this mirrors Elena's learning, two generations of transmission)
- Nadia's rehearsed vulnerability (she prepared the pitch like a lecture; this tells us about her relationship to knowledge and control)
- The question: does payment structure change craft quality? This is the seed of the entire Compact economy. Marcus doesn't have the language for it yet.

## Worries

- The episode is almost entirely setup — the build hasn't started. Is there enough forward momentum? I think yes, because the momentum is psychological (Marcus deciding), not physical (Marcus building). Episode 2 will be all physicality.
- Marcus's internal monologue risks being too articulate for a man who "thinks with his hands." I've tried to keep his reflections concrete and material-adjacent, but the closing section where he thinks about incentive structures might push it. Worth watching.
- Nadia could tip into "manic pixie professor." I've anchored her in competence (the spec, the legal instrument, the rehearsed pitch) rather than quirkiness. Need to maintain this.
