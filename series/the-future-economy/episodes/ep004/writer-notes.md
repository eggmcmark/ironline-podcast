# Writer Notes — Episode 4: "Signal"

## What I Was Going For

The episode needed to do two things simultaneously: deliver the information the outline promised (the campus, the communications array, the tense first meeting) while staying grounded in character and travel texture. Episodes 1–3 established Kirin's POV voice, the empty zone's physical reality, and the network of people positioned at the edges of the major systems. Episode 4 needed to honor all of that while moving the story forward significantly.

The Fen cold open was a deliberate choice to give the listener a window the characters don't have. We know someone is there, and we know they've been tracking the key's signal, before Kirin and Dez arrive. This creates a specific kind of anticipation — not dread, because the figure isn't threatening, but the resonance of two people on a collision course who don't yet know each other. The cold open also lets us sit in the campus before Kirin gets there, which pays off when she arrives and has to read the space from scratch.

Cleo was the hardest character to write because she needed to be distinct from Dez. Both are economical and practical and have been out here for years. I tried to make Cleo's economy feel more deliberate — she gives information in layers, she pauses before important things, she has the patience of someone who has been alone more than Dez has and has made a fuller peace with it. Where Dez is logistically precise, Cleo is informationally strategic. She held back the campus detail until she was sure Kirin was ready for it. The note — "The key works" — is her way of doing what the whole network does: handing forward.

The Tomas/Amira interlude was essential for ensemble purposes and for emotional grounding. Kirin is out there; home is still running. Tomas knowing the content of the letters but not being able to do anything about it was the right engine for his scene. He's a carpenter. He fixes things. He can't fix this. The cabinet door he's working on is doing a lot of thematic heavy lifting.

The letters scene was the one I was most careful with. Kirin reads exactly one and a half letters — enough to understand the shape of what Adaeze was doing (keeping the watch, passing the key forward, the claim network) without resolving everything. The line "Someone is always walking it" is the echo of Adaeze's song. The second letter's phrase "what was left when you came to the city is not lost — it is held for you" sets up the bigger reveal in the campus about the scale of the claim network.

The campus sequence is structured as a series of escalating discoveries: the open gate (intentional, not maintenance), the unlocked door (deliberately unlocked), the worn keyboards (someone is working here now), the cabinet (opened by the key, reveals the scale of the Rootstock records), the sub-level door (the deepest layer, the communications array). Each discovery builds the picture. The listener should be one step ahead of Kirin by the time she gets to the array — we've already seen it in the cold open, so we know what it is when she reaches it.

The final line — "Come in. I'll put water on." — was important to get right. The figure's grief at learning Adaeze is dead comes first, before hospitality, and it's brief and not for anyone but themselves. Then they recover, and they invite the visitors in, because that is what the protocol requires and also what a person does when they've been alone for two years and someone finally arrives carrying something they've been waiting for.

## Continuity Callouts

- Kirin reads the Tamsat letters alone for the first time — this was a promise from the arc-tracker (carried since Ep 2, not read alone until now). Delivered.
- The Rootstock receiver tracking the key's passive signal: this retroactively explains why Fen has been prepared. The key has been signaling its approach. This adds depth to Hasse's observation in Ep 1 that these keys are remarkable — they're not passive objects. They broadcast, quietly, to equipment tuned to hear them.
- The 43 binders of Rootstock registry records: this is the first concrete indication of the scale of what the Rootstock Cooperative Network built. It's not one settlement's claims. It's a region's worth. This sets up the full reveal in Episodes 9–10 about the scope of the Outside's history.
- Cleo's rest stop is now a confirmed waypoint — Kirin should send word back when she can.

## What I'm Worried About

- 7,139 words is at the lower end of the target range. The script is complete — the travel days are compressed without feeling rushed, and the campus sequence takes the time it needs — but a future run might want a slightly fuller treatment of the transit corridor section or Kirin's third letter.
- Fen is introduced but not characterized beyond posture and voice. That's correct — this is the tense meeting at the end of Ep 4, not the full introduction. Ep 5 builds Fen as a complete character.
- Cleo's potential as a recurring character: she's set up well enough that "send word when you get there" could be a thread that pays off later. Don't forget her.

## New Characters to Document

Cleo: should get a full character file before Episode 5 is written. Key facts: 62-ish, 11 years at the rest stop, white-gray locs, many-pocketed vest, copper-gray eyes, economical speech (layered, strategic about what she reveals and when), knows the northwest corridor's geography in precise detail, has been leaving supplies at the campus gate for 3 years, received the note "The key works" three weeks before Kirin arrived. Was she ever Compact or Free Zone? Not established — could be either, or neither. Suggest leaving this ambiguous.

Fen: unnamed in this episode, to be fully introduced in Episode 5. Facts established here: has been at the campus 2 years, methodical (calibration marks, dated notebooks), maintained equipment for a previous purpose (the logistics terminals), has been tracking the Rootstock signal for 3 days before Kirin arrived, speaks quietly and without performance, first words upon seeing Kirin are "How long have you had the key" (i.e., their first concern is the key's status, the second is Adaeze's), voice is gender-ambiguous in this episode.
