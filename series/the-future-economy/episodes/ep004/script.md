# Episode 4: "Signal"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin and Dez reach Cleo at the rest stop, then push east through the transit corridor toward a research campus where something has been receiving, and waiting.*

---

The person who lived in the research campus woke before the data arrived, and lay listening for it.

This was a habit from the first winter — lying in the dark of what had been a graduate student lounge, tracking the minutes until 0317 the way you track weather: not by watching but by feeling the air change. Some nights they counted the sounds the campus made. The sub-level's receiver array on its maintenance cycle, deep below the floor. The solar arrays on the roof, which hummed very softly when clouds passed over and the charge rate dropped. The building settling in the cold, the particular vocabulary of a poured-concrete pre-Compact structure built in the 2060s to last a century and now well into its seventh decade of doing exactly that.

At 0317 the array made its sound: not a beep, because that was not how the system worked, but a shift in the underlying hum of the receiver equipment, as though a door had opened somewhere and then closed. The person heard it the way they heard their own breathing — not as a sound but as an event accounted for.

They rose. Pulled on a wool sweater, the heavy kind knitted from an unraveled blanket found in the library building across the courtyard. Went downstairs. Through the student lounge, where the single lamp they kept burning in the window cast its yellow square of light onto the courtyard flagstones below. Through the server room on the ground floor, past the rack of legacy terminals that the previous residents had used for something and which the person maintained out of a principle they had never articulated but held firmly: if a thing is working, you keep it working. Through the utility panel that was not a utility panel.

The sub-level was twelve feet below the communications building, and it was warm. The equipment generated heat as equipment does, and the room's concrete walls held it. Amber display screens on the equipment racks: someone had wired them that way. The person had thought about this often. Amber was easier on the eyes at 0317. Whoever built this had built it for long hours, alone, at night. They had built it with that person in mind, even if they hadn't known that person yet.

The primary display showed the incoming packet. Consistent volume. Consistent encryption. Origin signature: the same it had always been, triangulated to a broad zone somewhere east of the Pacific's western edge — what the Compact's maps labeled, when they were being cautious, as "unverifiable territory," which the person had come to understand as a map's way of saying: real, but outside our categories. They noted the packet in the current notebook. Volume, signature, timestamp. A row among hundreds of rows.

Then they checked the secondary display.

The Rootstock receiver was a separate system. Smaller. Running on its own dedicated circuit from a power cell that required monthly replacement. It had been here when they arrived, already running — an older technology than the primary array, cobbled together from parts that were themselves cobbled together, but functional, which was the thing that mattered. When there was something to show, the small screen showed it: the passive signal of any live Rootstock authentication key within approximately a hundred miles.

For most of the two years the person had lived here, the screen had shown nothing.

Three days ago, a signal had appeared.

Today: twelve miles. Down from twenty-two yesterday. Moving east.

The person sat with that number. Their hands were flat on the desk. Twelve miles was half a day's walking. The campus would have a visitor by evening, or by tomorrow morning at the latest.

They had asked themselves, on the first day the signal appeared, whether they were afraid. The honest answer had been no — not because they were fearless, but because they had been expecting something like this from the beginning, and expectation held long enough becomes its own kind of calm. The key had been moving west of the Compact for three weeks, by the signal's first appearance, and it was now moving east. Someone had carried it through and turned around. That was how the protocol worked. It had always been how it worked.

They set down their pen. The campus at dawn was assembling itself around them: birds in the courtyard's overgrown planting beds, the solar arrays on the buildings coming online with the first measure of light, a deer moving across the flagstones with the unhurried certainty of something that had made this route its own long before the human arrived and fully expected to continue it long after.

The person watched the deer go. Then they went upstairs to put water on for tea.

There were things to do before the visitor came.

* * *

The road had given them good weather for a day and a half.

Kirin marked this as luck and said so. Dez agreed, briefly, and moved on. This was Dez's general approach to luck: acknowledge it, file it, let it go. They had been walking since the afternoon of the first day out of the settlement — Dez's road pace, which was not a hiking pace but a traveling one, the kind that consumed miles without announcing them. Kirin had adjusted. Her legs had adjusted. Her feet, the left heel reinforced by Silber's careful work with the adhesive tin, were holding.

The scrubland east of the settlement was giving way. Stands of birch and alder rising from what had been farmland for decades, the old geometry of cultivation still readable in the long parallels of soil color — the shapes of fields, still there, though what grew in them now was nobody's concern but the fields'. Dez named things as they passed: a water point at a maintained standpipe, a shelter she'd used in her first winter east, the location of a bridge that was fine but sounded wrong underfoot and was therefore worth knowing about. She delivered this the way she delivered everything: as logistics. Solved, filed, moving on.

Kirin listened and noted. Note-taking was how she processed things she didn't yet understand. She had been doing it her whole career — that instinct to reduce the unfamiliar to data points, to find the substrate under the surface and read it. The empty zone was teaching her that some substrates resisted that reading. Not because they were hiding anything. Because they were genuinely larger than the frame.

On the afternoon of the second day, Dez said "rest stop ahead" in the tone she used for practical landmarks, and looked east along the highway.

The rest stop was half a mile out. From a distance: the low institutional-beige building, the covered picnic roof, the worn parking lot — all the pre-Compact roadside architecture of an era that had built for utility and expected the users to supply the warmth. But on the south face of the roof: solar panels. Self-installed, not Compact or Free Zone spec. Newer than the building by decades. And in the parking lot: growing containers made from salvaged shipping materials, planted with late-season greens, their leaves darkened by the recent cold.

Someone had made a home here. Someone had made a home here carefully.

A woman sat in the shade of the covered roof on a folding chair, working with a coil of wire. She set it down when they crossed into the lot, and she looked at Kirin with the full, unhurried attention of someone who had been expecting something and was now assessing whether what arrived matched what they'd expected.

She did not stand.

"Dez," she said.

"Cleo."

Cleo looked at Kirin. She was in her early sixties, small and well-made, with white-gray locs pulled back at the neck and hands that were the specific dark-bronze of someone who worked outdoors in all weather and had not minded. She wore a many-pocketed vest, all pockets apparently occupied. Her eyes were the color of weathered copper, and they rested on Kirin with the patience of someone who had stopped performing patience long ago and had arrived at the real thing.

"Come sit," she said. "Water's on."

* * *

The interior of the rest stop was eleven years of intelligent habitation. Cleo had taken the bones of a vending corridor and a public restroom and made something that worked: water collection from the gutters, run through a filtration system she'd assembled from commercial treatment parts. Solar charge from the roof panels to a battery bank behind the workshop. The workshop itself occupied what had been the vending area — a full repair station, organized with the precision of someone for whom disorganization was not an option and never had been. Behind a curtained partition: a sleeping area. At the center of the room: a table, two chairs, a third chair that appeared to have arrived in anticipation of guests. A rack of data cores slotted in a custom frame, plus a dozen paper books with their spines worn to near-illegibility.

Cleo made tea from dried herbs — something resinous and faintly smoky, unfamiliar to Kirin — and set it in front of them without asking. She sat with her own cup and looked at Kirin directly.

"How far are you going."

"To coordinates four hundred miles east of Sable River. Roughly."

"Rootstock coordinates."

"Yes."

Cleo nodded. Not surprised. "From here the location puts you approximately two hundred miles east-southeast. There's a transit corridor sixty miles east — old rail infrastructure, running north to south, maintained. South of the corridor, following the service road, about ninety miles — the landscape changes. You'll see it. More intact structures. Less wild return. Pre-Compact research infrastructure, kept up by the autonomous systems." She drank from her cup. "There's a campus. University system, multi-building, been empty since the contraction. The solar arrays on the roofs were installed more recently. Someone put them there."

Dez set her cup down. "You've been there."

"I leave supplies at the gate. Every few months, when I make the circuit." She said this without inflection, the way she said most things: matter of fact, information delivered. "I've been doing it for three years. Each time I return, the supplies are gone."

"You didn't mention a campus," Dez said. Her voice was even.

"I mention it now." Cleo was untroubled. "You asked if there was anything maintained east of the corridor. There is. I said so." She looked at Dez with a quality of patience that had outlasted many conversations. "I wasn't certain there was a person. Now I am." She reached into a pocket of her vest and produced a small folded paper. She opened it and laid it flat on the table.

Three words, in a very precise hand: *The key works.*

Kirin looked at the paper. She looked up.

"They knew I was coming."

"Three weeks ago, I left a note with the supplies. I said I understood someone was resident, I wasn't a threat, and if someone was coming from the west with a Rootstock key, they should know they were expected." Cleo refolded the note and put it away. "That was waiting for me on my last circuit." She met Kirin's eyes. "Your grandmother was Adaeze Okonkwo."

Not a question.

"Yes," Kirin said.

"I knew the name. Not the woman. The name came to me over years — slowly, from people who'd been further east than I've gone, in the way information comes out here, which is sideways and slow and usually from someone you trust slightly less than you trust the information." A pause. "Adaeze Okonkwo was someone the network knew. She went into the Compact decades ago and she sent the key back when she was ready." She looked at Kirin steadily. "You're the first I've seen in eleven years carrying a live key eastbound. I've been here eleven years."

Kirin put her hand in her jacket pocket. The key was warm, as it always was, steady as a second pulse. She took it out and set it on the table.

Cleo did not reach for it. She leaned forward and looked at it with her hands in her lap, the way you look at something you have heard described many times and are now meeting for the first time. For a long moment she said nothing. Then, quietly, she said: "There."

Just that.

"You recognize it," Kirin said.

"I recognize what it is. I've never seen the actual object." She sat back. "Out here the long-timers have stories about the keys. Pre-Compact verification artifacts. Each one corresponding to something registered in the Rootstock protocol." She looked at Kirin directly. "Most of us gave up on Rootstock claims long ago. The Compact won't recognize them. The Free Zones don't care. What the claims point to has no institutional path. But this one was kept active. Passed forward. Your grandmother carried it into the Compact and kept it warm for thirty-five years." She picked up her cup. "Which suggests that what it points to isn't something that needs institutional recognition."

Kirin looked at the key on the table. At the etched patterns she had studied until they were as familiar as her own handwriting. At the warmth she could feel from across the table.

"Then what does it need," she said.

Cleo looked at her with her weathered-copper eyes, and something in them shifted — not warming exactly, but settling, the way a thing settles when it has been held in suspension and has finally resolved.

"A person to carry it the rest of the way," she said. She stood. "Get some sleep. I'll draw you the route in the morning."


* * *

In Sable River, in the converted tram barn on the south edge of the Guild district, Tomas Vanek worked past ten o'clock on a cabinet door that did not require this kind of attention at this hour.

He knew this. He kept working.

The smell of the workshop was sawdust and linseed oil, which was the smell of his life and had been since his apprenticeship at fourteen, and it was a smell that usually settled him. Tonight it was just a smell. He had the long bench pulled to task height under the overhead lamp, and the circle of light it cast was sharp enough that everything outside it — the rest of the barn, the high windows showing the dark of the city beyond — felt remote. Good. He did not want the rest of the barn tonight. He wanted the door and the tools and his hands doing something they knew how to do.

He heard Amira before she came in. Her particular step: quiet, unhurried, the footfall of someone who had spent three years in the eastern cultivated zone and had learned to move through spaces without announcing herself. She had a key to the side door. She had keys to most places in Kirin's life, the way trusted people do — not because they needed them, but because Kirin had decided, at some point, that her world was their world.

Amira pulled the short-legged stool from under the bench and sat. The stool rocked once, subtly. She shifted without thinking to account for it.

"Perun sent the translation," Tomas said, without turning.

"I know." She paused. "He sent me a copy too. I waited to see if you'd call."

"I didn't know what to say." He set down the smoothing plane. He looked at the door in front of him — ash, from the western Guild district, a very reasonable grain, nothing to do with anything. "She didn't know what the letters said when she left. She's been carrying them for weeks and she hasn't read the translation alone yet."

"We don't know that."

"Perun knows. He said — he said she took the translated pages and didn't read them in front of him. He thinks she needed to be further away first." He looked at his hands. Carpenter's hands, built for specific tasks, none of which were useful right now. "She'll read them alone, and she won't have anyone to talk to about it."

"She has Dez."

"She barely knows Dez."

"She barely knew the letters were in a language she couldn't read, three weeks ago." Amira's voice was not without feeling — she was not like that, never had been — but it was at a temperature that was useful rather than expressive. It was the temperature of someone who had made their peace with distances. "The translation isn't bad news, Tomas. Your grandmother kept a watch for thirty-five years because she was asked to and because she trusted that what she was watching over was worth it. That's not a tragedy. That's a commitment."

"It's thirty-five years of not telling her granddaughter who she was."

"It's thirty-five years of making sure the granddaughter was ready before she found out." She looked at the barn around them — the high space, the tool racks, the long quiet of a workshop after hours. "Kirin's ready. She was ready before she found the box. She just didn't know it yet."

Tomas picked up the plane. Set it down. "I know that," he said. "I've known since we were fourteen."

"Then what."

He was quiet for a moment. Outside the high windows, Sable River did its night maintenance: the climate system cycling on its usual schedule, the transit network running its empty late circuits, the city ticking correctly over regardless of who was awake to hear it.

"I need to be doing something," he said. "And I'm not doing something. That's what."

Amira stayed on the stool. The stool rocked slightly when she shifted. She did not say: that's the deal, sometimes. She did not say: she's fine, she'll call. She said nothing for a while, which was one of her better qualities, and then she said:

"She has range in three or four days. If Cleo's where she usually is, the point-to-point will reach me from there. I'll tell you as soon as I hear."

He picked up the plane and went back to the door.

Amira stayed. The workshop held them both.

* * *

Cleo cooked dinner from dried lentils and something she grew in the bins outside — a root vegetable Kirin didn't recognize, dense and slightly sweet, cut fine and roasted on the camp stove with a spice she couldn't identify either. She cooked with the efficiency of someone who had been doing this alone for years and was adapting for three without commentary.

They ate at the small table with their elbows in. Cleo and Dez talked territory — the specific, practical shorthand of people who had been covering the same ground from different positions. The bridge at the forty-mile mark on the service road south of the corridor: navigable, Cleo said, but the southern approach was better than the northern because of a drainage issue on the north side that got bad in heavy rain. A community two days north that had relocated, reasons secondhand. The behavior of the maintenance drones on the corridor itself — they ran a different pattern here than they did further west, more concentrated, as if the system had assigned more resources to keeping this particular infrastructure precise.

Kirin ate and listened. She was learning the shape of the empty zone from the inside: not the absence she'd expected, not the eerie maintenance-theater of the first two days. Something more particular. A world with its own geography of knowledge, distributed among the people who lived at its edges. Cleo knew the terrain south of the corridor. Dez knew the west. The person at the campus knew the deep interior. None of them knew the whole map. The whole map was between them.

After dinner, Dez spread Cleo's hand-drawn route chart on the table and they reviewed it together — precise annotations in pencil, distances in miles, notes about water and shelter and one section marked simply *confusing* in Cleo's hand with a small explanatory diagram beside it. Kirin looked at the diagram and thought: that is exactly the kind of information that doesn't fit a data core and is also exactly the kind of information you die without. She added what she could to her own notes.

Dez went to review the route more thoroughly. Kirin went outside.

* * *

The night was clear and cold, and the stars were more than she had words for.

In Sable River, even in her neighborhood's quieter blocks, the light was enough that the stars were a suggestion. Out here, twelve miles from any persistent artificial light, they were a fact — layered, dense, the galaxy's own structure visible in the long band overhead. She stood in the parking lot of the rest stop and looked at them for a while. The cold pressed against her face and hands. The smell of dried grass and something green coming from the east.

Then she sat on the low wall of the picnic area and took the waxed pouch from her pack.

She had been carrying the translated letters since the morning she left Sable River. Perun's handwriting, careful and unhurried, below the Tamsat lines in their original hand. She had not read them alone yet. There had been reasons — the frontier, the settlement, the road, the constant company of travel. But the real reason was that she had known, from the moment Perun handed her the pages, that reading them alone would mean something different than reading them beside him. It would mean being with what they said. Being with who her grandmother had been, outside the version of Adaeze she'd known.

She unfolded the first letter.

The Tamsat original ran across the page in its long connected lines, with the curled marks above the letters that Kirin could now half-read — not for meaning, but for shape. She read Perun's translation.

*The settlement is still standing. We are thinking of you in the city. We are not waiting. We are building. You knew this when you left and it was the right choice and we are not asking you to come back. Keep the watch. Keep the key warm. When the time comes, send it forward to someone who can carry it. The road is long but the road is maintained. Someone is always walking it.*

Kirin read it twice. Then she sat with it.

Someone is always walking it.

She thought about the maintenance drones — the lawn mowers, the road repair units, the traffic light at Meridian Road cycling green to red for nobody. She thought about the campus ahead of them, the lights on in the communications building after dark. She thought about Adaeze, going to work at the elder-care facility every morning for twenty years, singing in Tamsat to residents who were dying, in a language nobody could translate, the road is long but there is water at the end of it.

Not hiding. Keeping something warm.

She unfolded the second letter. This one was older — the paper more worn, the ink faded to brown at the edges. Same hand, different season of the writer's life.

*The foundations are being registered. There are seventeen claims now in the northwest corridor alone. What you left when you came to the city is not lost. It is held for you. The key holds it. When someone carries the key back, what was left becomes findable again. That is all it needs. Someone to carry it back.*

She did not unfold the third letter. She sat with the second one and looked at the stars and listened to the empty zone doing its patient maintenance around her — the distant cycling of something mechanical to the north, the sound of the wind in the dried grass, the very faint hum of the rest stop's solar system maintaining charge.

Her grandmother had been twenty-eight years old when she came into the Compact. The same age Kirin was now. She had walked through the frontier with the key in her pocket and a set of letters in a language she never taught anyone and a life she had carefully assembled to last decades. She had raised a granddaughter and watched her granddaughter become someone who could do this.

That is all it needs.

Dez appeared in the doorway of the rest stop, not looking for her — just getting air, the way Dez got air, without announcing it. She stood in the doorway for a moment, looking west, at something that wasn't there or maybe was. Then she came and sat on the wall a few feet away.

Neither of them spoke. It was not silence that needed filling. It was the silence of people who had been walking together long enough to share it.

After a while, Dez said: "Three to four days from here to the campus. Depending on the bridge."

"We'll keep the pace."

"I know."

The stars did not move, but they seemed to.

* * *

Morning came with Cleo already up and coffee already made — the Free Zone kind, dark and pungent, from a sealed tin she said she'd traded for and did not say with whom. She pressed a packet of supplies into Kirin's pack without asking: dried protein, sealed, three months' shelf life. A supplement to the route map covering the section marked *confusing*, annotated with Cleo's characteristic lack of flourish. She said: "The confusing part is before the bridge. Once you're past the bridge, it's obvious. Don't try to navigate the confusing part — follow the creek."

At the door, Dez clasped Cleo's hand in the way of people who keep careful accounts with each other. Kirin did the same.

Cleo looked at her as they clasped. Her eyes were the weathered copper again, and something in them was neither warm nor cold but steady — the gaze of someone who had made a long study of what people carried and had come to a conclusion.

"She was right to trust you," Cleo said.

Kirin held that for a moment. There was nothing to say to it that wouldn't be inadequate.

"Send word when you get there," Cleo said. "I've been here eleven years. I'd like to know what's on the other end."

She let go of Kirin's hand and went inside. They walked east.

* * *

The country south of the transit corridor was different.

They reached the corridor on the afternoon of the third day, following a highway that curved steadily southeast and then ended at a grade crossing where the rail line ran clean and exact through the overgrown landscape. The rails were maintained to a precision that the road had not been — clean metal, no rust, the ties in good condition, the whole installation running somewhere between here and nowhere with the patient exactness of a thing that knew its purpose even when its purpose had gone away.

The grade crossing signals were functional. They cycled red to green to red on a schedule that had no train to signal for. Dez stood at the crossing and looked both ways — north and south, the line disappearing into tree cover in both directions — and said nothing for a moment. Then: "South from here."

They followed the service road Cleo's map described. It was not smooth, but it was passable — maintained just enough to remain a road, not enough to be inviting. On either side, the forest had changed. Less scrub oak and pioneer growth, more hardwood — mature stands that had been growing without interference for thirty, forty years, with the particular stillness of a forest that has forgotten it was once farmland. The sounds changed too. Fewer open-country birds; more of the deeper, quieter sounds of closed-canopy forest. The wind, when it came, moved differently here — filtered through the branches rather than pouring across open ground.

On the afternoon of the fourth day, the forest opened. Not dramatically — gradually, the way a clearing reveals itself before you're in it. More light through the canopy. Then the canopy thinning. Then a maintained approach road, the grass cut short by a mowing system, running straight for forty meters to a chain-link gate.

The gate was open.

Kirin stopped at the edge of the approach road. Dez stopped beside her. Neither of them spoke for a moment.

The key in Kirin's pocket had not changed temperature. She had stopped believing in its temperature as information weeks ago — it was always warm, it had always been warm. But there was something else now. A weight. Not heavier in her pocket than before, but more present. The way a held breath becomes more present the longer it's held.

"Gate's open," Dez said.

"Yes."

"Maintenance system, maybe."

"Maybe."

Dez looked at her. Something moved through her expression — not quite humor and not quite apprehension, but the shape of both. "Go ahead, then," she said.

They went.

* * *

The courtyard was overgrown in the way that a tended space goes when the tending stops having a goal.

The maintenance systems had kept the perimeter — the approach lane, the gate, the flagstone path from the gate to the main building's entrance. But the planting beds along the courtyard walls had long since overflowed their edges, and what had been an ornamental arrangement was now a dense, opinionated mix of original plants and opportunists. Late-autumn color in the low-growing things, spent seed heads on the taller ones, the dried architecture of summer still standing, waiting for the cold to finish deciding. A tree at the courtyard's center — a mature linden, Kirin thought, from the shape of the bark, though she was not confident — bare now, its upper branches a fine winter drawing against the pale sky.

Six buildings around the courtyard: the main building facing the gate, two lab buildings to the left, a library on the right with shelving still visible through the windows, a maintenance building along the back wall, and the communications building. This last one smaller than the others, set slightly apart at the courtyard's far corner.

The light in its ground-floor window was a lamp. Steady. Warm.

Neither Kirin nor Dez said anything about the light.

The main building's doors were unlocked. Not in the abandoned way of the empty zone's houses and commercial strips — not left unlocked because nobody had come to lock them since everyone left. These were unlocked deliberately. Deliberately, and recently. The latch was clean. No oxidation on the mechanism. Someone kept this door.

Inside: a commons area. Chairs and tables still arranged for purpose, which was more than the empty zone's abandoned interiors usually managed. A whiteboard on the far wall with a work schedule in faded marker — 2084 in the upper corner, a term date, names assigned to tasks. She read the names. None she knew. She thought: someone stood here forty-two years ago and wrote those names with complete certainty that they would matter, that the schedule would be followed, that the term would proceed as planned. And then the contraction.

Dez was already in the hallway, moving with the efficient attention of someone who knew how to clear a space. Kirin followed.

The first lab was storage — the equipment pushed against the walls, shelving improvised from materials that were not originally shelving. Organized, though. Not abandoned. The difference was legible in the way things were placed: someone had made decisions about this room. Someone still used it.

The second lab was different.

The workstations were in their original configuration: two rows of terminals facing a central aisle, the kind of setup Kirin associated with shared computational work. Pre-Compact hardware, older than anything in the Compact's current inventory, but the surfaces were clean. The keyboards showed wear in the specific way of things that have been touched repeatedly, in the same places, for years. The function keys. The spacebar. Hands had been here recently.

Dez stood in the doorway and turned slowly. "Logistics terminals," she said. "The old Free Zone kind. Pre-Compact. I used versions of these in the freight operation." She crossed to the nearest workstation. The screen was dark. She touched the edge of the keyboard, not pressing anything. "The wear pattern. Someone was working here."

"Is working here," Kirin said.

Dez looked at her. "Yes."

Kirin was looking at the east wall. Not the workstations. A cabinet there — full height, double-doored, sealed. The lock was not a Compact latch or a Free Zone security seal. It was older than either: a ring of small contacts around a shallow depression, flush with the surface, the same geometry she had seen twice before. Once in a linen closet. Once on a box that her grandmother had hidden behind thirty-five years of neatly folded towels.

She had the key out before she'd consciously decided to take it.

The key fit the depression and did what it did: not a mechanical action but something warmer than that — the contacts receiving the key's warmth, or receiving something in the key's warmth, and releasing. A breath letting go. The cabinet doors swung open.

Inside: paper. Binders, dozens of them, filling every shelf. She lifted the top binder and looked at the cover page.

Rootstock Registry — Northwest Corridor — Fragmented Records Backup. 2071–2089. Hand-Transcribed.

She opened it.

Column after column in a small, precise hand — registration numbers, claim coordinates, witness names, registration dates going back to 2071. Names she didn't recognize. Locations that were, by the coordinates, in this region — in the empty zone, in the depopulated territory, in the country she was standing in right now. Claim after claim, registered in the Rootstock protocol, preserved in handwriting on physical paper because whoever had done this had understood that digital records could fail and paper could not.

She counted the binders on the shelves: forty-three.

"Dez," she said.

Dez came and looked at the open binder. Her expression did not change — or changed only in the way it changed when she was processing something that would take time to fully process. "How many claims."

Kirin turned pages. The columns were dense, consistent, the record of a systematic effort. "This binder alone — hundreds. And there are forty-three binders."

"That's not one settlement's claims."

"No." She looked at the cover page again. Northwest Corridor. "This is a region. Someone was registering claims for an entire region, in a protocol the Compact doesn't recognize, and keeping physical backup records in a locked cabinet in an empty building in the middle of the depopulated zone." She looked at the room. At the worn keyboards. At the careful organization of the shelves. "For decades."

Dez was quiet for a moment. Then: "Come look at this."

She was in the doorway, pointing across the hall. Not at a door — at a wall. The wall of the server room across the corridor: the same poured concrete as the rest of the building, the same utilitarian surface. But the seam was there, when you looked. A hairline rectangle, the height of a person and the width of a passage.

At the center: flush with the wall, a small panel. The same ring of contacts. The same shallow depression.

The key in Kirin's hand had not cooled. It was the same warmth it always was, the warmth she had long since stopped interpreting as information. But she felt it now the way she had felt the cabinet lock before she touched it: as a recognition, something on both sides of the contact registering the other.

She pressed the key to the panel.

The wall breathed.

A section of it — the rectangle she'd seen in the seam — swung inward on a pivot mechanism, smooth and silent, into darkness. Below the threshold: the top of a concrete staircase, lit from below by amber strips along the sides. Warm air rising. The smell of electronics and something dry — the smell of a room that has been running for a long time.

Dez looked into it. Then she looked at Kirin.

They went down.

* * *

The sub-level was a working room. That was the thing that hit first — not that it was remarkable, but that it was occupied. Not occupied in the sense of someone being present, but occupied in the sense of a space that had been organized around a person's daily life. The notebook on the desk shelf, current date on the spine. The warm cup next to the notebook, the ring it had left on the concrete surface just drying. The sweater draped over the chair back, shaped to the body that wore it.

The east wall held the equipment: receiver arrays, antenna feeds from above, processor racks with their amber displays showing live status. All of it functioning. The calibration marks on the housings, dated in the same precise hand as the cabinet records. Recent. Weekly, some of them. Someone had been here this morning.

On the primary display: a live data stream.

Kirin crossed to it. She was a materials analyst. She had never worked in signals or communications. But she knew how to read a display she hadn't been trained on — she'd been doing it her whole career, reading instruments in domains adjacent to her own, and the skill was the same regardless of the domain: look at what the system is telling you and ask what the system needs to be true for this to make sense.

The stream showed data arriving in packets. She timed them: roughly one every forty minutes. The compression ratio was high — large amounts of information reduced to small volumes, which meant either efficient encoding or lossy encoding, and from the consistency of the packet sizes she would have bet efficient. The encryption: a format she didn't recognize. The decryption key, obviously, she did not have.

But the origin signature was in the header. Unencrypted, because that was how transmission protocols worked — the routing information had to be readable by the systems along the path, or the path would fail.

She looked at the origin signature for a long time.

Not a Compact relay address. The Compact's addressing format was standardized and she knew it from years of Ledger verification work. Not a Free Zone format — she had seen those in the rare-earth provenance files that came through the Trade Board, the external records, and this was nothing like them. Not any national infrastructure she had reference data for.

The prefix was a format she didn't know. The suffix triangulated to a point east of the Pacific's western coast — east of the empty zone, east of the Free Zones, east of the Compact, east of everything she had any direct reference for. Somewhere across the ocean.

She was still looking at it when Dez said, quietly, from behind her: "It's been running."

"At least two years. The packet log goes back that far." She scrolled up through the log. Consistent arrivals, every day. Every day for two years, at the same intervals, from the same origin, the same volume. She scrolled back to the current entry. "Dez."

"I see it."

"Someone has been sitting in this room, alone, reading whatever this says, every day, for at least two years." She turned around. She looked at the room — the whole room now, the whole picture. The notebook. The cup. The sweater on the chair. The rack of records on the shelf, two years of daily accounting. "This is what the authentication key pointed to. Not the claims in the cabinet upstairs. This."

"What's on the other end," Dez said.

"I don't know. But it's not in the Compact. It's not in a Free Zone. It's not in any system our maps have categories for." She looked at the origin signature again. "It's coming from across the ocean. Every day. Something out there has been sending data to this room for at least two years, and someone has been here to receive it."

Dez exhaled, short and controlled, the sound she made when something was worth taking seriously. She looked at the equipment wall. She looked at the notebooks. She looked at the primary display, at the live stream running its patient accounting.

"Your grandmother," she said, slowly. "She wasn't connected to a settlement in the empty zone."

"No."

"She was connected to this."

"She was connected to something this connects to. This is a relay." Kirin looked at the room for another moment. "A relay between what's out here and something much further away."

A sound from the back of the room.

Soft. Not accidental. The sound of a door — a door she had not noticed, in the shadows at the far end of the equipment wall, where the amber display light didn't reach. It was open. Had been open, she understood. The room beyond was lit by something dim and warm — another lamp, the same kind as the one she'd seen in the window above.

A figure in the doorway.

Not large. Standing still. Holding a lamp, angled down — light forward, not upward. The deliberate positioning of someone who wanted to be seen without blinding anyone. The face was in the lamp's shadow, but the posture was clear: watchful, steady, not threatening. The posture of someone who had anticipated this moment, who had had time to make a decision about how to be in it, and had decided on stillness.

They stood in the amber-lit room under the research campus, all three of them, with the data stream running its forty-minute pulse behind Kirin and the notebooks on their shelf and forty-three binders of hand-transcribed records in the cabinet upstairs and the key warm in her hand, and nobody moved.

"How long," the figure said, "have you had the key."

Their voice was quiet. Not a performance of calm. The real thing — the calm of someone who had asked this question in their head many times and was now simply releasing it into the air.

Kirin looked at the figure. At the stillness of them. At the lamp in their hand.

"About six weeks," she said.

The figure did not move. But something changed in them — a very small thing, a breath, a settling. Then, quietly, not for Kirin, not for Dez — for themselves:

"Then she's gone."

The stream behind Kirin continued. Forty minutes it would deliver its next packet. And after that, forty minutes more. And after that. The world across the ocean sending its daily signal, patient and consistent, into this room that had been built to receive it, by people who had understood that the road is long and the maintenance does not stop.

The figure lowered their lamp slightly. Their face came partway into the light.

"Come in," they said. "I'll put water on."
