# Episode 4: "The Curated Mile"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin and Dez drive east on the curated road, arrive at an unmapped depot where the key is recognized, and learn the shape of the empty zone's three-way arrangement — at a price neither of them budgeted for.*

---

The key cycled three times in the first hour.

Kirin had been tracking it since the gate of Salt went behind them — not watching for it exactly, but keeping her hand loose in the jacket pocket so she would feel the change when it came. Warm for a count of three. Then cold. Then nothing for a stretch of road. Then again.

Dez drove without acknowledgment. Either she didn't notice or she had decided not to. Kirin was not yet sure which version of Dez she was getting, and she had not been in the vehicle long enough to ask.

The road was wrong in a way that took her twenty minutes to place.

She had spent four years at the CapEx Board reading materials submitted for Ledger verification. Stone. Concrete aggregate. Ceramic binder. She knew what a road looked like when it was maintained, and what it looked like when it was maintained *recently* — the color of fresh seal-coat, the geometry of drainage cuts, the way a patched section sat slightly proud of the original grade. She knew how to tell old work from new work by the angle at which light came off a surface at low sun.

The road they were on had been worked inside the last two years. Not the whole length — in patches, the way any maintenance program worked in patches, crew by crew, section by section. But the patches were not the emergency-repair style of a fleet running on inertia. They were systematic. Someone had a maintenance plan and was executing it.

"This road," she said.

"I know," Dez said.

"Someone's been keeping it."

"Yes."

"Not the standard fleet. Different contractor signature. The drainage cut is angled — fifteen degrees off the shoulder."

Dez glanced at her for the first time since they had left Salt.

"Where are you seeing that."

"Where it crosses the verge. Compact specification on secondary maintenance roads is twenty degrees. Whatever cut these came in at fifteen."

Dez looked back at the road. She said nothing for another quarter mile. Then: "You do surfaces."

"Materials analysis. But surfaces are surfaces."

Dez made a sound that was not agreement and was not disagreement. It was the sound of a person updating a file.

The road went east through country that Kirin's data core had mapped, eight years ago, as abandoned agricultural land. Eight years ago there had been a river on the south and fields on the north that had been fallow since the depopulation of the corridor districts in 2093. The data core showed an overgrown grid, broken fenceline, the outlines of farmsteads stripped to the metal before the scavenge market dried up. The data core was not wrong, exactly. The overgrown grid was still there. But the field along the north shoulder had been mown in the last three weeks, in a pattern that started twelve feet from the verge and went out as far as she could see in the flat gray morning light. The mow pattern was not random. It was not the perimeter control of a fleet running on inertia. It was a sight-line clearance.

Nobody cleared sight lines along a corridor they were not watching.

"The mow," she said.

"Yes."

"From where to where."

"Probably the whole sixty miles to Larch." Dez shifted her grip on the wheel. "I thought so from when we turned off the main route. Didn't want to say it while we were still close enough to Salt that it might matter."

"Why does the distance to Salt matter."

"It doesn't. Force of habit." She looked in her mirror. "This maintenance spur — it's a monitored road. Has been for at least two years. I drove it four times before that and it was different. Different feel. Then about two years ago it changed. The field clearance started on a stretch eight miles east of where we joined. It's been moving west since. Another year and it'll reach Salt's gate."

"Who monitors it."

"That," Dez said, "is going to take more than sixty miles to answer."

The key warmed in Kirin's pocket. It cooled.

* * *

They stopped once before the depot, at a concrete spillway running under the road where Dez needed to refill the vehicle's water reservoir. The spillway was clean-running — no algae, no mineral stain at the margins. Kirin knelt at the edge while Dez worked the pump and put her hand in. The water was cold and fast.

"This is managed," Kirin said.

"Springs don't manage themselves."

"The spillway walls were re-pointed in the last year. You can see the color difference in the mortar joints along the upper course." She stood and dried her hand on her trousers. "Fleet units don't point masonry. This is manual work."

Dez looked at the spillway as if she had used it fifty times and had never looked at it this way. The pump completed its cycle with a low click. She capped the reservoir.

"You're going to be useful," she said. "I am going to enjoy arguing with you about whether that is a compliment."

"It isn't one."

"No. It's a fact." She went to the driver's side. "Come on."

The road went on. The key was quiet for eight miles. Then, in the shallow dip before a concrete overpass, it warmed sharply and did not cool for a count of seven. Kirin held still in her seat. At seven the warmth dropped. Something at the overpass — above it, below it, housed in the concrete itself — had pinged the key and received a reply.

She counted the insulator posts on the utility poles as they passed under the bridge.

The insulators were new.

* * *

The depot was at mile fifty-three.

It appeared as the road came out of a long curve through a stand of old-growth birch that had taken back a former interchange and was in the process of taking back the ramps. On the far side of the trees, in a hollow that would not be visible from the main highway, a set of low prefab structures sat in an arrangement that was functional and nothing else. No signage. No cleared perimeter. Three vehicles in front — two utility and one heavy flatbed with active work on the rear axle. A row of drums along one wall with the yellow-and-black banding that meant lubricant. A satellite antenna on the roof of the longest structure, small, pointed.

Four people visible. Working.

None of them wore Compact colors. None wore the gray-green of Free Zone field crews she had seen in reference images at the Board. Their coats were plain, patched at the elbows, with the practicality of people who repaired things for a living. They had patches on the left shoulder she could not read from distance.

One of them saw the vehicle. They did not reach for anything. They went inside.

"This isn't on Naila's map," Kirin said.

"Naila's map is Naila's map." Dez slowed. "She told us to come to Larch. She didn't tell us everything between."

"She knew this was here."

"Probably."

"Then why didn't she mention it."

"Because mentioning it changes how you carry yourself at the gate, and at a place like this, how you carry yourself at the gate is more information than you want to give for free."

Dez pulled in off the road and stopped twenty meters from the nearest structure. A person came out of the door the first person had gone into.

This woman was in her early thirties, thickly built through the shoulders. Her hair cut to the practical length of someone who cut their own hair when it got in the way. She had the kind of face that had learned to stay even a long time ago and had been doing so long enough that the evenness had become actual. She was carrying a tablet and she had the look of someone who had been interrupted.

She came to Dez's side. Dez lowered the window.

"Naila send you."

"Yes."

"Going to Larch."

"Yes."

The woman looked across at Kirin. Not at Kirin's face — at her coat, at the shape of her inside pocket, at the way Kirin was sitting in her seat.

"Who's the passenger."

"She has her own business east."

"I can see she has business east. That's not what I asked."

Dez looked at Kirin. The look said: *your call.*

"Kirin Saleh. Materials analysis. Sable River."

The woman wrote something on the tablet with her thumb — the motion of someone with a callus built specifically for that task. She looked back at Kirin.

"You carrying something from the Adaeze line."

The name in that mouth, in that hollow, hit Kirin in the sternum.

"I'm carrying my own property."

"That's what I asked."

The woman studied her for a count of three. Then she stepped back from the vehicle and said, at a volume meant to carry to the people at the flatbed, "It's Naila's crew. Let them through."

* * *

The yard was graveled with the same systematic intent as the road outside. Dez parked near the flatbed. Kirin got out and stayed by the door.

The lubricant drums were industrial capacity. She read the lot codes from habit — manufacturing lot, batch date, transport registry. The transport registry was a seven-character code she did not recognize. Not Compact standard format. Not the Free Zone format she had been trained to cross-reference at the Board for cross-border materials assessment. Something else.

She was standing three feet from a forty-liter drum reading its lot code when a voice behind her said, "It's lubricant."

She turned. A man her age or a little older, lean with the forearm build of sustained manual work. His patch was different from the others — both shoulders, different color ground. He had grease to his elbows. He had been under the flatbed a moment ago.

"I know it's lubricant," she said.

"And yet."

"The transport registry is a seven-character code I don't have in my references."

He looked at the drum. He looked at her. He said, in a tone that was carefully neutral, "Where did you say you were from."

"Materials analysis. Sable River CapEx Board."

He went around the vehicle to where Dez was talking with the woman from inside and said something at a low register. The woman looked at Kirin again with the same reading-surface assessment.

Kirin stayed where she was. The key was warm.

It had been warm since they turned into the yard, and it was not cycling. A sustained, open warmth she felt the way she felt a minor pain — not sharp, just present. She put her hand in her pocket and curled her fingers around it. The contact pads on the back face were warm the way a circuit board was warm after operation. Not the way a body was warm.

She looked up and found the antenna on the building's roof.

The antenna was not still. Not weather-adjustment tracking — the arc it moved through was too small, too regular. It was tracking the yard. Something in the yard was responding to something the antenna was looking for.

Dez came around the vehicle. She said, quietly, "They want to see what the key does with their interface. I told them you'd need a minute first."

"I don't need a minute."

"All right." Dez did not argue. "The interface is inside. Back wall of the long building."

"What do they want from the test."

"Confirmation of authentication class. They've had three objects like yours come through this corridor in four years. None of them were tested because the carriers wouldn't allow it." She stopped. She looked at Kirin without the careful neutrality she'd been maintaining since Salt, something more direct underneath it. "They are also going to learn the direction of the terminal address. That is worth knowing. You may not want them to know it too. Make that decision in the room."

Kirin looked at the antenna on the roof, still tracking its small arc.

"Come on," she said.


* * *

The inside of the building was a workshop and staging room. Tool racks on the far wall. Two worktables with current projects — a disassembled pump on one, a length of relay cable being spliced on the other. A board on the side wall with maps pinned to it, most of the map surface covered over with sheets of paper that had notes in a cramped hand Kirin could not read from the doorway. A smell of mineral oil and old solvent and the faint residual warmth of a wood fire that had gone out an hour ago.

A woman was at the pump table.

She was in her forties, compact and self-contained, with gray-streaked hair pulled back with the kind of mechanical clip used to bundle cable. She was fitting a worn seal into the pump body with a tool that looked made for the purpose — a length of steel rod with a flattened head, filed down to fit a specific groove. She did not look up when Kirin came in.

"This is the one from Naila," said the woman with the tablet, behind Kirin.

"I can tell." The woman at the pump held the seal up to the light. She set the pump down. She stood and turned toward Kirin. She was a head shorter and she looked at her with the same flat, properties-reading assessment Kirin had been getting from everyone in this hollow — not at her face, but at her coat, at her hands, at how she was carrying her weight. "Interface is on the back wall. The panel with the yellow stripe at the top. Don't touch anything else on the panel. Put the key to the lower right corner — there's a reader window with a vertical line. Hold it there until something happens or nothing happens. Ten seconds, no response, take it away."

She had not said her name.

"What are you looking for," Kirin said.

"I'll tell you after."

Kirin looked at Dez, who had followed her in and was standing at the wall near the door with her arms at her sides.

The interface panel on the back wall was the maintenance type she had seen in the Board's infrastructure photo references — used for direct hardware access to grid equipment. Green status lights on one edge. The yellow stripe at the top was the standard *certified personnel only* marking. The reader window in the lower right corner was a small recessed square with a faint vertical line bisecting it.

She crossed the room. She took out the key.

It was already warm — warmer than the sustained warm of the yard, closer to the sharp heat at the overpass, focused. She pressed it flat against the reader window, contact-pads down, the way the four raised squares sat in the corners of the back face.

The key went very still.

Then the panel changed. Two of the green status lights shifted to amber. A readout on a small screen above the panel lit with a string of alphanumerics and she was reading it — her materials-analyst's eye taking in the character positions as fast as her eyes could move — when the woman crossed the room in four fast steps and killed the panel with a switch at the bottom left.

The room went quiet.

The woman stood with her hand on the switch and her back to Kirin for two counts. Then she turned around.

She looked at Kirin differently than she had ten seconds ago. Not warmer. More attentive, which in this room seemed to amount to the same thing.

"That's a live Rootstock bearer chit," she said. "Legacy authentication protocol. Active and current."

"I know."

"You know it. I wanted to see the confirmation code myself, and I got a partial. Enough." She crossed to the worktable and took a data chip from a small tray beside the pump body and set it on the table. "The partial is on that. An authentication record fragment and the directional address of your key's registered node. Do you know what's at those coordinates."

"I know the coordinates."

"I asked whether you know what's there."

Kirin held still.

"No."

The woman looked at her. The look had the quality of someone deciding how much of a particular truth to spend on a single conversation.

"We don't either," she said. "Not exactly. We know it exists. We know the general type of thing it is. The details of the terminal address are what we've been working toward through three bearer chits in four years." She picked up the chip and held it out. "This is yours. We pulled it from your key; it belongs with the key."

Kirin took it.

The woman with the tablet spoke from the end of the room. "Her name is Yusra. She doesn't give it easily. I'm telling you so you can use it."

The woman — Yusra — looked at the tablet-carrier with the expression of someone accepting a recurring, minor inconvenience. Then she looked back at Kirin.

"Sit down," she said. "I'm going to tell you about the road you've been on."

* * *

Far north of the corridor, in a pressurized communications shelter mounted on the back of a maintenance truck, a man looked at a photograph.

The photograph had come from a Compact transit registry — an exit stamp issued at the frontier depot at Kesmark, eleven days ago, on materials-assessment credentials. The type of photograph taken at a Guild certification ceremony: the subject looking at a fixed point to the left of the camera as instructed, with the stiff formality of a person who has been told to look natural and concluded that looking natural is not an option.

He had been looking at it for some time.

On the screen beside it: a partial intercept from the corridor. A live-authentication ping from a Rootstock bearer chit, source location approximate, sector four, thirty-seven minutes ago.

He had been expecting a ping from sector four since the Kesmark exit stamp. The route from Kesmark east — the route the chit's authentication tree pointed toward — ran through sector four, and there was nothing on that route you could use to avoid giving the grid a ping if your key was active. What he had not expected was for the ping to originate from inside Larch depot.

He studied the intercept coordinates. He studied the photograph.

He typed a request on the comm panel. *Request additional credentialed operator, sector four, primary intercept corridor.* Not *send a team.* The difference between those two formulations was the difference between a problem that could be resolved quietly and one that could not, and his professional interest lay firmly with the former.

The reply came back in four minutes.

*Operator available. Transit time 72 hours. Confirm priority.*

He confirmed priority.

He added one line: *Do not approach Larch. The facility is complicated.*

Whoever was reading his request on the other end knew what *complicated* meant when it was applied to Larch depot. If they didn't know yet, they would find out. Either way it was not his problem today.

He folded the photograph once along the horizontal and put it in his breast pocket. He closed the comm panel. Outside the shelter, the truck's engine was already running.

* * *

Yusra did not sit.

She stood at the worktable with the repaired pump seal between her fingers and talked the way she did everything else — direct, without prefacing, as if she expected the listener to keep up.

"The corridor from Kesmark east is divided into managed sectors. Eight of them, roughly forty miles each, not equal in content or in who runs them, some overlap at the edges. The sector you've been in since this morning is sector four." She looked at the seal. Set it down. "Sector four is complicated."

"Three factions," Kirin said.

Yusra looked at her.

"Naila mentioned factions."

"Naila mentioned factions because they are the simplest true thing to say about it." Yusra picked the seal back up. It was, Kirin had started to understand, a thinking gesture — not displacement, but the habit of a person who thought better when her hands were occupied. "Three factions maintain sector four. I can show you the patches for one. The second you've probably seen in infrastructure reference materials if you ever did cross-border assessment work at the Board. The third I can describe, and then you can decide what to do with the description."

"Show me."

The man with the different-color patches had come inside at some point during the key test and gone to the far worktable, working on the relay cable. He looked up at *show me* and came over. He turned his left shoulder toward Kirin.

The patch was a stylized wave in two colors — designed to be identifiable at fifty meters and unreadable from further. "That's an Outside maintenance patch," Yusra said. "Contracted through an Outside logistics network that has been operating in the corridor since 2112. Fourteen years. They maintain roads, utilities, water systems. They do not have Compact authorization to be here. They do not request it."

The woman with the tablet turned her own shoulder. Different patch — a geometric form rather than a wave, simpler, utilitarian.

"This is the second faction," Yusra said. "Compact internal maintenance. Shadow budget, not on the standard public ledger, run out of the infrastructure division under a project code that does not appear in any Board posting. Three crews in this sector. They have been here at least as long as the Outside crews, and they are aware of each other, and they do not speak to each other, and neither faction reports the other's existence to anyone, because reporting would require each of them to acknowledge that neither of them is supposed to be here."

"And the third."

Yusra put the seal in her coat pocket.

"The third you already know about. The one Naila mentioned." She looked at Kirin with the flat steadiness that seemed to be her default. "The Curator doesn't maintain the corridor. The Curator manages what the corridor is used for. The first two factions keep the road. The third faction uses the road — specifically, to control access to a set of coordinates that certain bearer chits in the Rootstock legacy protocol are authenticated for."

Kirin thought about the road she had walked that morning. About the sight-line clearance that extended twelve feet from the verge and no further. About the spillway walls, re-pointed to a standard that nobody in the Compact was paying for. About the maintenance drone that had followed her for half a mile on the corridor road the week before and then broken off. All of it was furniture in someone's house. Not abandoned infrastructure running on inertia — a managed space, kept to a purpose she hadn't known to look for.

"How do they enforce access," she said.

"Selectively." Yusra looked at the board on the side wall — at the paper covering the maps. "The Curator's curation is not a wall. There is no gate. People walk through this corridor every week. Travelers, scavengers, people moving between the Free Zones and the Compact by the back way. Most of them arrive at Larch and continue east, and nothing happens to them, because they are not carrying a bearer chit in the Adaeze authentication line."

"And the ones who are."

"There have been four, that I know of, in the last two years. One came through here with a chit and went east and we have not heard from them since. One was turned around before they reached Larch — we found their gear on the spur road. One was Renata." Yusra was quiet for a moment. "And one is you."

The room was quiet.

"Your coordinates," said the woman with the tablet.

"You know the coordinates."

"We know the direction. We know the corridor. We have never had a live bearer chit in this room until today." Yusra looked at the data chip in Kirin's hand. "We have a partial authentication record now. Enough to know that you are carrying something the Curator has been blocking access to for at least a decade. Enough to know something else as well." She paused. She looked at Kirin the way she had looked at the pump seal before she fitted it. "When your key came through this morning, it responded at three separate nodes on the maintenance spur. Two short cycles. One long. The third one — the overpass at mile forty-four — held for a count of seven."

Kirin held still.

"You timed it," she said.

"The node logs it. I read the log when you pulled into the yard." Yusra looked at her. "The overpass node is not ours. It is not the Outside crew's. It is a Curator sensor installation in a Compact-issued infrastructure housing that the Compact shadow crew installed and the Curator runs a secondary tap into. When your key held at that node, it was because that node asked for a longer authentication sequence, and the key gave it."

The room was very quiet for a moment.

"The Curator's network knows the key is here," Kirin said.

"The Curator's array logged a live-key response at the overpass node. Whether that information has reached a person who can act on it depends on how often their people check sector four logs." Yusra looked at her. "The answer is regularly. Sector four is not a low-priority sector for them."

"How long before someone comes to Larch."

"Two hours from a standing deployment. Three if they have to mobilize from base." Yusra glanced at the board on the side wall, at something beneath the paper covering the maps. "It has been thirty-seven minutes since the overpass."

Kirin looked at Dez. Dez had not moved from the wall near the door. She met Kirin's look with nothing.

"You know a route they won't have covered," Kirin said to Yusra.

"Yes."

"And you're going to tell me about it."

"Yes. After we talk about what it costs."

Kirin was quiet.

The man with the Outside patches had gone back to the relay cable at the far worktable. The woman with the tablet was standing near the door as if she might need to use it quickly. The smell of mineral oil and old solvent and cold ash sat undisturbed in the room. Nobody was rushing. This was, Kirin understood, not a place where people rushed — it was a place where people had learned that rushing was what got you dead at the overpass, and that a clear price established in advance was the alternative.

"Tell me the route first," she said.

Yusra looked at her with something that was not respect exactly but was in the same family.

"North door out of this building. Track through the alder stand, three hundred meters, then east on a ground-level maintenance path that connects to the secondary agricultural grid. No Compact sensor coverage on that path. No Curator sensor coverage either. The path connects to the main corridor road at a junction we call Harrow Creek, forty miles east of here." She put her hand in her coat pocket. "The Curator's people, when they come to Larch, will come to the main spur junction. You will not be there."

"What's at Harrow Creek."

"A junction. Nothing else."

"Who do we contact at Harrow Creek."

"Nobody. Harrow Creek is a handoff point, not a manned station. There is a marker at the junction — a concrete post with a yellow band. You leave a description of yourself on a chip under the cap of the post. Within four hours, someone will come."

"From where."

"From east." Yusra looked at her. "That's the shape of how it works. I don't name the east end to people I don't know."

Kirin accepted that. She looked at the data chip in her hand.

"And the price."

"The chip stays here."

Dez moved at the wall — a controlled stillness, which was worse than a step.

Kirin held the chip up between two fingers. It was small. It was the size of a thumbnail. What was on it was a partial authentication record that pointed in a direction she already knew, and pointed there less precisely than the key itself. The key would tell her more at the right interface. The chip told them something, but it did not tell them the terminal address. She had confirmed that herself.

"You said the chip has a directional address," she said. "Not the terminal address."

"Correct."

"So it tells you the general corridor but not the endpoint."

"That's right."

"And the key tells me more than the chip tells you."

Yusra did not answer. The not-answering was itself an answer.

Kirin looked at the chip.

She thought about the woman at mile twenty-two. She thought about Bex standing in the dark hall at Salt without coming through the door. She thought about the overpass node asking the key for a longer authentication sequence, and the key giving it, and a man thirty-seven minutes ago looking at a photograph of her Guild certification ceremony and typing something into a comm panel on a truck pointed north.

She put the chip on the worktable.

"Done," she said.

Yusra did not pick it up immediately. She looked at Kirin for a moment with the flat, assessing steadiness she had maintained since Kirin walked in.

"You'll want provisions for forty miles on the ground path," she said. "The path is slower than the spur. Allow six hours."

"We have a vehicle."

"You're leaving the vehicle here."

Dez straightened at the wall. "That vehicle belongs to Naila."

"Naila's vehicle is four-wheel and wide and will leave track on the path that a Compact survey drone can read from altitude. We'll hold it for her." Yusra said this without apology. She looked at Dez. "We have two packs and a light two-wheel frame that can carry your gear. It is not comfortable. It is not loud."

"You had this prepared," Dez said.

"I had the possibility prepared. When the key logged at the overpass, the possibility became a plan." Yusra picked up the chip and put it in her inside pocket. "I'll get the packs."

* * *

They were through the north door twenty minutes later.

The man with the Outside patches had loaded the frame with their gear from the vehicle, and he had done it in the compressed, exact way of someone who had loaded gear under time pressure before — no wasted motions, nothing double-handled, everything balanced over the axle. The frame was a simple single-axle thing with two poles for carrying handles. It weighed nothing empty. It weighed a manageable amount loaded. The man handed the front pole to Dez and the rear to Kirin and nodded once and went back inside.

The alder stand was three hundred meters on a path that had been cut to width some time ago and had been mostly maintained since, the alder reaching over from both sides but not yet closing. The light in the stand was the flat gray-green of filtered spring. The path was damp. It had been walked recently — Kirin could see heel prints in the softer ground beside the path's hard center, multiple sets, various sizes, over the last few weeks.

Dez walked without speaking.

Kirin walked and carried the rear pole and thought about the chip on the worktable.

She had given it without deliberating long enough to second-guess herself, which was either good judgment or the absence of good judgment and she could not yet tell which. What she had was this: Yusra had given her a route, and provisions, and a contact protocol at Harrow Creek, and the specific intelligence about node three — the Curator's tap, the longer handshake, the fact that the key had given the authentication sequence willingly. That last thing was worth more than the partial directional address on the chip. It told her the key's behavior was not random. It told her the key knew who was asking.

She had not said this to Yusra. Yusra had not asked.

The path turned east at the three-hundred-meter mark, as advertised, and came out of the alder into a long corridor of former agricultural fields managed to a low grass. The sky opened. A bird somewhere in the grass made a sound like a question mark. The two-wheel frame rolled easily on the path's center.

"You could have argued for the route without giving the chip," Dez said.

"Probably."

"You didn't."

"No."

Dez walked for a moment. "Why."

"Because she knew about node three before she asked for the chip. She knew the key held for a count of seven. She had been logging that node for four years without knowing what the count meant." Kirin adjusted her grip on the pole. "I told her the three responses were different. She already knew they were different. She just wanted confirmation that I knew they were different. The chip was the price of the confirmation. The route was the price of the chip."

Dez turned her head and looked at Kirin over her shoulder. The look lasted for two steps.

"You're learning to trade," she said.

"I'm learning what I don't know. It's the same thing."

Dez made the file-update sound and looked back at the path ahead of them.

* * *

They walked for three hours before Kirin allowed herself to think about the key.

It had been quiet since the panel test. The sustained warmth gone. The cycling gone. Whatever it had been doing since Salt — the handshakes with the maintenance nodes, the warm-and-cool of the road's underlying infrastructure — it had stopped. It sat in her pocket like an inert piece of metal the size of a coin. She had checked it twice, not counting, just touching it, the way she touched samples at the Board when the assay results did not make sense and she needed to start over from the object itself.

Cold. Still.

She understood, by the third hour, what the silence meant.

The maintenance path the Outside crew maintained ran on a different substrate than the main spur. It was a ground-level track rather than a surfaced road. The maintenance nodes were spaced for the spur, not for the path. She had been walking for three hours outside the coverage grid, and the key had nothing to handshake with.

The grid had a shape. The key knew the shape. She was now learning the shape too, by negative space — by where the warmth went absent.

She had been a materials analyst. She had read surfaces for a living. She had been very good at it, and she had understood it as a skill and not as a posture, a way of looking at things directly rather than inferring them from secondary sources. She was doing that now. She was reading a corridor by its infrastructure the way she had read a steel sample by its grain.

The thought of Hasse came and went. Hasse at the Board, forty-one years in materials analysis, holding her key for a long time and saying: *I've seen one of these before. Once. A long time ago. The person who brought it never came back.*

She put the thought down.

The path ran on through the long flat fields. The sky in the east had begun to clear — the gray lifting, blue coming up at the edge. Ahead, somewhere in the distance she could not yet resolve, the main corridor road ran east-west through the landscape like a ruling on a map, and at the junction point a concrete post with a yellow band was waiting.

Dez was ahead of her on the poles, a steady figure in a canvas jacket, walking at a pace that covered ground without announcing urgency. Kirin watched her for a moment. She had been watching her for three days now. She was not closer to knowing what Dez was watching for. She knew that Dez had eight years in this corridor. She knew Dez had worked all the standing factions. She knew Dez had driven her east out of Salt because Naila asked, and that Naila had been precise about the terms: *her interest does not require her to do you wrong this week.*

This week. The week was almost up.

She would ask about the next week when the next week arrived. That was the shape of how it worked, apparently.

"Seen anything on the wing mirror today," Dez said, without turning. The phrasing was deliberate — wing mirror, from the vehicle, as if the habit of watching had survived the vehicle.

"Nothing," Kirin said.

"Good."

"Is it."

Dez considered. "It means they don't know about the path. Or they haven't reached Larch yet. Or both." She paused. "Or they know about the path and they're watching from altitude, which we would not be able to see."

"That's comforting."

"It's accurate," Dez said. "Accurate is what I have."

Kirin adjusted her grip on the rear pole. The frame rolled on.

The path began a long shallow rise toward a tree line at the crest. Through the trees, on the far side, the corridor road would be there. The junction. The post with the yellow band. The four-hour wait. Someone coming from east.

She thought about the data chip on the worktable at Larch depot, and Yusra putting it in her inside pocket, and the partial authentication record on it pointing in a direction Yusra now knew. She thought about what Yusra would do with that direction. She thought about the woman with the tablet and the man with the Outside patches and the way the depot had been provisioned and positioned — not as a checkpoint, but as a node in something larger. A something larger that had been running in this corridor for fourteen years, at minimum, on a substrate that the Compact's official picture of the world did not include.

The key was cold.

She reached the crest of the rise. Through the trees, in the late-afternoon light, the road lay below them — smooth, straight, running east through the flat former fields toward a distance that had no end she could see from here. At the junction, twenty meters from where they stood, a concrete post stood with a yellow band.

Dez set down her end of the poles.

"That's it," she said.

They stood at the crest for a moment, looking at the road. The light was good. The road was empty in both directions as far as either of them could see, which was not the same as safe but was not nothing.

"I need to know one thing," Kirin said.

"Ask it."

"When you worked with the other factions — the Compact shadow crew, the Curator's people — did you work with them because they paid you, or did you work with them because you needed to be in the corridor and working with them was the price."

Dez did not answer immediately. She looked at the road.

"Both," she said. "The distinction stops mattering after a while."

"Has it stopped mattering for you."

Dez picked up her end of the poles.

"Not yet," she said. "Come on."

They went down the rise toward the road and the post with the yellow band, and the sky in the east had gone fully blue, and the last light of the afternoon lay long across the fields.

The post was exactly where Yusra had said it would be. Concrete, standard Compact highway-marking diameter, with a yellow band at shoulder height and a removable cap at the top. Kirin had carried a data chip from Larch — one Yusra had given her specifically for this, blank, with a stylus already loaded to write. She wrote five words on the chip: *two, eastbound, Naila's account.* She put it under the cap and pressed the cap down until it seated.

Dez set down her end of the poles and stretched her back. She looked east down the road.

"Four hours," she said.

"Yusra said four hours."

"Yusra said within four hours. The within is the variable."

They settled at the base of the post, packs against the concrete, and ate from the provisions Yusra's crew had packed — dried meat, flat bread, a block of hard cheese that had been vacuum-sealed at some point in the last three months. The food was plain and sufficient. Kirin ate without tasting it.

The road was empty in both directions. The grass on the verge ticked in the small wind off the fields. A large bird — she had no name for it, she had not grown up near open country — settled on a utility pole two hundred meters west and sat there against the sky, still and patient in the way of birds that were good at waiting.

The key was cold in her pocket.

She was beginning to understand that the key's silences were as specific as its warmth. In the Compact, in Sable River, it had been inert — a piece of old metal in a dead-technology format that nothing in the Compact was built to read. The moment she crossed the frontier, it had begun to find things to respond to. It was calibrated to this corridor. It knew where it was being carried, and it behaved accordingly.

The overpass at mile forty-four had asked for a long authentication. The key had given it without her telling it to. The key was not an object she was using. It was an object with its own relationships to the infrastructure around it, and she was the current carrier.

Her grandmother had carried it for thirty-five years. Her grandmother had brought it east in September of last year, six months before she died, and had confirmed one specific thing about one specific route, and had gone home.

Kirin looked east down the road.

The bird on the utility pole lifted, without apparent cause, and went east in a low direct line until it was gone.

"Someone's coming," Dez said.

She had not looked up from her food.

"How do you know."

"The bird didn't circle. Birds that are just leaving circle once first." Dez finished her bread and wrapped the remainder of the cheese with the mechanical efficiency of someone who had been eating in fields for a long time. "Birds that leave because something is moving behind them go straight."

Kirin looked east. Nothing yet. The road was still and clear and going a long way into the blue distance, and somewhere along it, not yet visible, something was moving toward them.

She put her hand in her pocket around the key.

Still cold. Still present.

She waited.
