# Writer Notes — Episode 9: "Landfall"

## What I Was Going For

The season is past its chapter break — Kirin is inside the Outside, and the episode needed to deliver two things simultaneously: the texture of the world she's entered, and the recontextualization that's been building since the archive's third instruction. The third instruction was always "bring the key to the plateau and let what happens happen," not "find Nnedi." Adaeze wrote it in shorthand because the full version was harder to write and would have sounded like a prophecy. The shorthand let Kirin carry a deliverable-shaped goal for twelve hundred miles. The envelope corrects this. The correction is harder than the original instruction.

Nnedi's death is the structural surprise. It needed to land as information, not as grief, because grief would be wrong — Kirin never knew Nnedi. The surprise is operational: the instruction has to be reinterpreted without the person it named. The episode is about Kirin absorbing that reinterpretation and continuing anyway, which she does, because the letter says she'll know what Adaeze meant when she arrives, and Kirin is a person who has been walking toward things she didn't fully understand for two months and has gotten good at that.

## Ade

The cold open is Ade's POV because the Outside's relay network is infrastructure the listener needs to understand, and the best way to show infrastructure is to show it working from the inside. Ade is not a guide and not warm. She is a logistics person who runs a relay network and tells Kirin what Kirin needs to know in the order she needs to know it. The "no urgency" code — meaning something is in motion, not collapsing — is borrowed from how real relay systems worked historically. The Outside's administrative seriousness is consistent with Ep 8 (Crucita's intake process). Different flavor: Crucita is procedural, Ade is operational.

## Lupe and Saren

Two characters from the same settlement with radically different relationships to the information they hold. Lupe is the structural one — the resource governance coordinator who explains the cedar grove resolution process because it's relevant to the settlement and to Kirin's operative question ("how do decisions get made here"). He was Compact-origin (Keeper class, CapEx Board) and has been here seventeen years. His answer to "which argument wins" is "both" — not as a dodge but as a genuine account of how the settlement's process works differently from the Board's.

Saren is older and has a different relationship to the history. She knew Adaeze for twenty-two years. Her account of Adaeze — respected and inconvenient, competent rather than warm — is the closest thing to a portrait Kirin has gotten from an outside source, and it matches the log. Her account of Nnedi is the non-sentimental answer the outline required: Nnedi was effective, difficult, divisive, right about most things and wrong about how to be right. The community persists despite her as much as because of her. People who wouldn't speak at her burial rites and people who traveled three days to be there, both with reasons.

## Berin Kosta

The outline required him to appear in Ep 9 per the arc tracker. The interlude keeps him short — he's an independent thread, not yet woven into Kirin's story, and this is his introduction. Key notes: he's been inside fourteen months, he found the archive node, found it already inventoried, stayed to fix the cataloguing system. The cataloguing work is genuine — not a metaphor for anything, just the actual work that was there. He's Compact-trained in the general way, not specifically archival. He's good at it without knowing he was going to be good at it before he stayed. His exterior: the Outside's sky, unlatched window. He's oriented. His decision to wait one week before coming is accurate and responsible, not delay — there's specific work that losing continuity on would cost more than waiting would.

## The Cedar Grove

A dispute about something small that is also about something large: the difference between documentation and communication, between a process that decides and a process that works through. Not resolved in this episode — the reparation proposal is just being made as Kirin leaves. The grove is two things at once (windbreak and habitat) and you can only see that from the right angle. That's doing double duty without being a metaphor.

## The Envelope

Adaeze's letter is in her flat, operational voice. Three paragraphs, no sentiment. It corrects the instruction to its actual form, gives the practical information about Nnedi and the key protocol (ask to see her key first, let her offer), and closes with "the road is long" — the phrase that has been traveling through the series since Mira's account of the song in Ep 2. Here it's Adaeze writing it to herself as much as to the bearer: acknowledgment that the road is long and she has been on it for thirty-five years and this is the end of her part of it.

## What I'm Setting Up

Ep 10 needs to deliver on:
- Rua and the plateau settlement
- Nnedi's key — who has it or where it is
- The "two keys in the same room" event that Adaeze's letter promises will mean something
- Berin Kosta's arrival (in about a week, if he leaves when he said he would)
- "Adaeze's people" — the community that persists, the woman who was Adaeze's closest friend, the man who was her adversary, the granddaughter of the child in the photograph

Ep 10 also needs to stage the decision: Kirin has to decide whether to accept the role she was offered. The outline puts this in Ep 11 (accepting after the briefing), but the offer itself comes from the plateau community. The plateau should feel specific and unidealized — Nnedi's shadow is there without sentimentality.

## The Third Instruction

"Let what happens happen" is the hardest kind of instruction to give and to receive. Adaeze held the line for thirty-five years, specifically preventing the EQU-convertibility play from getting the key. She is dead. The holding is over. The key now needs to be in the presence of whatever the plateau has been building toward. Kirin doesn't know what that is. She is going to find out by arriving. This is consistent with Kirin's mode throughout the series: she reads surfaces, not interiors, and she reads what's in front of her. The plateau will be in front of her.

## What I'm Worried About

The landing line — "the road is long, the end: not yet" — is close to a thematic statement. I left it because it's a phrase that's been doing work throughout the series (the song, Perun's message, Adaeze's letter) and landing the episode on it is earned repetition rather than thematic pinning. But it needs to work as a sentence, not as a thesis. If it reads as conclusion rather than continuation, cut it or push it to action: *She walked.* The episode's job is to send the listener toward the plateau, not to summarize what the journey has been.

Saren could run long — she's a character who could tell Kirin everything or nothing, and I gave her the middle ground. The risk is that she comes across as the "friendly stranger" reflex — Outside elder delivers wisdom to Compact traveler. She's not delivering wisdom. She's delivering specific information about two specific people she knew, with the specific mixed feelings that come from twenty-two years of watching someone do necessary and occasionally irritating work. Keep her grounded in the specific: Adaeze coming through four times, asking the specific questions she asked, staying the specific number of nights.
