# Episode 9: "Landfall"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Five days into the Outside, Kirin arrives at the first settlement and finds that the woman her grandmother told her to find died two months before she got there.*

---

The relay came in three days after Crucita sent it from the maritime facility.

Ade read it at the routing desk before the settlement's morning was fully underway — the particular quality of light in the east windows that she had learned to read as early, not by color but by angle. The facts: a bearer, Compact-origin, had crossed at the standard maritime gate. Key authenticated. Primary referral: Adaeze Okonkwo. Naila Brace as secondary.

She read it twice. The first time for content. The second time for what the content implied.

She had asked Crucita to flag Okonkwo-line transits eight months ago, after the third carrier in the line turned back west without completing the crossing and the fourth had not materialized. She had entered the flag into the transit facility's referral system — which was a paper ledger and Crucita's memory, two reliable things — and had received nothing against it for the first four months. She had stopped checking daily. She had started checking weekly. Then less than weekly. Then only when something in a corridor relay reminded her to look.

She wrote the dispatch in three lines. The code the interior chain used for a bearer in transit: the referral names, the arrival window, and the close — *no urgency,* which in the relay's vocabulary meant the situation was in motion but not collapsing, and input would be needed without immediate crisis requiring it. She called for the morning runner.

Ori was fourteen and ran the first link of the chain before the school session started. He had been running it for eleven months. He took the dispatch with the competence of a person who had been given real responsibility and chose to take it seriously. He was gone before she could say more.

She went to make tea.

Through the east windows: the settlement coming into its morning. The communal kitchen had its door propped — she could smell the first grain batch on the air when conditions were exactly right, and conditions were exactly right this morning. The managed plot's eastern section already had three people working, and, at the cedar grove's edge, two more people standing in a configuration she recognized. The same conversation as yesterday. Day four.

She had opinions about the cedar grove situation and was keeping them to herself. She was on the management committee. Her function was to evaluate the reparation proposal when it arrived, not to arrive at the meeting having already decided. She could hold both — opinions and function — so long as she did not confuse them. She had been doing this long enough to know where that line was.

She thought briefly about Berin Kosta. The relay would reach him at the archive node in two or three days, weather permitting. She had not thought about him in several months, which meant the work at the node was going well. People she needed to think about frequently were people for whom things were not going well.

She went back to her desk.

She had routing work to do.

* * *

The junction posts were eye height and marked with a glyph the clay discs could read.

The first one confirmed on the second morning, thirty meters from the transit gate. She stood with the sixth disc in her palm, back-face label matching the post's stamped glyph exactly, the direction clear. She took it.

She had expected the system to work and it worked anyway. The expected and the experienced were different things. A tool doing what it was built to do, in the absence of the person who built it, produced a specific satisfaction that she had not been anticipating and that had no good name. She filed the feeling and kept walking.

The first day was coastal. The sea audible and then less audible as she moved north and then east and then northeast, following the track that Crucita had described: four kilometers along the coast, then inland on the maintained surface. The track was not maintained the way the corridor's managed spur was maintained — no fresh resurfacing, no drainage cut to specification, no contractor signature in the slope angles. Maintained the way a thing was maintained when people needed it navigable and kept it so by using it. The surface clear of growth. The verge held back by passing feet. Footwork as infrastructure.

She read the discs and took the directions.

The second day the terrain changed. The coastal species fell back and the elevation began — a long, gradual rise, the kind that was in your legs before you registered it in the view. The air dried. The light changed quality. The plant life changed: taller scrub species, the edge of low woodland beginning, a different set of colors in the canopy. Not managed woodland. Not the corridor's curated maintenance. Growth where conditions allowed it, in the specific irregular distribution of a thing that had no foreman. Random in the way that nature was not actually random but looked it, from a vantage that had spent its time in systems with design logic.

She read the discs. She took the directions.

The third day: rain.

A cold front from the northwest, moving with the specific speed of a coastal front that had open water behind it and nothing in its way. She found shelter in the cavity of a long windbreak hedge — not planned shelter, just a hedge large enough and dense enough on the downwind side to turn the weather into sound rather than wet. She set the portable heater between her pack and the hedge's inner wall. She ate from provisions. She opened the log.

She was in 2124. Adaeze at seventy-three. The entries were shorter than the years around them — not deteriorating, just compressed, the way a practice compressed when it had been running long enough to stop requiring preamble. A March entry tracked a shift in the Curator's patrol rotation, noted without comment. Two May entries covered the eastern junction components, sectors six and eight: *both flagged, no replacement work order in current period, decline rate consistent with prior projection.* Below-spec components, logged annually since 2098. Twenty-six years of ratings. The Compact's infrastructure board had never responded to any of them. The Compact's infrastructure board did not have these sectors on its current active map.

Six to eleven million people. Dez's revised projection: eighteen months at the short end if the next winter ran heavy.

The rain outside the hedge ran for six hours. She slept through the second half of it and woke to a changed sky.

The fourth day: fences.

She came over a low rise and saw the first fence line at the base of the slope — post-and-wire, tensioned correctly, the posts set at angles that would not shift in hard weather. Someone built this and was maintaining it. Past the fence: fields in their post-harvest state, the stalks dry, the ground turned and resting, a gravity-fed water pipe running the length of the nearest row from a concrete tank positioned two hundred meters uphill. The tank was concrete. The pipe was something local — a flexible conduit she did not recognize, the joining technology not Compact-standard and not Free Zone standard. Third kind.

She followed the fence east until it turned south toward a building she could see but was not yet approaching. The disc confirmed the direction: northeast and away from the building. Not yet there.

She took the direction.

That evening she camped in the lee of a long stone wall — the remains of some pre-collapse agricultural infrastructure, repaired in sections where it had fallen and let go where the repair was not worth it. Not sentimental maintenance. The judgment of people who had limited hands and finite time and knew the difference between what needed holding and what could be let go.

She ate. She read the log. She did not open the envelope.

On the fifth day, in the early afternoon, she came down a long slope of recovering scrubland and saw the second way-station below.

Not a compound. A cluster of buildings that had grown from a single original structure over years of adding what was needed: an industrial-height main building, a smaller building attached at its south wall, a third set at a right angle from the second, and between them a yard organized with the logic she had been learning to read since the transit facility's supply racks. Not freight management. Community management. The difference was in the spacing — not packed for throughput, arranged to be found.

The key intensified.

Not authentication-warmth. Not the building-toward-reading warmth of the managed spur. The settled warmth it had taken on when she stepped through the transit gate, four days south, had increased — concentrated, as if the network she was now inside had more presence here than it had had on the open ground. More nodes. More density of whatever the key was receiving as signal.

She put her hand in her pocket and held it through the fabric.

She went down.

* * *

She asked for Ade at the entrance.

A young person assessed her — the professional inventory she had been receiving at every threshold since Kesmark: pack, boots, the condition of her hands, the gear arrangement on her shoulder straps — and went into the building and came back with a woman in her forties who moved with the settled efficiency of someone who had made a decision about efficiency a long time ago and was not revisiting it.

"You're the bearer," she said.

"Yes."

"The key."

Kirin opened her jacket pocket and held the key flat in her palm. Ade looked at the casing — not at Kirin's face. At the key's physical form. She studied it with the focused attention of someone comparing what was in front of her against what she had seen before.

"Third-generation rivet configuration," she said. "I've seen two others." She looked at Kirin. "Come in. I'm Ade."

The routing desk was at the east end of the building. Afternoon light through the windows. Ade cleared the working surface in three deliberate movements — relay dispatches sorted to one side, a rolled map relocated, a cup moved to the shelving — and sat. There was a chair across the desk. Kirin sat in it.

"Crucita's message came in yesterday," Ade said. "Berin Kosta is at the archive node in sector twelve. The relay went out this morning — two or three days to reach him, depending on conditions." She looked at Kirin with the quality of someone giving a briefing to someone she had not met but had been expecting. "He's been inside fourteen months. He knows the interior network better than most people I send relays to. If he comes to you at the way-station or you find your way to the node, either works."

"I need to reach the plateau settlement," Kirin said. "I need to find Nnedi Eze."

The pause was brief. Specific.

"Nnedi won't be there," Ade said. She looked at the desk, not at Kirin. "She died in January. Two months ago."

She said it the way she said everything — as information, delivered because information was what was needed.

Kirin sat with it.

"The plateau is four days northeast," Ade said, continuing as if the pause was administrative rather than significant. "The junction discs take you to the ridge. The descent into the basin is not in the disc notation — it has its own path system. You'll need the route from someone at the settlement here before you go." She paused. "Stay overnight. There are things here that are relevant to why you're going. The route deserves a clear head." She picked up a pen. "The envelope Crucita gave you — read it tonight. Then tell me what you need."

She turned back to the relay log.

Kirin looked at the back of Ade's head for a moment. Then she stood. She went outside and sat on the low wall at the north end of the yard, in the afternoon light coming over the main building's roof.

Around her: the settlement's late-afternoon operation. People crossing the yard with purpose and without it. The kitchen building two hundred meters south, door propped, the smell of the second preparation on the air — something cooked, not baked, warmer and richer than the morning. Through the managed plot's fence, visible from this position: the cedar grove at the plot's eastern edge, the trees at its perimeter bent in the prevailing wind direction. Structural trees. Windbreak species. She could see what they were for just by looking at them.

She took the envelope out of her jacket pocket.

Aged paper. Adaeze's seal: a paper tongue in the flap, functional. Nothing spent on style that wasn't working.

She opened it.

Three paragraphs. Adaeze's handwriting, but different from the log — more open, the lines further apart, composed with time between them rather than at a single sitting.

*To the bearer at the transit gate, September 2125:*

*The plateau settlement is one hundred and twelve miles northeast of the second way-station. The approach drops into a basin — get the route from the settlement before you leave. Nnedi Eze has been on the plateau for forty years. She does not easily trust Compact-origin visitors. She will trust the key. Ask to see her key before you show her yours. Let her make the offer first. The two keys have not been in the same room since 1978, before you were born.*

*The third instruction is not "find Nnedi." That was shorthand for something harder to write. The third instruction is: take the key to the plateau settlement and let what happens happen. I have been preventing one specific thing from happening for thirty-five years. The time for preventing is over. I have completed my term. You are not going to the plateau to deliver a message. You are going because the appointment lives in the key, and the key needs to be in the presence of what is waiting for it. You will know what I mean when you arrive.*

*Come home when it is done. The road is long.*

*— A.O.*

She folded the letter. She held it in her hands.

Around her: the yard continuing. A person with a load of material crossing toward the storage area. Two others coming out of the small attached building in conversation, pausing at the threshold, parting in different directions. The kitchen's smell shifting as the second preparation reached a new stage.

*You will know what I mean when you arrive.*

Not a complete instruction. A promise that completion was findable, if she went. Adaeze had been writing to a bearer she had never met — in September 2125, before she knew which of them would arrive, before she knew if any of them would arrive, given that three carriers before Kirin had not. The instructions had to hold for any bearer. They had to work without assuming foreknowledge.

She put the letter back in the envelope. The envelope back in her pocket, with the key.

She went inside.

* * *

Ade walked her through the settlement's orientation in five minutes: kitchen hours, water access, composting location, where the visitor spaces were in the small attached building. She gave it efficiently, in the order it would be needed, without elaboration. Kirin had met enough people by now to recognize the ones who considered elaboration a form of disrespect — who assumed the person they were briefing was capable and gave them what they needed to act on that assumption.

The visitor space was a room with a bed, a hook on the wall, a window that opened on the managed plot side, and on the ledge a ceramic mug with a smoothed chip in the rim. Someone had kept using the mug past the damage because it still held liquid and the chip was not where your mouth went. She put her pack against the wall and sat on the bed and looked at the window.

Nnedi Eze was dead.

She had been carrying the third instruction as a delivery: find this person, bring her the key, do whatever the meeting required. That was the shape she had given it since the archive, since the log entry — *find Nnedi Eze, she is not a child anymore.* She had been building a picture from scattered evidence: the founder of the plateau settlement, the child in Adaeze's photograph, the holder of a Rootstock key in a different form factor. A person who had been waiting for thirty-five years for what Adaeze had been preventing during the same period.

The letter changed the shape.

*The third instruction is not "find Nnedi."*

The third instruction was: go to where Nnedi had built what she built, with the key, and be in the room when whatever needed to happen happened. Adaeze had known, in September 2125, that Nnedi's health was declining. She might have known Nnedi would not be there when the bearer arrived. She had written the instruction to hold without requiring Nnedi's presence. *You will know what I mean when you arrive.* That sentence was a promise that was still active regardless of whether Nnedi was alive to keep it.

She took out the log. She read two more entries from 2124 — both brief, operational, the water junction tracking for that year's inspection cycle — and put it back. She looked at the window.

Through it: the managed plot in the late-afternoon light, and at the plot's eastern edge the cedar grove. From this angle she could see the grove's full length — a hundred meters or so, and deep enough that the interior trees were in different condition from the perimeter ones. The perimeter trees were bent. Windbreak function. The interior trees were straight, older, growing at a different pace. Not windbreak. Habitat. The grove was two things at once and the household that cut from the perimeter had cut from the structural element without realizing the interior element was what made the structural element worth protecting.

She filed that.

She went down to find the communal kitchen.

* * *

The kitchen was the largest room she had been in since the archive. The same industrial-height ceiling as the main building — maybe connected once, before the settlement grew additions around it. Warm in the specific way of a room that served this purpose for hours daily: cooking system heat and body heat and the warmth of accumulated use, a room that had absorbed the character of its function the way materials absorbed pressure over time. Long tables, people in the end-of-workday transition — some eating, some in conversation, some with small tasks in their hands while their attention was elsewhere.

She got food from the serving counter. She did not recognize the grain preparation. She sat at the end of a long table.

Three other occupants. Two in conversation at the far end, and a man in his sixties near her end, eating with the deliberate pace of someone who took the meal seriously. He noticed her looking at the plate.

"Teff and winter squash," he said. Not volunteering. Answering the question she had not yet asked.

"I don't know teff."

"It's not in the Compact grain catalog." He ate. "Grows well on the western slope here. High-altitude tolerant, good protein ratio relative to what else is available at this elevation." He looked at her. Reading her the way she had been read at every threshold since Kesmark. "You're evaluating it."

"Materials analyst. It's automatic."

He said nothing to that, which was itself an observation filed and left alone. He returned to his food. "Lupe," he said.

She ate. The teff was nutty, slightly bitter, with a texture that held under the squash preparation — she ate most of it before she noticed she was eating from hunger rather than assessment. She had not been eating from hunger, specifically, for long stretches of the corridor. She had been eating from schedule and availability and the operational understanding that not eating created deficits. Hunger was something different.

"The group outside today," she said. "By the east fence."

"Cedar grove issue." He did not stop eating. "A household cut from the managed windbreak stand. They say they didn't know it was managed, or they say they didn't know it clearly enough to know they should have checked. The distinction matters to some people more than to others." He drank from his cup. "The resolution process is running. I'm on the management committee."

"What does the process involve."

He set down his utensil. Not dramatically. With the deliberate intention of a man who had been asked a question he considered worth answering properly.

"The household proposes a reparation," he said. "Form and timeline, their design, within what's actually achievable — replanting, supplemental windbreak work, additional maintenance shifts on the grove for the next two seasons. The management committee assesses whether the proposal restores what was lost. If the assessment is yes, the matter goes to the settlement record as resolved. If no, we describe what's inadequate and they revise the proposal." He picked up his cup. "The dispute outside isn't about the reparation yet. The proposal hasn't been made. The dispute is about prior notice — whether the household could have known the grove had managed status if they had read the available documentation."

"Could they."

"Yes. The documentation was in the shared record — I authored the managed-status entry three years ago. The household has access to that record." He drank. "And the documentation system has a known gap that's been on the work list for two years and not been addressed. A household cutting timber for the first time might not know how to look for a managed-status entry, because the documentation structure is not intuitive for that query. Both things are true simultaneously." He set down the cup. "The dispute is about which truth matters more for assigning responsibility. Those are not the same question as whether both things happened."

"Which one wins."

He looked at her with an expression that was not quite amusement. "You're asking as someone from a Board process. You want to know which argument prevails."

"I want to understand how the decision gets made."

He was quiet for a moment. Deciding whether to give the long answer.

"The process ends when the people involved have said what they need to say and the resolution is clear enough to be recorded," he said. "Sometimes both sides are unsatisfied. Sometimes they arrive somewhere they didn't expect. In your process, the Board decides, and the people who lose carry the decision without having worked through it. Here, working through it takes longer and the satisfaction rate is variable and we currently have three of these running simultaneously." He stood. He collected his plate. "We have had years with none. It depends on the year."

She looked at her empty plate.

"My grandmother came here," she said.

"Adaeze Okonkwo." Not a question. Not surprised. Something more layered than either — the specific expression of a person who has been carrying information about this topic and is deciding what is his to give and what belongs to someone else. "Several times," he said. "If you want to know who she was in this settlement, speak to Saren. She'll be in the kitchen after the evening session. Long center table, usually working on something." He carried his plate to the return station. "She knew your grandmother better than I did and she'll tell you without softening it." He paused at the station. "Which is probably what you want."

He left.

She stayed at the table for a while.

The pair at the far end were still in their conversation — low, involved, working something out rather than reporting it. A younger person came in and went directly to the serving counter and ate standing, reading a folded paper while they ate. Someone called across the room about a tool that needed returning before morning; someone else called back that it had already been returned, and someone else called back that they hadn't known that and thank you. The room was not performing community. It was being one.

She ordered something warm from the serving station and sat with it and waited for the evening session to end.

* * *

The relay reached Berin Kosta at the archive node's exterior work table in the early evening, three days after Ade sent it.

He was sorting relay copies — routing duplicates for the week's dispatches, filed in the cataloguing system he had been rebuilding for eleven months, which had reached the functional state where filing was not an act of hope but a process that produced retrievable results. He had been Compact-trained in the general way that Compact Keeper-class people were trained — CapEx board work, skills portfolio, the expectation that the work would be what the Board matched you to. He had not been matched to archival cataloguing in the Compact. He had found it here by staying when the instructions ran out, and by the archive node's needs being more specific than what he had been sent to deliver.

He read the relay once. Then again.

A bearer, Okonkwo-line referral. Compound with Salt. Crossed at the maritime gate. Currently at the second way-station. Ade's close: *no urgency.*

He folded it and put it in his shirt pocket.

Fourteen months ago he had come through the transit gate with instructions that had seemed, from the outside, like a journey with a destination: find the archive node, document its contents, relay the inventory to the network. He had found the node, found it already inventoried by someone four years prior, found the cataloguing system that had been failing slowly since the person who built it died without training a successor. He had stayed to fix it. The fix was sixty percent complete. The archive's contents were now retrievable for the first time in eight years. The node was doing what it was supposed to do.

He had not known, when he left the Compact, that this was what he was going to do. He had come in with a set of instructions and the instructions had run out on the third day and the work had still been there. He had made a decision and the decision had become a different decision several times since, and he was now eleven months into the third version of it.

A new bearer. Okonkwo-line.

He thought about what it meant in operational terms. The line continuing. A key inside the network. He had been told the shape of the work — the archive, the cataloguing, the inventory function — without being told where it fit in anything larger. He had inferred what he could from the materials he was cataloguing, which were the Outside's own records, and his inferences had grown more specific over eleven months but had not grown complete.

The new bearer might have more of the whole picture. Or no one might.

He finished the current batch of entries — twelve documents, three-point assessment for each: category, subcategory, access tier. He shut down the work lamp at a reasonable hour.

He ate from provisions. He looked at the relay in his shirt pocket and thought about what to send Ade in the morning. *Coming when work allows, one week.* That was accurate. He had a section of the cataloguing that was close to complete; leaving it incomplete would mean someone else picking it up without the specific context he was working from, and creating that situation by leaving now would cost more time than staying one week would cost.

He went to sleep under the Outside's sky.

The window at the head of his sleeping area was unlatched for this purpose — a habit he had developed in the second week at the node, when he had understood that the sky here was not the sky above Compact cities. No ambient light scatter. No competing glow from a populated center. Just the actual dark and the actual stars in it, the full depth of the thing. He had looked at it with the intensity of someone discovering they had been missing access to something for years. That intensity had become routine. Routine had become something he thought of, obliquely, as home.

He looked at the stars.

He slept.

* * *

The evening session ended and the kitchen emptied by stages — people finishing and clearing out, the long tables going quiet from the ends toward the middle, the working noise of a communal meal giving way to the quieter activity of a few people remaining. By the time the kitchen had reached its late-evening state, the serving station was closed and the ambient sound had simplified to the baseline of a room where a handful of people were doing small things.

A woman at the center row of the long tables was working on a piece of textile. Needle and thread. Working steadily, not looking up.

She was older than Lupe — difficult to fix with more precision than that, in the way Ade had been difficult. The Outside's people aged differently than Compact people did, and she was still learning the markers. The woman's hands were the hands of someone who had done outdoor physical work for decades, and the face had the quality of someone who had been thinking about a particular subject for a long time and did not need to build toward the difficult part of it.

She looked up when Kirin sat down across from her. She looked at the shape the key made in Kirin's jacket pocket. She went back to the textile.

"Lupe sent you," she said.

"Yes."

"He usually doesn't." She worked steadily. "It means he has something to say that he thinks I can say better than he can. He's usually right about that." She set the needle down and looked at Kirin. "Saren. Your grandmother's name was Adaeze Okonkwo and I knew her for twenty-two years. Do you want the version that's comfortable or the version that's useful."

"The useful one," Kirin said.

Saren picked up the needle.

"She came through this settlement four times in the years I've been here," she said. "First time 2106. I had been here two years. She arrived, got the relay dispatched, got the route east from whoever was running routing then, and left. She did not stay longer than she needed to." She worked. "The second time, 2112. She stayed two nights. She asked about Nnedi. She asked about the plateau's water management and the junction components on the route southeast of the basin." She paused. "The third time, 2118. Four nights. She asked me specifically what I thought would happen to the plateau settlement when Nnedi was gone." She worked a moment. "I told her I didn't know. She told me that was the right answer."

"And the fourth time," Kirin said.

"2125. She was here one afternoon. She did not stay. She asked whether Nnedi had given the key to anyone yet." She looked at the textile. "I told her I didn't know."

The kitchen was quiet around them. The person who had eaten standing at the counter had left. The two at the far end of the center row were still in their conversation, low now, the voices the specific volume of two people who have been talking for a long time and have stopped worrying about who else is in the room.

"What she was in this settlement," Saren said. "Someone who passed through, asked the right questions, got what she came for, and left. Respected. Not because she was warm — she wasn't, particularly — but because she was competent, and because when she asked about something she asked about the thing that was actually important. And inconvenient, because the things that were actually important to her were always things that people here found uncomfortable to think about." She looked up at Kirin. "She was not a warm figure. The settlements in the Outside like to remember the people who connected us as warm — patient travelers, careful listeners, wise in the way of people who had seen more. That version of your grandmother is easier to carry. The actual version was useful and occasionally irritating and not particularly interested in being liked by people she had no operational need to be liked by."

"That's accurate," Kirin said.

Saren looked at her.

"She came from you," she said. "Not the other way."

"She raised me," Kirin said. "She was honest about inconvenient things, but she didn't explain them. You had to find out for yourself why they were inconvenient."

"Yes." Saren returned to the textile. "That's the version I knew." She worked for a moment. "Nnedi was different. Nnedi was her own problem."

"Tell me."

Saren set the needle on the table.

"Nnedi Eze was seven years old in the photograph your grandmother kept," she said. "That was 2067. Sixty years ago. The building behind them — the one in the photograph — was the third plateau settlement that had been established in that period before the Compact formalized. The first one with structural permanence. Nnedi's family was part of that founding group." She looked at Kirin. "When the Compact began establishing formal territorial edges in the 2080s — when they were offering registration paths, Commons access, Ledger enrollment, the whole structure — most of the people who had been living outside went in. The incentives were real. Stability. Healthcare. Access to everything the outside zones couldn't provide." She paused. "Nnedi stayed. And then she spent the next forty years building something that would still be there when the people who went in needed it to be there. Or changed their minds. Or needed to know it was possible."

"What did that look like," Kirin said.

"From outside it looked like a settlement. A good one — the plateau community has better water management than most comparable locations, better winter stores, better community governance structures than places three times its size." She looked at the textile. "From inside it looked like living with a person who believed she was doing something necessary and was willing to make everyone in her orbit accommodate that belief without being asked." She picked up the needle. "There are people on the plateau who would not speak at her burial rites. There are people who traveled three days to be there. Both groups have real reasons." She worked. "She was right about most things. She was frequently wrong about how to be right about them. She built something that will last past her because she was unwilling to compromise on the elements that make a community last. She was incorrect about what some of those elements were. The community persists. She is not in it."

Kirin sat with this.

Outside the kitchen windows, the settlement's late night. The managed plot in darkness. The cedar grove visible as a darker mass against the sky — lighter sky behind it, no cloud cover now, the stars working.

"The key," Kirin said. "You said you didn't know if she'd given it to anyone."

"I didn't. I don't." Saren looked up. "She had it for as long as I knew her, which is thirty years. She did not explain it to younger people. She kept it separately from the settlement's shared documentation. It caused friction — the plateau has strong collective thinking about individual versus communal holding of significant objects. She held this one outside that framework and did not apologize for it." She paused. "Whether she passed it on before she died, and to whom — I don't know. She declined in the autumn and died in January, and I was not on the plateau for the last months." She set the needle down again. "When you get to the plateau, ask for Rua. She was on the council and she was close to Nnedi at the end. If anyone knows what happened to the key, it's Rua."

Kirin looked at the table.

"What Adaeze meant," she said, "when she asked in 2125 whether Nnedi had given the key to anyone yet. The 'yet' matters."

Saren looked at her for a moment with the quality of someone who has heard an accurate thing and is not going to acknowledge it warmly but is registering it.

"It mattered to your grandmother," she said. "Which means it mattered." She returned to the textile. "Get the plateau route from Pit before you leave in the morning. He knows the approach."

* * *

In the morning the cedar grove was in better light.

She came out of the attached building in the early hour and could see the grove from the yard — the full length of it, a hundred meters along the plot's eastern edge, and deep enough that the interior was in different condition from the perimeter. The perimeter trees bent in the prevailing wind's direction: windbreak species doing windbreak work. The interior trees were straight, older, growing at a different pace entirely. Not windbreak. Habitat. Two bird species in the canopy without trying.

The household that had cut from the perimeter had cut the structural element without understanding the whole. The grove looked like one thing from outside the fence. It was two things from inside it.

Near the grove's edge: three people in a configuration she did not recognize from the previous days. Not the argument configuration — not the specific body language of people holding positions against each other. Closer together, more aligned, two people facing the same direction while a third wrote something down. The reparation proposal being made, or at least something being put into a form that could be made into one. She watched it for a moment through the fence.

She ate from the communal kitchen and found Pit, who turned out to be a narrow-shouldered man in his late thirties, a routing specialist who had walked every approach to every neighboring settlement in this section of the network more times than he claimed to have kept count. He gave her the plateau approach in specific increments: north to the ridge by three features he named and described in order, then the descent via the western slope — not the eastern, because the eastern slope had a deteriorated path section that was passable in summer and not in winter, and this was technically winter's end and technically not yet passable. He described the features in the order she would encounter them, at the pace of someone who had given this description to many people and had learned what they needed in what order.

She listened the way she listened to everything useful: full attention on the substance, the frame left behind.

"Four days," she said.

"Three and a half if you move." He looked at her pack. "You move." He went back to his own work.

She found Ade at the routing desk.

"I have the route," she said.

Ade looked up briefly. "Rua is the council's infrastructure lead," she said. "She's been on the council six years. She was also — " a pause in which she appeared to weigh words, which was unusual enough that Kirin noticed. "Close to Nnedi. In the last year."

She went back to the relay log.

Kirin went to pack.

* * *

She was at the settlement's northern edge by midmorning.

The sky was clear and cold, the pale blue of a cold morning after clarity, the wind from the northwest at a manageable speed. The managed plot was active behind her — people in the fields, two figures near the cedar grove in the new configuration, Ade visible through the routing desk window in the main building.

She stopped at the boundary where the maintained ground gave way to unmanaged scrub heading up toward the ridge.

She looked back.

The main building. The attached structures. The yard organized around function, not appearance. The managed plot with its post-harvest fields resting under the winter's end sky. The cedar grove at the eastern edge — two things at once, seen clearly only from the right angle.

Lupe was somewhere inside. Saren was probably not yet awake. Berin Kosta was at an archive node three days east, fixing a cataloguing system that had been failing for eight years. The relay had gone out this morning: Ade's note to him, and Kirin's own addition, which Ade had allowed without comment — a single sentence, Kirin's name and the plateau route and *find me at Rua's if you come before I leave.*

She turned north.

The route Pit had given her was clear in her head: three features on the ridge, then the western descent. Four days. Three and a half if she moved.

In her pack: the log, reading into 2124 now, close to the end. The clay disc case with the four remaining discs — the plateau approach used its own glyph system, Pit had said, simpler than the primary corridor notation but consistent. The infrastructure photographs, still in their sealed sleeve. Adaeze's letter in the outer pocket with the key.

She had been carrying an instruction to find Nnedi Eze for weeks. She was still going to the plateau. Nnedi was dead, and the instruction had always been: take the key there, and let what happens happen, and you will know what I mean when you arrive.

She did not know what Adaeze meant. She was going to find out by arriving.

The key was warm in her pocket. The settled warmth it had taken on at the transit gate, which had neither increased nor decreased in five days of open ground, which had intensified at the second way-station where the network was denser — a quality it had that she was only beginning to have words for. Not a signal from outside. Something internal to the key, the way a thing had when it was in the condition it was designed for.

She had been carrying it dormant for most of her life without knowing it. She had been carrying it active for five days.

She adjusted her pack. She took the route north.

Behind her: the settlement doing its work. The cedar grove dispute in its current form, the reparation proposal taking shape, the management committee that would review it when it arrived. Lupe at some desk, holding opinions clearly separate from his function. Ade at the routing desk, dispatching the morning's chain. Ori carrying a relay to its first link before school.

Ahead: the ridge, and then the basin, and then the plateau.

And Rua, who had been with Nnedi at the end, who would know whether the key had been passed on and to whom or whether it had gone with Nnedi into the ground or whether it was somewhere in the plateau settlement waiting for Kirin's to arrive and recognize it.

The wind came down from the ridge at her face. She walked into it.

The scrub rose around her, closing off the view of the settlement behind. The ridge was not visible yet — it would be visible when she climbed above the scrub line. She could see where the gradient steepened ahead, the specific quality of the slope when it was preparing to become something else. She had been reading terrain for weeks now and she was getting better at it.

She walked.

The road is long. The end: not yet.
