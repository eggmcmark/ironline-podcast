# Episode 11: "The Thing That's Coming"
## Season 1 | The Future Economy

*Kirin and Berin head west with a failing junction behind them, the Curator makes his move, and a message goes to Meridian that cannot be taken back.*

---

The room had no windows. This was not an accident.

Ardent had worked in rooms without windows for eighteen years. Long enough to have stopped registering the absence. The lighting was adequate. The equipment was better than it looked. The coffee had been acceptable two hours ago.

The operative's report was twelve lines. He read it three times.

Both keys. Authenticated. Joint handshake completed at the plateau settlement — twenty-sixth of March, late evening. Protocol status: active. The Rootstock Protocol had moved from registered-dormant to operational for the first time in forty-eight years.

The bearer's name: Kirin Saleh. Twenty-nine. Materials analyst, Level Two, Sable River CapEx Board. On leave since the second of February. She had crossed the maritime transit facility on the twelfth of March, fourteen days before the handshake. Her route home: west to the second way-station, south to the transit gate, through the corridor to Kesmark, rail to Sable River.

Across the room, Kel was at the second desk. She had been at the second desk for four years and had learned, in that time, not to prompt him on first readings.

"The sector six junction," he said.

"Active failure," she said. "Point seven percent pressure drop per eighteen hours, as of the relay date. The eastern infrastructure monitoring board received the same flag — logged and unread, standard for that sector." A pause. "Timeline is nine to fifteen days from the relay date."

He set the report on the desk.

He had the junction's condition data going back twenty-seven years. The Outside's sensor network and the Compact's abandoned monitoring systems were receiving the same telemetry — the sensors predated the Compact's current territorial classifications and had never been decommissioned. He had the ratings. He had tracked the decline. He had known, two years ago, that the failure would arrive before any procurement cycle could respond. The board was staffed for a different map.

He had known and he had held the knowledge. Because flagging the junction meant explaining why he had the data. And explaining that required explaining the systems he monitored. And explaining those systems required explaining what was at the other end of the sensor networks. Seven million people in zones the Compact had classified as empty for thirty-six years. The problem under the problem.

"The bearer's route timeline," he said.

Kel looked at the routing table. "Eight to twelve days to Sable River."

"And the junction window."

"If the rate holds at point seven, nine to fifteen days from today." She paused. "If the rate accelerates — membrane fatigue in this failure mode characteristically accelerates once micro-cracking begins to propagate — the window could close to six to nine days."

He stood. He went to the routing map pinned to the wall. Compact territorial overview. The zones east of Kesmark marked inactive. The standard designation. Every Compact map used it.

"The filing requires three components," he said. "The population atlas — held by a broker in Meridian, outside our jurisdiction. The infrastructure survey — distributed across archive nodes in the Outside network, not centralized. The witness authentication — the keys, with the bearer." He looked at the map. "We can't touch the atlas. The infrastructure survey is not a single document she's carrying. She has photographs. She doesn't have the formatted survey the protocol requires." He turned. "She'll need to receive the formatted documentation from the network after she reaches Sable River. The documentation has to travel."

"Which means a relay point," Kel said.

"Which means a relay point. Find it." He sat. "Not the bearer. Not the keys. The documentation chain. That's the point we can reach."

He picked up the report.

The interior witness appointment had been dormant for thirty-five years. The woman who held it before this bearer — Adaeze Okonkwo, a quiet Keeper-class resident of Sable River, a small garden, a granddaughter she was raising — had held it so quietly that his predecessor's full apparatus had spent a decade looking for the key without finding it.

It had been in a closet. Behind thirty years of folded linens. In a box.

Not tradecraft. Inheritance.

He had been watching the channels a professional would use. She had used none of them. She had waited in an apartment in Sable River with a key in a box and had trusted that the right person would open it when the time came.

The right person had opened it.

"The bearer," he said. "She has no operational background."

"Materials analyst," Kel said. "No Guild certification outside her Board work. No corridor transit history. No Free Zone connections prior to this journey."

He looked at the report.

"The appointment is key-bound," he said. "Not person-bound."

"Yes."

"Then the person doesn't determine the outcome." He looked at the wall. "The key does."

He opened the routing file.

Outside — wherever outside was — a young woman was walking west with two authenticated tokens in her jacket pocket and the Compact's longest-running infrastructure failure in the eastern sector measuring itself in days. He had managed this situation for eighteen years. He had managed it because the equilibrium held: no bearer had crossed with a completed protocol, no key had reached an active state, no filing had been possible.

The equilibrium had held.

Until now.

* * *

They left before the kitchen opened.

Rua was at the gate in the early cold with her field case and a relay copy — the previous evening's sensor reading from the sector six junction, printed and folded. She held it out without preamble.

"Updated every forty-eight hours," she said. "Ade gets the chain at the second way-station. She'll have current readings when you arrive." A pause, the pause of someone selecting which additional piece of information will actually be useful. "The Compact infrastructure designation for sector six is S-6E. Eastern sector, sixth junction of the primary distribution line. If you're presenting this to a Foundation Assessor at a CapEx Board, that is the designation she will need for the query." She looked at Kirin's face. "The query will return twenty-seven years of below-specification ratings and no active work orders."

"S-6E," Kirin said.

"And the seven others." Rua held out the slip of paper. "S-8N, S-8S, N-4W, N-7E, N-2E, SW-12, SW-14." She paused. "Below those, the three from the archive notes Berin gave me last night. I added the condition date on each one. None of them have current assessments." She looked at the paper in Kirin's hand. "A single failing junction is an infrastructure problem. Eight failing junctions in the same sector, with the same documentation history, the same Compact non-response going back twenty-seven years — that is a pattern. Someone who knows how to read Compact infrastructure filings will see the pattern."

She closed the gate.

No ceremony. The settlement was entering its morning. Berin was beside Kirin with his pack arranged in the way of someone who had been doing this for eleven months and had stopped optimizing for anything except the ability to keep moving indefinitely. His pack was lighter than hers. Better organized.

They went down the western slope.

* * *

Going east, she had been reading the terrain for the first time: the scrub zone's species composition, the transition into managed sections, the soil changes that marked water. She had been arriving somewhere.

Going west, she was leaving it.

The route you came up was the route you went down, but the pitch reversed and your weight shifted forward, and the scrub that had been opening ahead of you was closing behind. Every landmark arrived at the wrong angle. She had walked this once and thought she knew it. She found she knew it differently.

They cleared the plateau's transition zone by midmorning and hit the scrub plain.

"Seven other junctions," she said.

Berin was beside her, pace-matched without effort. "I have current data on S-8N and S-8S — the plateau monitoring chain covers those two plus sector six. The others are from the archive. Some of those entries are two years old."

"Condition on S-8N."

"Steel housing, corrosion failure mode. Last assessment I have is fourteen months old and rated it at four to seven months from failure." He walked. "That window is expired, depending on which end of the range you take."

"S-8S."

"More variance — the location runs wetter, corrosion rate less predictable. Three to twelve months from the same fourteen-month-old data point." He paused. "The three-month end of that range is past. The twelve-month end is two months away. We don't know where in the range it is."

She walked with this.

"And N-4W."

"Pre-Compact irrigation infrastructure," he said. "Original construction, 2040s. Extended past design life twice. The archive rated it at already-past-threshold on the most recent entry." He looked at his notes. "That entry was eighteen months ago."

She counted. Already past threshold, eighteen months ago.

"What does it serve."

He found the page. "A cluster of three settlements in the northern reach. Combined population in the 2123 census: approximately three hundred and forty."

"The 2123 census."

"Three years old." He looked at her. "The relay-dark zone I mentioned — a monitoring node near N-4W that went silent four months ago. I don't know if that's a relay failure or something else."

She kept walking. Three hundred and forty people, approximate, three years out of date, in a settlement cluster served by a junction rated past failure threshold eighteen months ago, in a sector where the routing traffic had gone dark four months ago.

"The total population reach," she said. "All eight junctions."

"Rough figure," he said. "One to two million people across the eastern sector and northern reach. High end if the relay-dark zones have population density the older census didn't capture." He paused. "The Outside's total is 7.1 million, per the current census. The eastern sector has the highest density. The northern reach is sparser but the junctions there serve longer distribution lines with fewer backup options."

She thought about Veda, in the plateau kitchen. *We have been building something here that does not need the Compact to validate it.* Four decades of building. Water management that worked. Community governance structures that produced better outcomes than the Compact's mediation process.

But the water management was built on infrastructure laid in the 2040s and 2050s by people who had stopped being responsible for it in 2090 when the population map changed. The plateau community had working water management because Rua spent every working week in the drainage channels with mud on her knees. Not because the system worked without her. Because someone was watching.

The scrub thinned. She could read the land ahead as a former cultivation zone — the soil in the access path a different color, worked once, let go, but not forgotten by the earth.

They walked.

At the midday break she sat against a post-and-wire fence and looked at the slip of paper with the eleven junction designations. Rua's writing above, Berin's below. Eleven points on a map she had not seen but was beginning to be able to read.

"The relay-dark zone by N-4W," she said. "When did the traffic drop."

He thought. "Four months ago, approximately."

"Before or after the junction passed threshold."

He was quiet. "After, if the three-month projection was accurate." He paused. "I don't know the order. I know what the archive had."

She folded the paper.

A settlement cluster where the relay had gone quiet and the junction was past its failure threshold and nobody on the Compact's side had flagged it because the monitoring board was staffed for a different map. She did not know what had happened there. She might not find out.

She stood up.

"How far to the transit gate," she said.

"Six hours at the pace we've been walking." Berin repacked the notes. "We can make the second way-station by nightfall from there."

She adjusted her pack and walked.

The managed section's path ran south. She followed it. The afternoon light changed quality as the terrain opened into the lower plain — colder shadows replaced by more direct sun, the season beginning to assert itself in ways the plateau had suppressed. Spring was further along here. The scrub at the road's edge had the first green growth, low to the ground, not yet committing.

At the far edge of the plain, before the path descended toward the coast route: a relay post. Older than its neighbors. The glyph weathered in a way the posts around it weren't. She stopped.

Berin looked at it. "Outside relay network, extended into the northern approach route," he said. "Decommissioned since 2114. The routing log shows it as inactive." He looked at the glyph. "Whatever it served, it stopped serving."

She looked at the post.

Decommissioned relay. A station that had existed and then not existed. The network managing itself, pulling back from the parts that weren't needed because the people they served had made different calculations. What those calculations were and what they had cost: the archive had some of it. Not all.

She walked on.

The transit gate was ahead. The route south. The corridor. Five days, if they moved.

The key was in her pocket, warm and unchanged. Both keys together. She had stopped checking it. It was there or it wasn't. It was warm. It was what it was.

She walked.


* * *

They came off the scrub plain in the early afternoon into the lower terrain that led toward the coast route and the transit gate.

The light changed at the topographic shift — the plateau's colder shadows giving way to the lower zone's more direct sun. Spring was further forward here. The scrub at the road's edge showed the first green growth, low, not yet committing. The smell of the sea was somewhere in the air before the sea was visible — salt and cold and the particular quality of open water that she had not been smelling for two weeks and had not realized she was missing.

She had been reading the terrain for two months. Now she read it differently. Not for what was there but for what was wrong. A line of vegetation stopping abruptly, indicating a buried water line that had stopped running. A relay post weathered out of sync with its neighbors, installed from an earlier era when the network had reached further. A stretch of path better-maintained than the sections around it, which could mean recent attention or could mean the momentum of prior work running on.

There was another decommissioned post marker, two hours south of the first. Different glyph. Same weathering signature.

She did not stop to look at it.

The transit facility was the same transit facility she had crossed twice before. Same pre-Compact hardware in the intake corridor. Same bureaucratic patience. The officer on intake was young, methodical, the particular precision of someone who had been doing this long enough for accuracy to become automatic.

"Return crossing," she said. "Bearer referral, Okonkwo-line."

He found the entry on the board. Looked at the date. "Thirty-one days inside."

"Yes."

Berin presented his documentation from the February intake. The officer processed them both without comment and handed Kirin the corridor transit form — standard format, unchanged from March.

"Current throughput to Kesmark: four days walking or one day vehicle from the Larch junction." He stamped the form. "We're not holding southbound traffic."

She took the form.

They were through the gate and into the corridor before the afternoon was over.

* * *

The second way-station in the early dark was different from the first time.

The first time she had come from the east with five days of walking behind her and a key in her pocket intensifying at the network density and a letter from her grandmother she hadn't opened yet. Now she came from the same direction, from further in, and she knew what she was looking at. The yard arranged for function, not visitors. The kitchen light in the south windows. The routing desk lamp in the main building's east windows.

She went directly to the main building.

Ade was at the routing desk. She looked up when Kirin came through, looked at Berin behind her, and returned her attention to Kirin with the quality of someone who had prepared for this conversation.

"Junction update," she said. She set the relay on the desk without being asked. "S-6E. This came in this afternoon."

Kirin read it.

Point eight-seven percent per eighteen hours. Up from point seven. Up from the point seven-eight in the intermediate reading.

Threshold window: nine to fifteen days from the relay date. The relay date was today.

"That means —"

"Nine to fifteen from today," Ade said. "If the rate holds at point eight-seven."

Berin was reading the relay over her shoulder. "It won't hold," he said. "Membrane fatigue in third-stage stress shows a characteristic acceleration as the micro-cracking propagates. The linear projection underestimates the closing window." He paused. "If the current trend rate continues — point seven to point seven-eight to point eight-seven — the actual failure window is six to nine days."

Ade looked at him. "Who are you."

"Berin Kosta. Sector twelve, eleven months. I came west."

She held his look for a moment, filing something for later. Then she looked at Kirin. "Six to nine days. You need to be at Sable River in six." She looked at the routing table. "What's your route."

"Larch," Kirin said. "Naila's vehicle, if she released it."

"She released it. The message came back this morning." Ade looked at the routing table. "Naila's instruction: the vehicle goes back to her contact at Kesmark. Not at Larch. She has a contact on the Compact side." She picked up a card. "The contact name." She set it on the desk without sliding it over — it was for Kirin to take.

Kirin took it.

"Vehicle range," she said.

"Kesmark with a margin. Charge infrastructure doesn't resume until Sable River. You'll be on foot from Kesmark — eighteen kilometers to the transit rail junction, then rail." Ade looked at the routing table. "If you make Larch by tomorrow evening, you can be at Sable River in four days. Five at the outside."

Kirin looked at the card with the contact name. She did not know the name.

"I need the full picture," she said.

Ade looked at her.

"What the network knows that I don't," Kirin said. "Sit down with me."

Ade set down her pen.

"Sit," she said.

They sat.

* * *

She told it in the order it would be needed.

"The EQU-convertibility play," she said. "The Meridian broker reported in October that the market queries from the east financial district firm had stopped. She had three possible explanations and did not know which was correct."

"I know this from when I was in Meridian."

"What you don't know is what came after." Ade looked at the routing log. "Three weeks ago, the Outside network received a relay from a Free Zone contact in Meridian's eastern district. Not the broker — a different source. A firm in the financial district had made a procurement inquiry through the Free Zone commercial registry. They were looking for authentication hardware. Pre-Compact era. Rootstock-compatible. The specification was narrow enough to apply to only two known instances of the protocol." She paused. "Your key is one of them."

Kirin set the relay on the desk.

"They stopped querying the open market," Berin said.

"Because they found a different avenue," Ade said. "What the avenue is, the network doesn't know. But the procurement inquiry is for hardware, not for the software. They already have the protocol implementation or can reconstruct it. What they needed was a physical authentication token."

Kirin thought about Renata, at mile 22 on the corridor highway. Mid-twenties. Pack opened. A small pre-Compact chit in her pack, similar in style to the key but not identical. The Curator's operative had searched Renata's pack and not found the bearer chit he was looking for. She had taken the chit. She had never been able to read it.

"The other instance," she said. "The second Rootstock-compatible key the specification applies to."

"The network doesn't know what it is or where it is," Ade said. "What we know is that the firm stopped querying openly. That's consistent with having found what they were looking for through another channel."

"And if they file a claim with their own key," Berin said. "Independently of the Rootstock Protocol witnesses. What does that produce."

Ade looked at the table. "Not a legal expert. What I know from the protocol documentation: the Rootstock Protocol requires two witnesses in specific appointment categories — interior and founding. One witness can authenticate but cannot file. The filing needs both. If the firm has a token from a different protocol instance, they can authenticate as a filing party, but they can't represent both witness appointments." She paused. "The claim they could file would be a different claim."

"Different how," Kirin said.

"The claim you're carrying — interior witness from your grandmother's appointment, founding witness from the disc — files on behalf of the Outside settlements as communities with prior equity claims predating the Compact's territorial designation. The claim is for recognition and for infrastructure obligation. The Compact would be bound to respond to that population as a recognized class." Ade looked at her. "A claim filed by a financial firm, using whatever authentication they've sourced, files on behalf of the firm. The settlements would be evidence for the claim. Not parties to it."

"They'd be recognized," Berin said. "Formally. But the terms of recognition would be set by the filing party."

"Not by the settlements," Ade said. "Not by the witnesses." She looked at Kirin. "This is what your grandmother spent thirty-five years preventing. The key inside the Compact's prohibition zone, held by her, meant a legitimate Rootstock filing couldn't happen without Compact-witness authentication she controlled. The convertibility play couldn't proceed while she held the appointment."

Kirin looked at the desk.

She was the interior witness now.

"The Curator," she said. "You mentioned a relay query."

Ade set down her pen. "Two days ago. Formatted in a method that wasn't standard Outside routing — an encoding convention we recognize as his. He was querying for bearer traffic and relay-point data. Not for you specifically. For the documentation chain."

"He knows about the documentation chain," Berin said.

"He's had eighteen years to think about how the protocol works," Ade said. "He knows the atlas is in Meridian, out of his reach. He knows the keys are with you, inside our network, out of his direct reach. What he doesn't know is where the formatted infrastructure survey documentation is and how it reaches you." She looked at the routing table. "He's looking for the relay point. The vulnerable node between the archive's documentation and your filing position in Sable River."

Kirin looked at the junction list in her pocket.

"The infrastructure photographs," she said. "Adaeze's second instruction. The sealed sleeve I've been carrying since the archive. Those are field photographs. They're evidence of the junction conditions. Are they sufficient for the protocol filing?"

Ade shook her head. "The protocol requires a formatted survey document, signed in hardware by the founding witness. Field photographs are supporting documentation, not the survey itself. The formatted version has to come from the archive nodes — the infrastructure survey specifications are documented there, and the formatting requires the founding witness's authentication." She looked at Berin. "Which means the disc key."

"She has the disc key," Berin said.

"And the disc key is here, in the corridor, on the way to Sable River. But the formatted survey is at the archive nodes." Ade looked at the desk. "I sent a relay to sector twelve tonight. If the steward currently on rotation can locate the formatting templates and begin assembly, the document could be in motion in five to six days." She paused. "I do not know if that's enough time."

"It might not be," Berin said. "Sector twelve is three days from here by relay, and the archive's cataloguing is incomplete. The formatting templates are in sections that hadn't been reached when I left."

"Then we need someone at the archive who can find them," Ade said. She looked at him.

He looked at the routing table.

"I can send a specific location note," he said. "The templates are in the documentation section. I know approximately where. If the steward has the cataloguing index I built, she can locate them with a specific reference."

"Write it," Ade said. "It goes in the morning chain."

He found paper and wrote without asking for more clarity. She watched him write with the attention of someone who had decided to trust the specificity rather than verify the competence.

"The Curator," Kirin said. "He's watching the broker in Meridian."

"Almost certainly," Ade said. "It's the only external node he can reach. He has no jurisdiction there, but he has information pressure — Free Zone trade relationships, barter negotiation leverage. A Compact-origin broker who has been in the Free Zone for eleven years would understand exactly how much leverage the Compact can apply and how to manage it." She paused. "Whether your contact in Meridian is reachable under that pressure — I don't know her. You do."

Kirin thought about Dara. Second floor above the dyers' lane. Eleven years of holding Outside materials out of the market. The specific stillness of a person who was very good at exactly one thing and had structured her life around it.

"She'll hold," Kirin said.

"Then the relay can route through her," Ade said. "The broker as a node between the archive's documentation and your Sable River position."

"And the Curator monitors her but can't stop the relay without direct pressure he hasn't yet applied."

"That's the assumption."

"What changes his calculation," Berin said. "What makes him apply pressure when he hasn't yet."

Ade looked at the relay log. "If the junction fails before the filing, the calculus shifts in his favor. A crisis that has already happened is harder to leverage than a crisis that's been documented and projected. He wants the crisis to come first." She paused. "He's waiting."

The room was quiet. Outside: the settlement doing its late-evening work. The kitchen cooling. A relay runner somewhere on the morning preparation. The cedar grove at the plot's eastern edge, whatever stage its dispute had reached.

"How long do we have," Kirin said.

"Six days to nine," Ade said. "If Berin's acceleration model is correct. Six days."

"And our route puts us at Sable River in five."

"If you make Larch by tomorrow evening. If the vehicle is ready. If the transit rail is on schedule." She looked at the table. "Those are three conditional statements."

"Make it two," Kirin said. "The rail is on schedule. It's the Compact."

Ade's mouth moved slightly. Not quite an expression. The specific quality of a person who found the observation accurate and had no occasion to dispute it.

"Sleep," she said. "First light: kitchen, then find me. I'll have Naila's vehicle status confirmed before you leave."


* * *

The visitor space had the same ceramic mug on the window ledge. Chip in the rim. Someone had kept using it because it still held liquid.

She set her pack against the wall and stood at the window for a while, looking at the managed plot in the dark. The cedar grove was a line of deeper shadow at the plot's eastern edge. Its dispute was resolved or it wasn't — she didn't know and wasn't going to stop to find out.

She had one message to send.

She went back to the routing desk.

Ade was still there. Berin was already there ahead of her, the location note for the archive sector twelve ready in his hand. He set it on the desk. Ade encoded it without comment and put it in the overnight stack.

"The broker," Kirin said. "In Meridian. I need to send ahead of me."

Ade looked at her.

"My return contact commitment was six months," Kirin said. "From Sable River. I'm sending now, four months early, from the Outside, because there won't be time to route it after I arrive."

Ade was quiet for a moment. Not reluctant. Calculating.

"Five to seven days to Meridian," she said. "You'll be in Sable River before it arrives. Whatever action the broker takes runs parallel to yours, not ahead of it."

"I know."

"Then why send it."

Kirin thought about it.

"Because if the junction fails before I reach Brigid," she said, "and the broker gets to Brigid first with the junction list — then Brigid has the pattern before the crisis rather than during it. Even a day ahead of the crisis changes what she can do with it." She paused. "And because the broker needs to know the protocol is active. She's been holding the atlas for eleven years. She should know."

Ade picked up her pen.

"Write the junction list," she said. "Formatted with the designation, the condition status, the assessment date, the Compact monitoring case number if you have it." She held out paper. "One entry per line. I'll encode it for Meridian in the morning chain."

Kirin wrote.

She took her time with it. The designations in order of urgency: S-6E first, with the current pressure drop rate and the relay date and the failure window from Berin's acceleration model. Then S-8N and S-8S with the archive condition data and the condition date flagged. Then the northern junctions in declining urgency, assessment dates noted, the condition threshold status on each one.

At the bottom of the list, three lines of context. Not explanation — context. The Rootstock Protocol, active, authentication complete, two witnesses, bearing documentation west. The filing pending, formatted survey in transit. The S-6E Compact monitoring flag: case number, infrastructure board designation, first filing date.

Twenty-seven years of filings. All received. None read.

She looked at the list and handed it to Ade.

Ade read it without expression. She folded it into an envelope, encoded the Meridian address convention, and set it in the morning stack beside Berin's note.

"When it arrives," Kirin said, "and the broker acts — whatever she does, I can't see it from here."

"No," Ade said.

"And I can't guarantee Brigid Aelfgar will know what to do with the junction list when the broker gets it to her. If the broker can get it to her."

"No," Ade said.

"And the formatted documentation may not arrive before the junction fails."

Ade looked at her. "Do you need me to tell you something that makes those statements not true."

"No," Kirin said.

"Then we're done for tonight." She put the pen down. "The relay goes in the morning. What happens next happens."

She went to her desk lamp and adjusted its angle. The conversation was over.

Kirin went back to the visitor room.

She sat on the bed with her pack against the wall and the ceramic mug on the window ledge and the light from the settlement's working lights coming under the door in a thin line. Both keys in her jacket pocket — she had stopped taking them out. They were warm in the specific settled state the handshake had left them in, the disc slightly warmer than the card. The protocol was active and the filing was possible and the junction was failing at a rate that was accelerating and the message was in the envelope and would go in the morning.

She lay down.

She did not sleep immediately. She looked at the ceiling — low stone, sealed with a clay compound, the work of someone who had done it long enough ago that the compound had smoothed and settled into the surface. A room that had been doing its function for a long time without requiring attention.

Outside: the settlement. The cedar grove in the dark, its dispute in whatever state it was in. Lupe somewhere in there, or not. Ori asleep, who would run the morning's relay chain before school.

The junction was in a gully below a basin four days east. The pressure gauge needle was holding. The membrane was flexing.

She slept.

* * *

The relay intercept arrived at Ardent's terminal in the middle of the night.

Not the relay itself — the metadata shadow. The encoding signature. The routing timestamp. The destination-field convention. Not the content; the content was encoded in the Outside network's current cipher, which had shifted in October and which his office had not cracked in the available window. What he had was the fact of a relay departing the second way-station for Meridian, late evening, using an encoding technique that had a provenance he recognized. Someone at the way-station had used a convention derived from a two-year-old Meridian cipher combined with a current Outside network access token. That combination had one likely origin.

The bearer, or someone with her.

Kel was not at her desk. He had sent her home at hour twenty-two. He sat at the terminal in the adequate light and looked at the relay shadow.

Destination: Meridian. The broker.

He had the broker's address. He had been monitoring her traffic for eleven years — not constantly, but with the regularity of a person who checks a system he depends on. She had been careful for eleven years. She had never given him an actionable reason to apply pressure. Her position in the Free Zone gave her legal insulation. Her trade relationships with the Compact were real but not decisive — she was one node in a barter network that did not depend on any single intermediary.

He could apply pressure to her through trade mechanisms. The Compact imported from Meridian. What the Compact imported could be conditioned. A trade review flagging a specific broker for enhanced customs processing was not illegal, not unprecedented, not even unusual. It was friction. Enough friction, applied precisely, could slow a relay's effective transit time.

He sat with the question.

The bearer was six days from Sable River. The junction was six to nine days from failure. The formatted documentation — the infrastructure survey that the protocol filing required — was not yet in motion. If the assembly started now and ran perfectly, the formatted survey could reach Sable River in eight to ten days. Later than the junction window. Later than the bearer.

If the junction failed before the filing, the obligation structure was different. The Compact would have to respond to the crisis, but the response would be humanitarian rather than protocol-bound. Different terms. Different leverage for the Outside settlements. More room for the Compact's infrastructure board to define what "response" meant rather than having the definition specified by an active Rootstock claim.

He would prefer the crisis to arrive before the filing.

He would prefer that less pressure had been necessary to produce this outcome.

He thought about Adaeze Okonkwo.

He had been tracking the Okonkwo-line for twelve years. Not her — the key. He had known someone inside the Compact held the interior appointment because the protocol records in the archive documents showed the appointment as active-and-held, which meant the key was somewhere inside the Compact's boundaries. The key had been inside the Compact for thirty-five years, which meant the holder had chosen to remain inside. The holder was compliant with the Compact's system, or appeared to be. She was not moving the key. She was not attempting to file. She was holding.

He had run a systematic search. He had flagged every pre-Compact financial instrument registration in Sable River's territorial perimeter for twelve years. Nothing. He had searched the Board records of every materials and assessment professional in the district — the most likely cover roles for someone with her background. Nothing. He had had her building monitored — not her specifically, because he hadn't known who she was, but the building her address-of-record pointed to after he narrowed the search. He had found a quiet woman with a small garden and a granddaughter.

He had not found the key.

Because the key was not in the building he was monitoring in a way that monitoring could find. It was in a closet. In a box.

He had been looking for a professional concealment. She had given it no concealment at all, because concealment was a behavior and behaviors were what his systems were trained to find. A box in a closet, in an apartment, in the name of a woman who had been on the Ledger since 2091 and had done nothing notable in thirty-five years — that was not concealment. That was furniture.

He had spent twelve years unable to find a key that was not hidden from him.

The right person had found a box.

He looked at the relay shadow on his screen.

He could apply pressure to the broker. Small. Targeted. Enough to slow the relay by two or three days. Enough for the junction to fail first.

He opened the trade review filing interface.

He sat with his hand over the input.

The Compact's Value Ledger backed 4.2 billion people's economic lives. Property rights. Pension claims. The structure of every transaction that had been made in every Compact territory for thirty-six years. If the EQU-convertibility play succeeded — if a working Rootstock authentication token reached a Compact registry in the hands of financial actors with outside interests — the Compact's Ledger was, for the first time, subject to external claims. One successful external claim was the precedent. The precedent made the next claim easier. The exception grew. Slowly — not catastrophically, not immediately. Over years.

He had managed this for eighteen years because the alternative was not a small thing.

The bearer's claim was different from the convertibility play. He knew that. The Rootstock Protocol's two-witness structure meant the bearer could file on behalf of the Outside settlements as a recognized community, not on behalf of a financial firm. Different outcome. Different leverage.

He knew the difference.

He also knew that a legitimate Rootstock filing — any legitimate Rootstock filing — was the precedent that made the next filing possible. That established the Compact's obligation to process outside claims. That opened the door.

He had been keeping the door closed.

He withdrew his hand from the input.

He was not going to apply pressure to the broker tonight. Not yet. He needed to watch the documentation chain before he moved. If the formatted survey was in motion, if the relay was on a fast route, he would know where to intervene. If it was slower — if the assembly was taking longer than the junction's failure window — there was nothing to intercept yet.

He closed the trade review interface.

He monitored the broker's traffic.

The room was quiet and adequate and without windows. Outside, somewhere, the sector six junction was measuring itself in hours. The bearer was sleeping in a borrowed room. The broker was in a second-floor office in Meridian where the morning light would come through the window above the dyers' lane in five days or seven.

He watched.

* * *

In the morning Ade had the vehicle confirmation and a relay update.

The update came in overnight: the pressure reading from the sector six junction. Point nine-four percent per eighteen hours. Up again.

Berin looked at the number. He did not say anything. He did not need to.

"Five days," Kirin said.

"If that trend holds," he said. "The acceleration has been consistent — point seven to point seven-eight to point eight-seven to point nine-four, each reading eighteen hours apart. If it stays on that curve: four to five days." He paused. "The failure mode could also plateau. Third-stage membrane fatigue sometimes stabilizes when the flexion finds a new stress distribution. I don't know if sector six will do that. The archive doesn't have enough resolution on similar cases to say."

"Assume it doesn't plateau," she said.

"Then four to five days," he said. "We need four."

Ade looked at the routing table. "Larch by tonight, Kesmark by midmorning tomorrow, rail by noon. Four and a half days from now."

"Four," Kirin said.

"Four," Ade said. She did not argue.

The relay to Meridian had gone in the morning chain. The relay to sector twelve had gone overnight. Whatever happened at the archive node would happen without their monitoring. Whatever Dara did with the junction list would happen without their monitoring. Both things were in motion and beyond reach.

The kettle on the kitchen's side shelf was still hot. Ade poured without offering — she poured a cup and set it at Kirin's position on the desk. An operational accommodation, not a gesture. A thing that would keep someone functional.

"The Curator," Kirin said.

"He's watching," Ade said. "He can't stop the relay without applying pressure to the broker directly, and applying that pressure leaves a record of a kind he has been careful not to leave for eighteen years." She paused. "Whether the crisis urgency changes his calculation — I don't know. He may move. He may wait." She looked at the desk. "If he moves, the broker will know. She's been managing this for eleven years."

"She'll hold," Kirin said again.

"You said that last night."

"I believe it more this morning."

Ade's mouth moved. The same not-quite expression.

They ate from the kitchen at first light. Berin had his pack ready when she came back through. He looked rested in the way of someone who slept when sleeping was available and did not use the available time for anything else.

She picked up her pack.

Ade was at the routing desk when they came through the main building on the way out. She looked up briefly.

"The junction failure," she said. "When it happens, the network will log it with the Compact monitoring designation and the condition history." She paused. "For the record. In case the record matters."

"It will matter," Kirin said.

Ade went back to the relay log.

They went out.

* * *

The route south from the second way-station was familiar in the way of a route she had walked from the opposite direction.

Different gravity. The landmarks at different positions in the view. What had been ahead on the way in was behind now; what had been behind was gone. She had learned to read this landscape at one angle and now she was reading it at the mirror image of that angle, which was its own distinct skill.

The terrain opened after the first kilometer into the low scrubland that ran toward the coast approach and the transit gate. She had been through the transit gate twice already. The gate knew her key. The key knew the gate. This was a thing she had learned to take for granted.

"The archive," she said. "When you left sector twelve. Was the section you were in close to completion."

"Within a week of clean work," Berin said. "I had a decision point: finish the section cleanly or leave midway and come back to it later. I left midway." He walked. "The person on rotation after me will either find the work clear enough to continue or will need to restart the section from the beginning. I don't know which."

"Ade's relay."

"Will tell them what to look for. Whether they find it is a different question."

She walked. Not fast. Fast enough. The pace Berin had described as hers — the pace that covered ground and did not cost more than it needed to.

"If the documentation doesn't arrive before the junction fails," she said.

"Then we make the case with what we have." He looked at the path ahead. "You have the infrastructure photographs. You have Rua's junction list with the condition history and the Compact monitoring designations. You have the authentication keys and the protocol status." He paused. "You have a materials analyst's professional assessment of a failed infrastructure component in a sector the Compact has been monitoring for twenty-seven years without acting on the data." He paused again. "That is not nothing."

"It's not a Rootstock Protocol filing."

"No," he said. "It's not."

They walked.

She thought about Brigid Aelfgar. In Sable River, in her position at the CapEx Board, who had been described in Adaeze's log as a Rootstock-adjacent placement. Someone placed her the way I was placed, in a role adjacent to mine. Adaeze did not approach her. Brigid has been waiting.

Waiting could mean many things. It could mean patient. It could mean alert. It could mean present and capable and ready for something to arrive that she had known was coming for years and that had not come.

Kirin was coming.

She was four days out. The junction was four to five days from failure, assuming the acceleration held. If she arrived before the failure, Brigid had something to act on before the crisis. If she arrived after, Brigid had the documented history of a failure the Compact had been informed about for twenty-seven years, and the active Rootstock Protocol, and a filing that could follow the crisis and change what the crisis meant.

Both timelines led somewhere. Only one of them led somewhere before the water stopped.

"How do you walk faster," she said.

"You walk faster," Berin said.

She walked faster.

The scrub gave way to the lower zone's sparser vegetation, the coast approach visible in the distance — the specific quality of sky that meant the sea was near, that particular lightness at the horizon line where reflected water altered the atmosphere. She had come through this zone on the way in without fully noting it. She was noting it now.

The key was in her pocket. Both keys. The disc slightly warmer. The settled state of the active protocol, continuous and unchanged. The warmth she had been carrying since the handshake, through the four days west from the plateau, through the second way-station, through the morning's departure and the route south. She had stopped checking it. It was there.

The gate was ahead.

Behind her: the second way-station, and the plateau behind that, and the sector six junction in the gully below the basin with the pressure gauge needle at its current reading. Point nine-four. Moving.

Ahead: the transit gate, and the corridor, and Kesmark, and the rail, and Sable River.

Brigid Aelfgar at the CapEx Board. Tomas somewhere in the city, building something, knowing more than he had said. Her grandmother's apartment — empty now, or given over to someone else, or both.

She walked into the coast zone's smell.

The junction would fail when it failed. She would arrive when she arrived.

The road ran on ahead of her, south and then west, out of the Outside and back into the world she had been living in for twenty-nine years without knowing what she was living in the middle of.

She walked.
