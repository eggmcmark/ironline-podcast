# Nadia Okoye — The Architect

## Vitals
- **Born**: 1976, Hartford, CT (parents emigrated from Nigeria, 1969)
- **Died**: 2079 (age 103), in her brownstone. She never sells it.
- **Season 1 age**: 51–64
- **Occupation**: Professor of Economic History, Brooklyn College
- **Home**: Paid-off brownstone, Crown Heights (inherited from her mother)

## Physical
- Tall, angular, moves fast. Always looks like she's on her way somewhere even when sitting down.
- Natural hair, usually wrapped or pulled back. Silver threading through by her mid-50s.
- Wears reading glasses on a chain — the one concession to academic cliché she allows herself.
- Her brownstone is her: books everywhere, half-drunk cups of tea, notes on scraps of paper pinned to walls, beautiful objects placed with no system that nonetheless form a coherent aesthetic.

## Voice (speech patterns for dialogue)
- **Expansive**. Where Marcus uses two words, Nadia uses twenty — but each one earns its place. She doesn't ramble; she unfolds.
- **Precise vocabulary**. Uses the exact word, even if it's not the common one. "That's not efficiency, that's velocity disguised as purpose."
- **Thinks out loud**. Her dialogue often sounds like a lecture that's become a conversation, or a conversation that's become a lecture. She's aware of this and doesn't care.
- **Questions are probes**. She asks questions to test her own thinking, not because she doesn't know. "But what if the equity appreciation is front-loaded — what does that do to the incentive structure?"
- **Nigerian-American cadence**. Subtle — her parents' rhythm surfaces when she's passionate or angry. The emphasis shifts to the second syllable. The pace drops before a key point.
- **Laughs easily**. Especially at her own ideas when they surprise her.

## Psychology
- **Core trait**: She sees systems. Where Marcus sees a kitchen, she sees an economic model. Where he sees a handshake, she sees a theorem. She has been thinking about asset-based economies for twenty years — academically, theoretically, in papers that twelve people have read.
- **Relationship to work**: Writing is building. Her papers are structures. She revises the way Marcus sands — methodically, obsessively, until the grain is right.
- **Relationship to Marcus**: He is the empirical proof she never had. She respects him the way a theorist respects data. She also likes him — his directness, his refusal to be impressed by ideas that haven't been tested. He makes her better.
- **Flaw**: She can mistake elegance for truth. A beautiful model can blind her to the ugly parts of reality it doesn't capture.
- **Blind spot**: She underestimates politics. She believes that if the model is correct, the world will adopt it. She will learn otherwise through Elena.

## Key relationships
- **Marcus**: See above. Their partnership is the engine of the story. They are not lovers. They are something more useful: people who make each other think clearly.
- **Her students**: She is demanding and generous. She does not tolerate laziness. She will spend hours with a student who is trying.
- **The academy**: She has tenure and uses it as armor. She doesn't care about status. She cares about being right.

## Arc (Season 1)
Tenured academic with an unproven theory → desperate woman making a deal → the person who names what Marcus discovered → controversial prophet watching the world ignore her, then steal from her

## The Paper
"The Hale Model: Equity Participation as Economic Reorientation" (2036). Initially ignored, then ridiculed, then plagiarized, then celebrated, then taught in every school in the world.
