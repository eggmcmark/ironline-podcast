# THE FUTURE ECONOMY — Series Outline (v5)

## Season 1: "The Inheritance"
### POV: Kirin Saleh (primary) + ensemble encounters

*Last updated: 2026-04-29 — outline v5 prunes for stakes, plants the antagonist by Ep 3, gives every episode a grounded engine, and recasts the Outside reveal as a sequence of recontextualizations rather than a single late-season door-opening.*

---

## Season Arc — Beginning, Middle, End

**Beginning (Ep 1–3): The hook.** A grieving woman finds an object her grandmother kept hidden for thirty-five years. The object is real, the protocol it belongs to is real, the coordinates are real — but the moment Kirin acts on it, the world she's lived in stops being safe. Other people have been hunting these keys. The empty zone isn't pastoral; it's curated. The grandmother wasn't a watcher in retreat; she was active to the day she died. Kirin commits to the road with somebody she has reasons not to trust.

**Middle (Ep 4–9): The shape of the unseen.** What the Compact calls *empty* is in fact a managed space, contested by several factions: the Outside, a Compact internal-security shadow that keeps the Outside's existence a secret, Free Zone financial brokers who want EQU convertibility through the back door, and at least one non-Compact power. The grandmother lived inside that contest. Kirin crosses into the Outside in the middle of this stretch — and the second half of the middle is the discovery that the Outside is not what she imagined either.

**End (Ep 10–14): The weight of what she knows.** The thing the Outside has been preparing for arrives — and it arrives because of choices Adaeze made. Kirin has to decide what to bring back, and to whom, and at what cost. The season ends not with revelation but with delivery: she puts something in front of someone who could change something, and we don't know if they will.

Each episode works as a self-contained story (a complete experience in a new place) while advancing the larger journey. Characters recur — some as allies, some as obstacles, most as both at different times. The world builds through immersion AND friction; nothing is given to Kirin without cost.

## Episode Plan

### Episode 1: "The Box"
**Setting**: Sable River — a mid-sized inland Compact city
**POV**: Kirin Saleh

Three weeks after her grandmother Adaeze's death. Kirin is going through the motions — work at the CapEx Board, evenings in her grandmother's apartment sorting a lifetime of belongings. She's grieving in the way that practical people grieve: by organizing. Adaeze was a quiet woman. Small life. Small garden. The apartment is modest — Keeper-class, everything in its place.

We see Sable River through Kirin's daily routine. The city is alive but not crowded — a place built for more people than live there now, with whole blocks repurposed as Guild workshops and community gardens. The CapEx Board where she works is a vivid set piece: the matching algorithms, the skill postings, the disputes she mediates. Her colleagues, her routines, the texture of work in the Compact.

Then: the box. Hidden in the back of a closet, behind decades of neatly folded linens. Metal, old, sealed with a mechanism Kirin doesn't recognize. Inside: the object. A physical token — dense, unfamiliar material, warm to the touch in a way that materials shouldn't be, etched with patterns that are half-circuitry and half-something else. And a handwritten note from Adaeze: "This was my mother's. Now it is yours. Be patient with what it asks of you."

Kirin takes the object to work — she's a materials analyst, she reads surfaces for a living. She runs it through standard assays. The results don't make sense. The material composition doesn't match anything in the Compact's database. Not because it's exotic — because the database doesn't go back far enough. The object predates the Ledger.

She shows it to a senior colleague, Hasse, who's been at the Board for forty years. Hasse holds it for a long time, turns it over, and says something she doesn't expect: "I've seen one of these before. Once. A long time ago. You should go to the Archive."

The episode ends with Kirin at the Territorial Archive — a quiet building full of pre-Compact records that almost nobody visits. The archivist, an old man named Perun, examines the object with hands that tremble slightly. He looks up at Kirin and says: "Your grandmother. What was her name before she married?"

Kirin doesn't know. She never asked.

**What the episode establishes**: Kirin's character (competent, practical, slightly restless), the texture of Compact daily life, the grandmother as mystery, the object as engine. The city of Sable River. The quiet grief of sorting someone's things and finding you didn't know them.

### Episode 2: "What She Left Behind"
**Setting**: Sable River — Adaeze's apartment, the Archive, Kirin's social world
**POV**: Kirin

Kirin digs into her grandmother's past. What she finds: almost nothing. Adaeze's records on the Ledger begin in 2091 — the year the Compact was formalized in this territory. Before that: a gap. No pre-Compact records. No birth certificate in any Compact database. No Guild history. No family connections. It's as if Adaeze appeared from nowhere at age 40 and built a quiet life from scratch.

Perun at the Archive helps her trace the object. It's an authentication key for a pre-Compact equity protocol — a verification system that predates the Ledger but is structurally compatible with it. The key corresponds to a registered claim. The records are fragmentary — stored in a format the Archive can barely read. But the location data is clear: coordinates in a depopulated zone, 400 miles east of Sable River. Deep in the empty.

Meanwhile: Kirin's life. Her best friend, Tomas, a Guild carpenter who thinks she's insane for pursuing this. Her boss at the CapEx Board, who's noticed she's distracted. A dinner with friends where someone casually mentions that "the empty zones are empty for a reason" and everyone nods, and Kirin realizes she's never questioned this. Why would she? Everything she needs is here.

A subplot: Kirin visits an old-timers' social club where people who remember the transition era gather. She asks about pre-Compact protocols. Most don't know anything. One woman — very old, sharp-eyed — says: "There were settlements out there. Before the Compact. People who were building something. Most of them came in when the Compact formalized. Some didn't." She won't say more.

The episode ends with Kirin making a decision. She requests leave from the CapEx Board. Tomas tells her she's making a mistake. She packs a bag. She's never been more than 50 miles from Sable River in her life.

### Episode 3: "Inheritance Tax" *(rewrite — supersedes "The Frontier")*
**Setting**: The depopulated zone east of Sable River — Kesmark, the corridor highway, a way-station called Salt
**POV**: Kirin (primary), with an unnamed-antagonist cold open

**Engine.** Kirin gets from the last Compact settlement to the first place she'll need a guide. She thinks she's walking out alone into a melancholy landscape. The episode shows her three things she did not bargain for: someone is hunting people who carry these keys; the empty zone is not autonomous, it is curated; and her grandmother was not a quiet watcher but an active operator who returned to the corridor in the months before she died.

**Beats.**
- Cold open: an unseen man searches a dead traveler's pack on a stretch of highway. He doesn't find the key he expects. He reports failure to a voice on a comm. He moves on. The listener now knows what Kirin doesn't.
- Kesmark is not warm. Foundation Assessors stationed at the depot. A clinic that has run out of two of three staples. A shopkeeper who notices Kirin's pack and asks the wrong questions. Kirin lies competently for the first time in her life.
- The walk east. The deer-in-the-school is replaced or inverted: signs that the empty zone is hunted by people who eat what they kill. The maintenance grid is doing things it isn't supposed to do. A drone follows her for a quarter mile and then breaks off.
- She finds a body. Mid-twenties, female, propped against a guardrail. Pack opened. A small pre-Compact chit in the woman's pocket — close enough in style to Kirin's key that Kirin's chest goes cold. Kirin does the wrong thing (or the right one) — she covers the body, takes the chit, runs.
- She arrives at **Salt**, a way-station built in the bones of a former gas-station-and-diner: a checkpoint, not a community. Run by **Naila Brace**, a fifties ex–Free Zone medic, hard, exhausted, careful. Naila does not welcome travelers; she charges them. In salt, mostly. Salt is one of the few currencies the corridor can't fake.
- Among the resident transients: **Dez**, reinvented — former Free Zone logistics coordinator, eight years out here, but with a side trade no one announces: she sells passage and she sells information about who's passing. She is sharp, suspicious, and she is *interested* in Kirin in a way Kirin has the sense to find alarming.
- Confrontation. Someone tries to take Kirin's key. The intervention that saves her is not friendship — it is somebody else's rule about what happens at Salt. Kirin keeps the key. She also loses something (a piece of equipment, a comm, an illusion).
- Recontextualization 1 (the grandmother): in Salt's lost-and-found ledger of passages — a real ledger, salt-stained — Kirin finds Adaeze's pre-Compact name, Adaeze Okonkwo, dated *September 2125*. Six months before Adaeze died. Naila confirms the date with a flat sentence. The Sable River elder-care facility had Adaeze on shift the entire month of September 2125. The cover was deeper than Kirin understood.
- Recontextualization 2 (the world): Naila tells Kirin what almost no one in the Compact knows. The maintenance grid in the empty zone is not autonomous. It is being directed. Has been for at least a decade. Somebody is paying to keep what gets kept and let go of what gets let go of. The empty zone is *curated*. By whom: unknown. To what end: unknown. But Adaeze knew. Adaeze came back to look at something specific.
- A comm chirps on a frequency it should not be able to use. Kirin's name, broadcast clearly. The way-station goes still. The voice on the cold-open comm has caught up to her.
- Landing: Kirin and Dez leave Salt at speed in a vehicle that is not on Kirin's data core. The route they take is freshly resurfaced and goes somewhere the Compact's maps do not show. The key warms, then cools — for the first time it does something Kirin can feel happening in her hand. She watches Dez and tries to figure out who she has just bought passage with.

**What this episode does for the season:**
- Plants the antagonist (Ardent — name not yet given on screen) by Ep 3.
- Recasts the empty zone from pastoral mystery to contested territory.
- Recontextualizes Adaeze: not a watcher in retreat, an active operator who was running the corridor in her last year. This re-reads everything from Ep 1–2.
- Makes the key *do* something for the first time.
- Introduces Naila and the way-station economy as recurring infrastructure.
- Reframes Dez from wise sage to morally ambiguous partner — a person Kirin needs and cannot yet trust.

### Episode 4: "The Curated Mile"
**Setting**: The road east of Salt; an unmapped maintenance depot; the rim of a "managed sector"
**POV**: Kirin (primary), with one short interlude POV on the antagonist's operative

**Engine.** Kirin and Dez run east on the curated road. They get caught in the mechanism of the curation itself — and have to decide whether to spend the inheritance to get out of it.

**Beats.**
- The road they're on is too good. Recently resurfaced. Drainage cut to current spec. Dez recognizes the contractor signature (or pretends not to).
- They reach a depot that is on no map and is staffed — by people who are not scavengers, are not Compact, and are not Free Zone. Their patches are unmarked.
- A test of the key: Dez asks Kirin to use it on a maintenance interface. The key handshakes with the depot's network for half a second before the depot's people shut it down. They look at Kirin differently after that.
- Kirin makes her first real Outside-side acquaintance: a depot mechanic named **Yusra**, who does not give her name freely and does not invite trust. Yusra is competent, prickly, and the first character in the show who treats Kirin like a problem rather than a guest.
- The interlude POV: Ardent's operative listens to a partial intercept. He still doesn't have Kirin's location. He has a face — pulled from a Compact transit log — and a window. He requests a partner.
- Recontextualization: the curation is not done by a single party. There are at least three sets of patches at the depot. The empty zone is a tripartite condominium — Outside, Compact internal-security shadow, and a third party Kirin can't yet identify. They tolerate each other because acknowledging each other would require acknowledging that none of them are supposed to exist here.
- Landing: Yusra hands Kirin and Dez off — at a price they each privately resent — to the next handler east. The road from here will not be on any map at all.

### Episode 5: "The Reading Room"
**Setting**: A pre-Compact research campus, deep in managed sector, partially still active
**POV**: Kirin, with one scene POV on Naila back at Salt

**Engine.** Kirin reaches the coordinates from the key — and the coordinates are not where she thought they were going. The "claim" the key is registered to is not assets. It's a library.

**Beats.**
- The campus is intact and quietly working. Solar arrays. Functioning HVAC. Empty common rooms with current-year coffee cups.
- The key's behavior changes here. It heats predictably. Not magic — circuit. It is a hardware authentication token doing what it was built to do. Stephenson-grade specificity: Kirin sees the contact pads, recognizes the protocol family, understands she's been carrying a smartcard the size of a coin.
- A person is here: **Fen**, a steward of what's underneath. Not the warm guide of v4. A working person who has been alone for too long and is awkward and abrupt about it.
- The reveal: the underground level is not a comms relay. It is a reading room — a physical archive of the Outside's documentation. Materials. Schematics. Histories. Treaties. Population estimates. The Outside is not a small thing.
- Adaeze's signature is in this room, in places. She read here. Recently.
- The Naila scene at Salt: Ardent's people arrive. Naila lies competently. Naila is paid in something other than salt for the lie, and we see the cost of the lie in her face. We do not yet know what she gave them, or whether she gave them anything.
- Landing: Fen tells Kirin what the room contains. He does not tell her what to do with it. He gives her two paths: stay and read, or take a piece of it and run.

### Episode 6: "What She Read"
**Setting**: The reading room and a transit corridor toward the coast
**POV**: Kirin, with substantive interior reckoning with Adaeze

**Engine.** Kirin reads what her grandmother read. It changes what she thinks the inheritance is. She also finds Adaeze's last note — one written in Tamsat in 2126, weeks before her death.

**Beats.**
- Specific documents. Not vague. Kirin reads three things: a population atlas of the Outside (real numbers, larger than she imagined), a technical bulletin on a maintenance-grid vulnerability that the Compact's security shadow has been trying to close, and a personal log from Adaeze.
- The personal log is dry. Adaeze did not write lyrically. She wrote like a logistics auditor. She catalogued what she saw, who she met, what she couldn't verify. The dryness is what hits Kirin hardest.
- Adaeze's last note: practical, addressed not to Kirin by name but to "the witness who comes after me." It assumes Kirin will arrive. It tells her three concrete things to do, and one thing not to do.
- Dez reads over her shoulder partway through. Their relationship turns. Not into trust. Into a working alliance with explicit terms.
- Antagonist pressure increases: Fen is contacted by someone in the network and told to clear the room. They have less time than they thought.
- Landing: Kirin chooses one of Adaeze's three concrete things and they leave the campus heading not east but south, toward the coast and Meridian.

### Episode 7: "Meridian"
**Setting**: A Free Zone city — bright, fast, expensive, dangerous
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** Kirin needs to convert one of the things she carries into one of the things she needs. To do that she has to negotiate inside an economy she has spent her life looking down on.

**Beats.**
- Meridian is granular. Specific neighborhoods. Specific subcultures. The Free Zones are not "us, kept going" as decoration — they are a vivid, contested place where Kirin is out of her depth in ways that have nothing to do with the Compact-vs-Free-Zone framing.
- The introduction of **Dara**, a capability broker. Charming, calibrated, transactional. She is the first person who treats Kirin's key as an asset with a market price, and the conversation about that price is uncomfortable in a way that teaches Kirin something about herself.
- Kirin discovers she is being followed — not by the antagonist's people this time, but by Free Zone interest. Word about the key is on a market she didn't know existed.
- Recontextualization: the EQU-convertibility tension is not an abstraction. There are Free Zone parties who would pay enormously for a working pre-Compact equity protocol that the Ledger could be made to honor. Kirin's key may be one. Adaeze's whole adult life inside the Compact may have been about preventing exactly that.
- Dez is in her element here. Dez also disappears for six hours and returns without explanation. Their working alliance acquires its first real fracture line.
- Landing: Kirin gets what she came for, at a price she did not plan to pay. Dara gets something she did not expect. They part as people who will see each other again.

### Episode 8: "The Cut"
**Setting**: A repurposed maritime facility; the threshold of the Outside network
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** The crossing into the Outside is not a gentle threshold. It is a vetted, paid passage with a paperwork process, a price, and people who say no.

**Beats.**
- The facility is bureaucratic. There is a clerk. There are lists. The Outside is not an anarchic utopia — it has its own administrative seriousness, and its seriousness is different from the Compact's in ways that interest and unsettle Kirin.
- Dez reaches the limit of her involvement. Her exit is not warm. It is honest. They have done business well together.
- A character returns: someone from a previous episode shows up at the crossing in a way that recontextualizes their earlier appearance. (Candidate: Dara, on her own account, brokering. Or: one of the "scavengers" Amira mentioned from her work at the Compact's eastern reach turns out to have been a placed contact.)
- Kirin's first real conversation with someone Outside-born — a person whose worldview was built without either Compact or Free Zone categories. The conversation is short and odd and Kirin doesn't understand half of it.
- The antagonist's people miss her by hours. Kirin sees evidence of that and it is not relieving. It is sobering.
- Landing: she crosses. The chapter break.

### Episode 9: "Landfall"
**Setting**: The first Outside settlement Kirin enters
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** Kirin arrives somewhere whose rules she does not know, with one of Adaeze's three concrete instructions to deliver, and discovers that delivering it requires her to participate in a community process she finds both impressive and uncomfortable.

**Beats.**
- The settlement is granular and unidealized. People disagree. Decisions take time. Children misbehave. There is a fight about firewood that is also a fight about something larger.
- World-building through participation, not through lecture.
- Kirin learns who Adaeze was here. Not legendary. Respected and inconvenient. People remember her with the mixed feelings real people are remembered with.
- The unknown child in the photograph: identified. Their identity is not the sentimental answer.
- Landing: Kirin is told what Adaeze meant by one of her three instructions. It is harder than Kirin expected.

### Episode 10: "Adaeze's People"
**Setting**: Adaeze's home community, on the plateau
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** Kirin meets the people who knew Adaeze before she went to the Compact, and she has to decide whether the inheritance she came to claim is one she will accept.

**Beats.**
- The settlement is the one Reva named — Nnedi's plateau settlement. Nnedi is dead. The community persists.
- Kirin meets a woman who was Adaeze's closest friend, a man who was Adaeze's adversary, and a child who is the granddaughter of the child in the photograph. The pictures get re-painted.
- The inheritance is articulated for the first time. It is not property. It is a role: witness inside the Compact. Adaeze held it for thirty-five years. The community asks Kirin to consider holding it.
- Kirin says she'll think about it. The community is unsentimental about her hesitation.
- Landing: a piece of news arrives that gives the next episode its shape.

### Episode 11: "The Thing That's Coming"
**Setting**: A briefing room in the Outside; intercut with a Compact internal-security room
**POV**: Kirin (primary), with one substantive POV scene on Ardent

**Engine.** The threat. Specific, granular, NOT save-the-world. A real, plausible, paradigm-blind problem that the Compact cannot see and the Outside has been documenting.

**Beats.**
- The threat is concrete. Candidates for what it is: a planned hostile EQU-convertibility scheme by Free Zone brokers backed quietly by a non-Compact power, executed via captured pre-Compact protocols (i.e., keys like Kirin's), that would functionally hollow out the Ledger from inside. Or: an aging maintenance-grid component with a known failure mode that will cascade across a region the Compact considers empty but where the Outside has substantial settlement, because the Compact wrote that region off in 2090 and stopped mapping it. Or: both, simultaneously.
- We meet Ardent on screen. He is articulate, principled, and wrong in a way the listener can argue with. His reasons for keeping the Outside hidden are reasons that real people have for real cover-ups.
- Kirin understands that Adaeze's cover at the elder-care facility was not metaphor. Kirin's grandmother spent twenty years specifically preventing what is now coming. Kirin is being handed her grandmother's project, mid-execution.
- Landing: she accepts the role she was offered last episode. The decision costs her — she tells someone back home, and she cannot guarantee what they will do with the information.

### Episode 12: "Salt Owed"
**Setting**: Mixed — an Outside operation; intercept at Meridian; a Compact cell
**POV**: Kirin (primary), with rotating short POV scenes

**Engine.** Kirin runs an operation. It is not a heist. It is a paperwork operation — a documented filing into a particular Compact registry that, if it lands cleanly, forces the Compact to acknowledge something it has spent thirty-six years not acknowledging. The opposition is real.

**Beats.**
- The operation involves several characters from previous episodes — Naila, Dara, possibly Yusra, Fen — each acting in their own interest in a way that aligns with Kirin's only partially.
- Naila pays a debt she's owed since Episode 5.
- Tomas (back in Sable River) is contacted for the first time since Episode 2. His reaction is not what Kirin hoped.
- The operation partially succeeds. Ardent's people block one filing pathway and Kirin pivots to another.
- Real cost: somebody Kirin has come to care for is hurt or compromised. Not killed. Not kidnapped. *Compromised* — outed, blacklisted, made unable to do what they did before.
- Landing: Kirin starts the route home with a sealed packet and a price on her head.

### Episode 13: "The Empty Way Back"
**Setting**: The corridor in reverse — Salt, the curated mile, Kesmark
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** The road that felt melancholy outbound is operational on the way back. Ardent's people have warned the corridor about her.

**Beats.**
- Naila's way-station is no longer a checkpoint Kirin can walk into. Naila is doing something to keep her safe that the listener has to work to understand.
- The maintenance grid behaves against her. Items it kept on the way out — water at standpipes, lighting in shelters — are quietly reassigned away.
- Dez returns, on her own terms, for her own reasons.
- A trap. A real, near-fatal moment. Kirin survives it because of a piece of skill she developed earlier in the season — not because of luck, not because of a friend.
- Brigid's "three of three returned" statistic from Episode 2 acquires its meaning: at least one of those three returns was not what it looked like. We learn how.
- Landing: she crosses back into Compact territory not at Kesmark, where they are watching for her, but somewhere else.

---

### Episode 14: "Assessment Day"
**Setting**: Sable River
**POV**: Kirin

**Engine.** Kirin delivers what she carried home, to a person, in a room. We do not learn whether it works.

**Beats.**
- The city is exactly as she left it, and that is its own knife.
- She does not go to the CapEx Board first. She goes to Brigid, in private. The Brigid scene is the central scene of the episode. Brigid does not promise to act. Brigid asks Kirin a question Kirin cannot answer.
- The grandmother's apartment, one more time, with what Kirin now understands.
- The packet is delivered. The recipient is somebody the listener didn't expect — but, on reflection, will see was set up.
- Landing: Kirin makes a tea Adaeze used to make. The kettle hums. We hear the city outside. A door, somewhere in another building, opens. The season ends on the sound of someone deciding what to do.

## Season 2: "The Build"

The heist-movie season. Kirin has the knowledge. Now she has to build something — assemble a crew from every corner of the world, navigate Compact bureaucracy and Free Zone chaos and Outside strangeness, and construct a response to the thing that's coming. Each episode introduces a new crew member with a new skill set. The tone shifts from journey to ensemble. The world-building deepens through the project.

## Season 3: "The Weight of Real Things"

The crisis arrives. Everything built in Season 2 is tested. Characters face real consequences. The Compact is forced to confront what it's been unable to see. The question: can a civilization change its mind fast enough to survive?

## Season 4: "New Foundations"

Aftermath. What gets built from the wreckage. Not a resolution — a beginning. The world that comes next.
